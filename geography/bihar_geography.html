Rivers of Bihar

Bihar has a dense network of rivers, primarily fed by Himalayan and Central Indian mountain systems. The Ganga is the master stream, flowing eastward across the middle of the state, dividing it into North Bihar (Himalayan-fed) and South Bihar (Peninsular-fed).

Total drainage area: ~94,163 sq km | Rivers flow SW to NE in North Bihar, SE in South Bihar

River System Summary

  • Ganga System: Ganga + Son + Punpun + Kiul + Chandan (South) and Ghaghra + Gandak + Kosi + Mahananda (North)
  • Gandak System: Gandak + Burhi Gandak (parallel streams, Burhi originates inside Bihar)
  • Kosi System: 7 streams (Saptakoshi) — Sun Kosi, Indravati, Bhote Kosi, Arun, Tamur, Dudh Kosi, Likhu Khola
  • Bagmati System: Bagmati + Kamla + Balan — drain into Kosi floodplain
  • Son System: Son + Rihand + Koil + Auranga — largest southern tributary of Ganga

Soil Types of Bihar

Bihar has diverse soil types due to its unique geography — from Himalayan foothills in the north to plateau fringe in the south. The Indo-Gangetic alluvium dominates, covering >90% of state area.

1. Piedmont Swamp Soil

Location: West Champaran (foothills of Himalayas)

Feature: Waterlogged, thick clay, rich in organic matter. Found in Chaur (oxbow lake) lands. High carbon content.

2. Terai Soil

Location: West Champaran, East Champaran, Kishanganj

Feature: Fine silt, high moisture, rich humus. Dark grey to black. High fertility — ideal for jute, rice, sugarcane. High water table.

3. Gangetic Alluvium — New (Khadar)

Location: Supaul, Saharsa, Darbhanga, Madhubani, Sitamarhi, Purnea, Katihar, Muzaffarpur, Samastipur, Khagaria

Feature: Fresh silt deposited annually by floods. Sandy-loam, light grey. Neutral to slightly alkaline. Excellent for rice, wheat, maize, sugarcane.

4. Gangetic Alluvium — Old (Bhangar)

Location: Patna, Nalanda, Bhojpur, Buxar, Vaishali, Saran, Gopalganj, Siwan, Rohtas, Aurangabad

Feature: Older alluvium above flood level, dark grey to black. Contains calcareous concretions (kankar). Clay-loam, rich in potash and lime.

5. Calcareous Soil (Rarh)

Location: Muzaffarpur, Saran, East Champaran, West Champaran, Vaishali

Feature: High lime content (CaCO3), sticky when wet, hard when dry. Rich in calcium but may be deficient in nitrogen and humus. White efflorescence visible in summer.

6. Red & Yellow Soil

Location: Gaya, Nawada, Jamui, Rohtas (southern parts), Kaimur, Banka, Munger

Feature: Derived from granite/gneiss. Red due to iron oxide, yellow when hydrated. Low fertility, sandy, needs irrigation and fertilizers. Found on plateau fringe.

7. Sandy Soil (Diara)

Location: Buxar, Saran, Patna, Vaishali, Bhagalpur, Katihar, Munger (along Ganga)

Feature: Coarse sand deposited on river islands/banks. Low water retention. Suitable for watermelon, vegetables, cucurbits with irrigation.

8. Tal Soil

Location: South bank of Ganga from Patna to Bhagalpur

Feature: Heavy clay with high moisture retention. Waterlogged in rainy season. Suited for rice cultivation (especially in Nalanda, Sheikhpura, Lakhisarai).

9. Balthar Soil

Location: Southern plateau fringe — Kaimur, Rohtas, Aurangabad, Gaya, Nawada, Jamui, Banka, Munger

Feature: Coarse-grained, gravelly soil formed from sandstone. Poor fertility, low organic matter. Light coloured, needs significant inputs for farming.

10. Karail-Kewal Soil

Location: Rohtas, Gaya, Aurangabad, Jahanabad, Munger, Bhagalpur

Feature: Clay-loam with calcareous nodules (Kankar). Dark coloured. Moderately fertile. Kewal is lighter variant; Karail is heavier. Found in undulating plains.

Crops of Bihar

Bihar is predominantly agricultural, with ~76% of its population dependent on agriculture. The state has three cropping seasons: Kharif (June–Oct), Rabi (Oct–Mar), and Zaid (Apr–Jun). Cropping intensity >150% is among the highest in India.

Major Cereals

Rice (Kharif)

Area: ~3.3 million ha (largest crop by area)
Production: ~8 million tonnes
Key Districts: Gaya, Rohtas, Aurangabad, Nalanda, Muzaffarpur, East Champaran, Purnea, Katihar, Darbhanga, Madhubani, Saharsa, Supaul, Saran, Gopalganj, Siwan
Types: Sali (main kharif), Aghani (winter), Garma (summer/irrigated)
Bihar's share: ~3.5% of India's rice area

Wheat (Rabi)

Key Districts: Rohtas, Bhojpur, Gaya, Patna, Nalanda, Buxar, Saran, Siwan, Gopalganj, Muzaffarpur, East Champaran, Vaishali

Maize

Minerals of Bihar

Bihar has significant mineral wealth concentrated in its southern plateau region (Kaimur, Rohtas, Gaya, Jamui, Munger, Bhagalpur, Banka). The state is a national leader in pyrite, kyanite, and gold resources.

Coal Reserves

Limestone

Districts: Kaimur (Bhabhua), Munger, Rohtas
Key: Major cement industry raw material. Kaimur has the largest deposits in Bihar. Munger deposits in Kharagpur Hills. Used by Bihar State Cement Corp.

Mica

Districts: Nawada, Jamui, Gaya, Bhagalpur, Munger
Key: Found in pegmatite veins. Ruby mica variety present. Once a major export item from Bihar. Mostly mined in small scale now.

Quartz & Silica

Districts: Bhagalpur, Jamui, Munger, Nalanda
Key: High-purity silica for glass and ceramic industries. Bhagalpur quartz used for glass manufacturing. Quartzite in Lakhisarai, Munger, Nalanda.

Bauxite

Districts: Munger (Kharagpur Hills), Rohtas
Key: Aluminium ore. Deposits found in lateritic caps. Not extensively mined due to forest coverage and logistics.

China Clay (Kaolin)

Districts: Bhagalpur, Munger, Banka, Vaishali
Key: Used in ceramics, paper, paint and rubber industries. Bhagalpur has the highest quality china clay deposits in the state.

Gold

Districts: Jamui (Sono block), Gaya, Nalanda
Key: Bihar holds 57% of India's gold resources. Sono, Jamui is the most prominent gold-bearing area. GSI has identified multiple gold belts in the state. Ancient gold mining sites exist at Dumri, Ganeshpur, Chakai.

Iron Ore

Districts: Gaya (magnetite), Jamui (magnetite), Bhagalpur (hematite)
Key: Magnetite deposits in Rahatia, Karmatia (Gaya). Hematite in Champanagar, Bhagalpur. Small-scale, not commercially mined at scale.

Pyrites

Districts: Rohtas (Amjhor)
Key: Bihar holds ~95% of India's pyrite reserves. Sulfur source for sulfuric acid production. The Pyrites, Phosphates & Chemicals Ltd (PPCL) operates at Amjhor. Used for fertilizer industry.

Coal

Districts: Bhagalpur, Munger (Rajmahal region)
Key: Estimated 160 million tonnes in the Rajmahal basin area (extending into Jharkhand). Not extensively mined in Bihar portion due to forest cover and proximity to Rajmahal coalfield in Jharkhand.

Lead-Zinc

Districts: Banka, Rohtas
Key: Small deposits found in Banka district (Kurumdih area). Not commercially exploited at scale. Rohtas has minor lead occurrences.

Forest Types

Tropical Moist Deciduous

Found in West Champaran (Valmiki Tiger Reserve). Sal is the dominant tree species. Other species: Khair, Semal, Asan, Jamun, Amla. High biodiversity with tiger, elephant, deer, leopard.

Tropical Dry Deciduous

Found across Kaimur, Rohtas, Gaya, Aurangabad, Munger, Banka, Nawada, Jamui. Sal, Mahua, Palash, Tendu, Aonla, Bel. Largest forest type in Bihar. Rain-dependent, leafless in summer.

Riverine Forest

Along Ganga, Gandak, Kosi river banks. Khair, Shisham, Semal, Babul, Bamboo. Narrow strips of riparian vegetation. Important for wildlife corridors and soil conservation.

Plantation Forest

Distributed across all districts, especially North Bihar (flood-prone areas). Species: Eucalyptus, Poplar, Subabul, Acacia, Shisham, Bamboo. Social forestry and farm forestry initiatives.

Additional Geography

Physical Divisions

1. The Northern Terai Region

Districts: West Champaran, East Champaran, Kishanganj
Feature: Sivalik foothills and marshy plains. 60–90m elevation. Dense forests, high rainfall, numerous rivers (Gandak, Bagmati). Valmiki Tiger Reserve located here. Chaur lands (oxbow lakes) are typical.

2. North Bihar Plains

Districts: All of Tirhut, Darbhanga, Kosi, Purnia divisions
Feature: Flat alluvial plain, elevation 30–60m. Formed by Ganga and its northern tributaries. Highly fertile but flood-prone. Kosi megafan covers ~15,000 sq km. Tal (depression) lands common. High groundwater table.

3. South Bihar Plains

Districts: Patna, Magadh, Munger divisions (northern parts)
Feature: Bhangar (old alluvium) dominated. Elevation 40–80m. Relatively less flood-prone. Diara lands along Ganga (sandy islands). Rich in potash and lime. Higher cropping intensity.

4. Southern Plateau Fringe

Districts: Kaimur, Rohtas, Aurangabad, Gaya, Nawada, Jamui, Munger, Banka, Bhagalpur (southern parts)
Feature: Northern extension of the Chotanagpur Plateau. Elevation 100–500m. Undulating terrain with hills and valleys. Rich in minerals. Kaimur range (highest point in Bihar). Forested. Soil is red/yellow and Balthar type.

Climate

Climate Overview

Type: Sub-tropical monsoon (Cwa Koppen classification)
Summer: Mar–Jun. Max temp 40–46°C (Gaya hottest)
Monsoon: Jun–Sep. Average rainfall ~1,200 mm
Winter: Nov–Feb. Min temp 4–10°C (Gaya, Aurangabad coldest)
Highest Rainfall: Kishanganj (~2,500 mm)
Lowest Rainfall: Gaya, Aurangabad (~800 mm)
Rainfall Pattern: Decreases from NE to SW
