1. Introduction – What are Acids & Bases?

Everything around us is either acidic, basic (alkaline), or neutral. Acids and bases are two fundamental categories of chemical substances that we encounter in daily life.

Definitions
  • Acid – A substance that tastes sour and turns blue litmus red. Derived from Latin word acidus meaning sour.
  • Base – A substance that tastes bitter, feels soapy/slippery to touch, and turns red litmus blue.
  • Neutral – Substances that are neither acidic nor basic (e.g., pure water, sugar solution).
Natural Indicators

Indicators are substances that change colour in acidic or basic media. Natural indicators are obtained from plants and lichens.

3. Acids – Properties & Classification

Definition (Arrhenius Concept)

According to Arrhenius (1884), an acid is a substance that donates H⁺ ions (protons) when dissolved in water.

Example: HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻ (in water)

Classification of Acids

1. Based on Strength – Strong vs Weak

PropertyStrong AcidsWeak Acids
IonizationCompletely ionize in waterPartially ionize in water
H⁺ concentrationHighLow
pH0–33–6
ExamplesHCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HBr, HI, HClO₄CH₃COOH (acetic), H₂CO₃ (carbonic), H₃PO₄ (phosphoric)
Corrosive natureHighly corrosiveLess corrosive

2. Based on Source – Mineral vs Organic

PropertyMineral Acids (Inorganic)Organic Acids
SourceMinerals (chemical synthesis)Living organisms (plants/animals)
StrengthGenerally strongGenerally weak
Carbon chainNo carbon backboneContain -COOH (carboxyl) group
ExamplesHCl (hydrochloric), H₂SO₄ (sulphuric), HNO₃ (nitric), H₃PO₄ (phosphoric)CH₃COOH (acetic), C₆H₈O₇ (citric), H₂C₂O₄ (oxalic), C₄H₆O₆ (tartaric), C₃H₆O₃ (lactic), CH₂O₂ (formic)

3. Based on Concentration – Concentrated vs Dilute

  • Concentrated acid: Large amount of acid dissolved in small amount of water (high solute). Corrosive, requires careful handling.
  • Dilute acid: Small amount of acid dissolved in large amount of water (low solute). Safer to handle.
Safety: Always add acid to water, never water to acid! Adding water to concentrated acid produces extreme heat that can cause explosion.
Physical Properties of Acids
  • Sour taste (e.g., lemon, vinegar).
  • Turn blue litmus red.
  • Corrosive in nature – can damage skin, paper, fabric, and metals.
  • Good conductors of electricity in aqueous solution (due to ionization).
  • React with active metals to liberate hydrogen gas.
  • pH less than 7.
Chemical Properties of Acids

Reaction with Metals:

Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas ↑

Zn(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)↑

Test for H₂: Bring a burning splinter near the mouth of test tube – it burns with a pop sound.

Reaction with Carbonates & Hydrogen Carbonates:

Acid + Carbonate → Salt + CO₂ + H₂O

CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)

CO₂ turns lime water milky (Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O).

Reaction with Bases (Neutralization):

Acid + Base → Salt + Water + Heat

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + Heat

Reaction with Metal Oxides:

Acid + Metal Oxide → Salt + Water

CuO(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CuCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)

Black copper oxide dissolves to form blue-green copper chloride solution.

4. Bases – Properties & Classification

Definition (Arrhenius Concept)

A base is a substance that donates OH⁻ ions (hydroxide ions) when dissolved in water.

Example: NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻ (in water)

Classification of Bases

1. Strong vs Weak Bases

PropertyStrong BasesWeak Bases
IonizationCompletely ionize in waterPartially ionize in water
OH⁻ concentrationHighLow
pH12–148–11
ExamplesNaOH (sodium hydroxide), KOH (potassium hydroxide), Ca(OH)₂ (calcium hydroxide)NH₄OH (ammonium hydroxide), Mg(OH)₂ (magnesium hydroxide), Al(OH)₃ (aluminium hydroxide)

2. Alkalis (Water-soluble) vs Insoluble Bases

  • Alkalis are water-soluble bases. E.g., NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, NH₄OH.
  • Insoluble bases do not dissolve in water. E.g., Cu(OH)₂, Fe(OH)₃, Zn(OH)₂, Al(OH)₃.
  • All alkalis are bases, but NOT all bases are alkalis.
Physical Properties of Bases
  • Bitter taste.
  • Soapy or slippery feel to touch (due to saponification of skin oils).
  • Turn red litmus blue.
  • Good conductors of electricity in aqueous solution.
  • pH greater than 7.
  • Corrosive in concentrated form (especially strong alkalis like NaOH).
Chemical Properties of Bases

Reaction with Metals:

Base + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas ↑

2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) → Na₂ZnO₂(sodium zincate) + H₂(g)↑

Reaction with Acids (Neutralization):

NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + Heat

Reaction with Non-Metal Oxides:

Base + Non-metal oxide → Salt + Water

2NaOH(aq) + CO₂(g) → Na₂CO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)

This is why NaOH pellets absorb CO₂ from air and should be stored in airtight containers.

5. pH Scale – Acidity & Alkalinity

Definition

pH stands for Potenz of Hydrogen (power of hydrogen). It is defined as the negative logarithm of H⁺ ion concentration.

Importance of pH in Everyday Life

a) pH in Digestive System

Our stomach produces HCl (pH ~1.2) to kill bacteria and activate digestive enzymes. Excess acid causes acidity/heartburn, treated with antacids (mild bases).

b) pH of Soil

Plants grow best in specific pH ranges. Most prefer pH 6.5–7.5. Acidic soil is treated with quicklime (CaO) or slaked lime (Ca(OH)₂). Basic soil is treated with organic matter (compost).

c) pH & Tooth Decay

Bacteria in mouth produce acids by breaking down sugars. Acids attack tooth enamel (pH < 5.5 causes demineralization). Toothpaste is basic (pH ~8) to neutralize acid.

d) Acid Rain

Rain with pH < 5.6 due to SO₂ and NOₓ dissolving in rainwater forming H₂SO₄ and HNO₃. Damages buildings (marble – CaCO₃ reacts), soil, and water bodies.

e) Self Defence by Animals

Bees and ants inject formic acid (HCOOH) when they sting. Nettle stings contain methanoic acid. These are treated with mild bases like baking soda paste.

Remember: pH of human blood is strictly maintained at 7.35 – 7.45. If it drops below 6.8 or rises above 7.8, it is life-threatening.

6. Neutralization Reactions

Definition

Neutralization is a chemical reaction in which an acid and a base react with each other to produce a salt and water. The reaction is exothermic (releases heat).

General Equation:

Acid + Base → Salt + Water + Heat

Examples:

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + Heat
H₂SO₄(aq) + 2KOH(aq) → K₂SO₄(aq) + 2H₂O(l) + Heat
HNO₃(aq) + KOH(aq) → KNO₃(aq) + H₂O(l) + Heat

7. Salts – Types & Properties

Definition

A salt is a compound formed by the neutralization of an acid by a base. Salts are ionic compounds consisting of a cation (from base) and an anion (from acid).

General formula: Salt = Cation from base + Anion from acid
Types of Salts
TypeDefinitionExamples
Normal SaltAll H⁺ of acid replaced by metal; all OH⁻ of base replacedNaCl, Na₂SO₄, KNO₃, CaCO₃
Acidic SaltContains replaceable H⁺ atoms (from polybasic acid)NaHSO₄, NaH₂PO₄, NaHCO₃, KHCO₃
Basic SaltContains replaceable OH⁻ groups (from polyacidic base)Mg(OH)Cl, CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂, Pb(OH)NO₃
Double SaltTwo different salts crystallized together in fixed ratioPotash alum K₂SO₄·Al₂(SO₄)₃·24H₂O, Mohr's salt FeSO₄·(NH₄)₂SO₄·6H₂O
Complex SaltContains a complex ion (metal + ligands)K₄[Fe(CN)₆] (potassium ferrocyanide), [Cu(NH₃)₄]SO₄
pH of Salt Solutions (Hydrolysis)

When a salt dissolves in water, it may produce acidic, basic, or neutral solutions depending on the strength of the parent acid and base.

8. Common Salts – Preparation & Uses

Sodium Chloride – NaCl (Common Salt / Rock Salt)
  • Sources: Sea water (2.7% NaCl), rock salt deposits (Himalayan pink salt, Khewra mine).
  • Uses: Seasoning food, preserving meat/pickles, manufacture of NaOH, Cl₂, Na₂CO₃, soap.
  • Electrolysis of brine (Chlor-alkali process):
2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g)↑ + H₂(g)↑ (electrolysis)
  • Products: NaOH (caustic soda), Cl₂ (chlorine), H₂ (hydrogen).
  • Name "Chlor-alkali" because Cl₂ and alkali (NaOH) are produced.
Sodium Hydroxide – NaOH (Caustic Soda / Lye)
  • Manufacture: Chlor-alkali process (electrolysis of brine).
  • Uses: Manufacture of soap (saponification), paper, textiles (rayon), drain cleaners, petroleum refining, detergents, artificial fibres.
  • Storage: NaOH is hygroscopic (absorbs moisture) and reacts with CO₂ → must store in airtight containers.
Bleaching Powder – CaOCl₂ (Calcium Oxychloride)
  • Manufacture: Passing Cl₂ gas over dry slaked lime.
Ca(OH)₂(s) + Cl₂(g) → CaOCl₂(s) + H₂O(l)
  • Uses: Bleaching cotton, linen, wood pulp (in paper industry); disinfecting drinking water; oxidizing agent in chemical industry.
  • Chlorine smell due to slow decomposition with moisture.
Sodium Hydrogen Carbonate – NaHCO₃ (Baking Soda)
  • Preparation: Solvay process intermediate.
  • Uses: Baking agent (cakes/bread rise due to CO₂), antacid (neutralizes stomach acid), ingredient in fire extinguishers, cleaning agent.
  • On heating:
2NaHCO₃(s) → Na₂CO₃(s) + CO₂(g)↑ + H₂O(g) (heating)
Sodium Carbonate Decahydrate – Na₂CO₃·10H₂O (Washing Soda)
  • Preparation: Solvay process or heating baking soda.
  • Na₂CO₃ is obtained from baking soda by heating; then crystallized with 10H₂O.
  • Uses: Manufacture of glass (SiO₂ + Na₂CO₃ → Na₂SiO₃), soap, paper, water softening (removes Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺), detergents, borax.
  • Anhydrous Na₂CO₃ = soda ash.
Plaster of Paris – CaSO₄·½H₂O (Calcium Sulphate Hemihydrate)
  • Preparation: Heating gypsum to ~100°C (373 K).
CaSO₄·2H₂O(s) → CaSO₄·½H₂O(s) + 1½H₂O(g) (heated ~100°C)
  • When mixed with water, it sets into a hard mass (rehydrates to gypsum).
  • Uses: Making plaster casts for broken bones, sculptures and statues, construction (false ceilings), dentist's moulds.
  • Store in moisture-proof containers – otherwise it absorbs water and loses setting property.
Gypsum – CaSO₄·2H₂O (Calcium Sulphate Dihydrate)
  • Naturally occurring mineral.
  • Uses: Cement manufacturing (retarder – slows setting), fertilizer, plasterboard, blackboard chalk.
  • On heating to 100°C → Plaster of Paris; on heating to 200°C → anhydrous CaSO₄ (dead burnt plaster – does not set).
Key Difference: Baking soda (NaHCO₃) on heating gives Washing soda (Na₂CO₃). Gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) on heating gives Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O).

9. Water of Crystallization

Definition

Water of crystallization is the fixed number of water molecules chemically bound to a salt in its crystalline structure. These water molecules give the crystals their shape and colour.

Heating Effect on Hydrated Salts
  • CuSO₄·5H₂O (Blue) → CuSO₄ (White) + 5H₂O – on heating, blue colour disappears, white powder remains. Add water, blue colour returns (reversible).
  • FeSO₄·7H₂O (Green) → FeSO₄ (White) + 7H₂O – on heating, green turns white, then decomposes to Fe₂O₃ (red-brown) + SO₂ + SO₃.
Efflorescence, Deliquescence & Hygroscopy
TermDefinitionExample
EfflorescenceLoss of water of crystallization when exposed to air (crystals lose water and become powdery)Na₂CO₃·10H₂O → Na₂CO₃·H₂O (crystals turn white/powdery)
DeliquescenceSubstances absorb moisture from air and eventually dissolve in it (form solution)CaCl₂, MgCl₂, NaOH, KOH, P₂O₅
HygroscopySubstances absorb moisture from air but do NOT dissolve (just become sticky/damp)Concentrated H₂SO₄, NaOH pellets, Silica gel, CaO
Memory Aid: Efflorescence = Exit of water (loses water). Deliquescence = Dissolves (absorbs & dissolves). Hygroscopy = Holds moisture (just damp, no solution).

12. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Indicator Colour Tricks
Methyl Orange: Acid → Red, Base → Yellow. Trick: "MARA" = Methyl = Acid = Red. "MOBY" = Methyl Orange = Base = Yellow.
Phenolphthalein: Acid → Colourless, Base → Pink. Trick: "PBP" = Phenol = Base = Pink. In acid, it's "PAC" = Phenol = Acid = Colourless.
Litmus Trick: "BRA" = Blue litmus turns Red in Acid. "RAB" = Red litmus turns Blue in Base. The R in BRA and the B in RAB tell you the colour change direction!
pH Scale Trick
0–6 Acidic, 7 Neutral, 8–14 Basic. Trick: "ANB" = 0-6 Acid, 7 Neutral, 8-14 Basic. Or remember: "A is 1st letter, 0–6 (first range). N is middle, 7. B is 2nd letter, 8–14 (last range)."
Common Salts Formulas
Baking Soda = NaHCO₃: "Na HCO₃" – Think "NaHCO₃ = Na + H + CO₃ = Sodium Hydrogen Carbonate". The "Bi" in Baking is like "Bicarbonate".
Plaster of Paris = CaSO₄·½H₂O: "CaSO₄ dot half H₂O" – Plaster sets with half a water molecule. Gypsum = CaSO₄·2H₂O (full 2 water molecules).
Chlor-alkali products: "C·H·Na" = Chlorine (Cl₂) + Hydrogen (H₂) + Sodium hydroxide (NaOH). Listen to the name: Chlor (Cl₂) + alkali (NaOH).
Neutralization Applications
Bee sting vs Wasp sting: "Bee = Base treatment" (Baking soda). "Wasp = Acid treatment" (Vinegar). Remember: Bee → B (Baking soda / Base). Wasp → W (Well, use Vinegar/Acid).
Water of Crystallization
CuSO₄·5H₂O (Blue → White): "Copper Blue loses 5 H₂O turns White". Cu = Copper (brown/red metal). The hydrated salt is Blue. Anhydrous = White.
Strong Acids (6 common)
6 Strong Acids: "HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HBr, HI, HClO₄". Trick: "Clint's Black Socks Never Smell Briny In Hawaii" – HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HBr, HI, HClO₄.
