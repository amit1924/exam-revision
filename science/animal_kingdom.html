1 Introduction — What is the Animal Kingdom?
Key Characteristics of Animals
  • Heterotrophic — Cannot produce own food; depend on plants or other animals for nutrition
  • Multicellular — Body made of many cells organized into tissues, organs, and systems
  • No cell wall — Cell membrane is the outermost covering (unlike plants)
  • Motile — Most can move from place to place (at least during some life stage)
  • Eukaryotic — True nucleus and membrane-bound organelles
  • Store food as glycogen (not starch like plants)
  • Nervous system — Respond to stimuli via specialized nerve cells
  • Sexual reproduction — Most reproduce sexually; some also asexually

Types of Animals Based on Food Habits
  • Herbivores — Eat only plants/plant products. Examples: Cow, Deer, Rabbit, Horse, Elephant
  • Carnivores — Eat only other animals/flesh. Examples: Lion, Tiger, Snake, Eagle, Shark
  • Omnivores — Eat both plants and animals. Examples: Human, Bear, Crow, Cockroach, Pig
  • Scavengers — Feed on dead animals/carrion. Examples: Vulture, Jackal, Hyena, Beetle
  • Parasites — Live on or inside host, deriving nutrients at host's expense. Examples: Tapeworm, Leech, Louse, Mosquito
  • Decomposers — Break down dead organic matter. Examples: Bacteria, Fungi (not animals)
Habitat Classification
  • Terrestrial — Live on land. Examples: Lion, Elephant, Lizard, Human
  • Aquatic — Live in water. Examples: Fish, Whale, Octopus, Starfish
  • Freshwater — Rivers, ponds, lakes. Examples: Rohu, Frog, Hydra
  • Marine — Oceans, seas. Examples: Shark, Jellyfish, Coral, Dolphin
  • Amphibious — Live both on land and water. Examples: Frog, Crocodile, Turtle
  • Aerial — Capable of flight. Examples: Birds, Bats, Insects
  • Arboreal — Live in trees. Examples: Monkey, Squirrel, Koala, Chameleon
  • Burrowing — Live underground. Examples: Earthworm, Mole, Rabbit, Snake
2 Basis of Classification
Levels of Organization
  • Cellular level — Cells arranged as loose aggregates; no tissues. Example: Sponges (Porifera)
  • Tissue level — Cells form tissues (groups of similar cells). Example: Cnidaria, Ctenophora
  • Organ level — Tissues organized into organs. Example: Platyhelminthes
  • Organ system level — Organs work together in systems. Example: Nematoda to Chordata
Evolutionary Trend
Cellular \u2192 Tissue \u2192 Organ \u2192 Organ System \u2014 animals become more complex as we move up.
Body Symmetry
  • Asymmetrical — No plane of symmetry. Example: Sponges
  • Radial symmetry — Any plane through central axis gives equal halves. Examples: Cnidaria, Ctenophora, Echinodermata (adult)
  • Bilateral symmetry — Only one plane divides into mirror halves. Examples: Platyhelminthes to Chordata
Note
Echinoderm adults have pentamerous radial symmetry but larvae are bilaterally symmetrical.
Germ Layers & Coelom
  • Diploblastic — Ectoderm + Endoderm. Examples: Cnidaria, Ctenophora
  • Triploblastic — Ectoderm + Mesoderm + Endoderm. Most phyla
Coelom Types
  • Acoelomate — No body cavity. Example: Platyhelminthes
  • Pseudocoelomate — Cavity not lined by mesoderm. Example: Nematoda
  • Coelomate — Cavity lined by mesoderm. Examples: Annelida to Chordata

3 Phylum Porifera — Sponges
General Characteristics
  • Level of organization: Cellular level (no true tissues or organs)
  • Symmetry: Asymmetrical (irregular body shape)
  • Germ layers: None (no distinct germ layers)
  • Coelom: Absent (acellular mesenchyme)
  • Habitat: Mostly marine (few freshwater like Spongilla)
  • Body: Porous body with numerous pores (ostia) and one large opening (osculum)
  • Canal system: Water flows through body via ostia → spongocoel → osculum
  • Choanocytes: Specialized flagellated cells (collar cells) that create water current for food capture and gas exchange
  • Skeleton: Spicules (calcareous CaCO₃ or siliceous SiO₂) and/or spongin fibers (protein)
  • Digestive: Intracellular digestion only (within cells)
  • Circulatory: Absent (water current brings nutrients and oxygen)
  • Excretory: Absent (diffusion through body surface)
  • Nervous: Absent (no nerve cells; no response to stimuli)
  • Reproduction: Asexual (budding, fragmentation, gemmules) & Sexual (hermaphrodite; internal fertilization); lecithotrophic larvae
  • Regeneration: Remarkable regenerative capacity; can regenerate entire body from small fragments
  • Totipotency: Cells can change function and reorganize (unique among animals)
Detailed Examples

Sycon (also called Scypha) — Urn sponge

Marine, solitary or colonial; cylindrical body (1-3 cm); distinct fringe of spicules around the osculum; synconoid type of canal system (more complex than asconoid).

Spongilla — Freshwater sponge

Only freshwater genus; found in ponds, lakes, slow streams; encrusts submerged logs and plants; produces gemmules (asexual resting bodies with spongin coat) for overwintering and dispersal; green color due to symbiotic algae (zoochlorellae).

Euspongia — Bath sponge

Skeleton composed entirely of spongin fibers (no spicules); soft, elastic, highly absorbent; commercially harvested and used as bath sponge; Mediterranean and Caribbean species are most valuable.

Euplectella aspergillum — Venus flower basket

Deep-sea sponge (500-1000 m depth); beautiful glass-like skeleton of fused siliceous spicules forming a latticed cylindrical basket; often houses a breeding pair of shrimp (Spongicola) inside — symbol of eternal love in Japanese culture; used in jewelry.

Cliona — Boring sponge

Bores into calcareous substrates (oyster shells, coral skeletons, limestone) using both chemical dissolution and mechanical etching; yellow-orange encrusting form; causes significant bioerosion in coral reefs; can be a pest in pearl oyster beds.

Key Terminology
Ostia — Incurrent pores on body surface; Osculum — Large excurrent opening at top; Spongocoel — Central cavity (paragastric cavity); Choanocytes — Flagellated collar cells lining spongocoel; Pinacocytes — Flattened outer covering cells; Porocytes — Tubular cells forming ostia; Archaeocytes — Totipotent amoeboid cells; Gemmules — Internal buds for asexual reproduction; Monaxon/Triaxon — Spicule types based on number of axes.
4 Phylum Cnidaria (Coelenterata) — Jellyfish, Corals, Sea Anemones
General Characteristics
  • Level of organization: Tissue level (first animals with true tissues)
  • Symmetry: Radial symmetry (body parts arranged around central axis)
  • Germ layers: Diploblastic (ectoderm + endoderm + mesoglea in between)
  • Coelom: Absent (acoelomate)
  • Habitat: Aquatic, mostly marine; few freshwater (only Hydra)
  • Body forms: Polyp — Sessile, cylindrical, mouth upward (Hydra, Adamsia). Medusa — Free-swimming, umbrella-shaped, mouth downward (Aurelia).
  • Cnidoblasts (Nematocytes) — Specialized stinging cells on tentacles containing nematocysts (stinging capsules); used for defense, prey capture, and attachment
  • Polymorphism — Different types of individuals (zooids) in same species specialized for different functions (e.g., Physalia has gastrozooids, dactylozooids, gonozooids)
  • Metagenesis — Alternation of generations between asexual polyp (produces medusae by budding) and sexual medusa (produces gametes, zygote develops into planula larva which settles as polyp). Seen in Aurelia and Obelia.
  • Gastrovascular cavity — Coelenteron (single internal cavity) with mouth surrounded by tentacles; serves both digestion and distribution
  • Digestive: Incomplete (single opening serves as both mouth and anus); extracellular + intracellular digestion
  • Circulatory: Absent (gastrovascular cavity distributes food throughout body)
  • Nervous: Simple nerve net (diffuse network of neurons); no brain; nerve impulses spread in all directions
  • Excretory: No specialized organs; diffusion through body surface
  • Reproduction: Asexual (budding in polyps); Sexual (gametes produced by medusae); planula larva in many
Classes & Detailed Examples

Class Hydrozoa (alternation of polyp and medusa; medusa with velum)

  • Hydra — Freshwater polyp; solitary; no medusa stage; remarkable regenerative ability; studied extensively for cell biology and regeneration research; buds produced in favorable conditions; can reproduce sexually when conditions deteriorate.
  • Physalia physalis — Portuguese man-of-war; colonial, polymorphic; floats on ocean surface with gas-filled float (pneumatophore); long tentacles (up to 30m) with powerful neurotoxic venom; not a true jellyfish but a siphonophore colony.

Class Scyphozoa (True jellyfish; medusa dominant; no velum)

  • Aurelia aurita — Moon jellyfish; transparent, saucer-shaped medusa up to 40 cm diameter; 4 horseshoe-shaped gonads visible through bell; mild sting; metagenesis present (ephyra stage).

Class Anthozoa (Polyp only; no medusa stage; includes corals & sea anemones)

  • Adamsia palliata — Sea anemone; commensal with hermit crab (Eupagurus); attached to the crab's shell; provides camouflage and protection; crab provides locomotion.
  • Gorgonia ventalina — Sea fan; colonial anthozoan; forms fan-shaped colonies with horny gorgonin skeleton; common in Caribbean reefs.
  • Pennatula — Sea pen; feather-like colony with central axis (rachis) bearing polyp-bearing branches (pinnules); can bioluminesce when touched.
  • Meandrina meandrites — Brain coral; massive, dome-shaped; surface with meandering grooves (valleys) resembling brain convolutions; tentacles extended only at night.
  • Fungia — Mushroom coral; solitary (non-colonial); disk-shaped, up to 30 cm diameter; free-living on reef floor.
  • Tubipora musica — Organ pipe coral; bright red, parallel vertical tubes (like organ pipes); skeleton of fused calcium carbonate tubes with red color; living polyps are green.
Coral Reefs — Rainforests of the Sea
Corals (Anthozoa) secrete calcium carbonate (aragonite) skeletons that accumulate over thousands of years to form massive coral reef structures. The Great Barrier Reef (Australia) is the largest living structure on Earth, spanning over 2,300 km. Coral reefs support ~25% of all marine species. Zooxanthellae (symbiotic algae) live within coral tissues and provide up to 90% of coral's energy requirements through photosynthesis. Coral bleaching occurs when rising water temperatures cause corals to expel their zooxanthellae.
5 Phylum Ctenophora — Comb Jellies
General Characteristics
  • Level: Tissue level
  • Symmetry: Biradial symmetry (combination of radial and bilateral)
  • Germ layers: Diploblastic (ectoderm + endoderm + thick mesoglea)
  • Coelom: Absent
  • Habitat: Exclusively marine; planktonic in all oceans
  • Body: Transparent, gelatinous, soft-bodied; spherical, ovate, or flattened
  • Comb rows: 8 longitudinal rows of ciliated plates (ctenes) — each comb row is composed of many comb plates that beat in coordinated waves for locomotion; iridescent, refract light producing rainbow colors
  • Colloblasts (“lasso cells”) — Specialized adhesive cells on tentacles that secrete glue-like substance to capture prey (instead of cnidoblasts/stinging cells of Cnidaria). Each colloblast has a spiral filament that ejects and sticks to prey.
  • Tentacles: Two long tentacles (in tentaculate forms) or absent (in nudan forms); tentacles are retractile into sheaths
  • Bioluminescence: Most ctenophores are bioluminescent (produce blue-green light); they are the largest bioluminescent animals; responsible for much of the visible light in deep ocean waters
  • Digestive: Complete — mouth → pharynx → stomach → intestine → anal pores (2 small openings). Pharynx is muscular.
  • Nervous: Subepithelial nerve net; statocyst (balance organ) at aboral pole containing calcareous statolith; coordinates movement and orientation
  • Reproduction: Hermaphroditic (both sexes in each individual); gonads develop in walls of gastrovascular canals; external fertilization; direct development (no larval stage in most; cydippid larva in some)
  • Regeneration: High regenerative capacity; pieces can regenerate into complete individuals
Detailed Examples

Pleurobrachia pileus — Sea gooseberry

Small (1-2 cm), spherical body with 2 long retractile tentacles bearing colloblasts; 8 prominent comb rows; transparent in day, bioluminescent at night; feeds on copepods and small crustaceans; extremely common in coastal waters worldwide.

Ctenoplana — Creeping comb jelly

Flattened, creeping (not swimming) form; can both swim using comb rows and crawl on surfaces; known from Indian and Indonesian waters; interesting as it is considered an evolutionary intermediate between swimming and creeping ctenophores.

Hormiphora

Similar to Pleurobrachia; 8 comb rows producing iridescent colors; 2 tentacles with side branches (tentilla); high bioluminescence.

Important Notes for Exams
Ctenophora means “comb-bearing” (Greek: ktenos = comb, phoros = bearing).
Ctenophores are the largest animals that swim by ciliary action.
Unlike cnidarians, ctenophores have no alternation of generations.
They possess colloblasts (not cnidoblasts) for prey capture.
They have complete digestive tract (unlike cnidarians with incomplete digestive tract).
They are hermaphrodites with direct development (no metamorphosis).
6 Phylum Platyhelminthes — Flatworms
General Characteristics
  • Level: Organ level (first phylum with organ systems)
  • Symmetry: Bilateral (first bilaterally symmetrical phylum)
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic (ectoderm + mesoderm + endoderm)
  • Coelom: Acoelomate (no body cavity; mesoderm fills space between body wall and internal organs)
  • Body: Dorsoventrally flattened (ribbon-like/leaf-like), soft, unsegmented
  • Habitat: Free-living (freshwater, marine, moist soil) & Parasitic (endoparasites in animals and humans)
  • Digestive: Incomplete (single opening, mouth + pharynx + branched gastrovascular cavity; no anus). In tapeworms (Cestoda), digestive system is completely absent — they absorb nutrients directly through body surface (tegument).
  • Circulatory: Absent (no specialized circulatory or respiratory organs; flattened body allows diffusion for all cells)
  • Excretory: Flame cells (protonephridia) with solenocytes; excretory canals open through excretory pores. Flame cells are specialized ciliated cells that beat like a flame to propel waste fluids.
  • Nervous: Bilateral, ladder-like — cerebral ganglia (primitive brain) + longitudinal nerve cords connected by transverse commissures (ladder pattern). Sense organs: eyespots (ocelli) in free-living forms; chemoreceptors, tangoreceptors.
  • Reproduction: Most are hermaphroditic (both sexes in same individual); complex reproductive system with male and female organs; internal fertilization; cross-fertilization common; copulation often involves reciprocal insemination.
  • Life cycles: Many have complex life cycles with multiple larval stages and intermediate hosts (necessary for parasitic forms); can produce enormous numbers of eggs (e.g., Taenia produces up to 50,000 eggs per proglottid).
  • Regeneration: Free-living forms (Planaria) have remarkable regenerative ability; can regenerate complete head, tail, or entire body from small fragments; used in regeneration and stem cell research.
Detailed Examples with Medical Importance

Dugesia (Planaria) — Free-living flatworm

Freshwater, found under stones and leaves in ponds and streams. Body covered with cilia for gliding. Has auricles (lateral sensory projections) and two eyespots that detect light. Pharynx is protrusible (extends through mouth to capture food). Remarkable regeneration — can be cut into 279 pieces and each regenerates a complete organism. Neoblasts (pluripotent stem cells) are responsible for regeneration.

Taenia solium — Pork tapeworm

Endoparasite in human small intestine (adult). Worm can reach 2-7 meters length. Body: Scolex (head with 4 suckers + rostellum with hooks for attachment) + Neck (growth zone) + Strobila (chain of proglottids, up to 1000). Each proglottid is a reproductive unit (hermaphroditic). Gravid proglottids detach and pass out with feces. Intermediate host: pig (larvae form cysticerci in pig muscle). Humans get infected by eating undercooked pork containing cysticerci. Disease: Taeniasis (intestinal) and Cysticercosis (larvae in brain, eye, muscle — dangerous).

Fasciola hepatica — Liver fluke

Leaf-shaped (~3 cm), dorsoventrally flattened. Lives in bile ducts of sheep, cattle, and occasionally humans (zoonotic). Causes Fascioliasis (liver rot). Complex life cycle: Adult (bile duct) → eggs (feces) → miracidium larva (in water) → intermediate host: snail (Lymnaea) → sporocyst → redia → cercaria (free-swimming) → encysts on vegetation (metacercaria) → ingested by herbivore → excysts in duodenum → migrates to liver/bile duct. Economic importance: causes huge losses in livestock industry.

Schistosoma — Blood fluke

Unique among flatworms: dioecious (separate male and female individuals, rarely found in this phylum). Male is larger and forms a gynecophoral canal in which the slender female resides. Causes Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia) — affects over 200 million people in tropical countries. Life cycle: Eggs released in urine/feces → miracidium hatches in water → infects snail intermediate host → develops into sporocysts and cercariae → cercariae penetrate human skin during water contact → migrate to blood vessels (mesenteric/bladder). Symptoms: blood in urine, liver damage, bladder cancer (chronic cases). Control: snail eradication, improved sanitation, mass drug administration with praziquantel.

Echinococcus granulosus — Hydatid worm

Small tapeworm (3-6 mm, only 3-4 proglottids). Adult in intestine of dogs (definitive host). Larval stage causes Hydatid cysts in internal organs (liver, lungs, brain, bones) of intermediate hosts (sheep, cattle, humans). Hydatid cysts grow slowly (1-5 cm/year) and can reach enormous sizes (football-sized). Contains daughter cysts and protoscolices. Rupture can cause anaphylactic shock and secondary hydatidosis. Important in pastoral communities (sheep-raising areas).

Key Point for Exams
Platyhelminthes are the first bilaterally symmetrical, triploblastic, acoelomate animals. The name means “flat worms” (Greek: platys = flat, helmins = worm). They are the first phylum to have organ systems and cephalization (concentration of sensory and nerve tissues in head region).
7 Phylum Nematoda / Aschelminthes — Roundworms
General Characteristics
  • Level: Organ system level
  • Symmetry: Bilateral
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: Pseudocoelomate (body cavity not lined by mesoderm; pseudocoel develops from blastocoel)
  • Body: Cylindrical, elongated, unsegmented, thread-like; tapering at both ends
  • Cuticle: Tough, flexible, multi-layered outer covering (secreted by hypodermis); resistant to digestive enzymes; molted 4 times (ecdysis) during growth
  • Habitat: Ubiquitous — free-living in soil, freshwater, marine; parasitic in plants and animals (including humans); found in extreme environments (deep sea, polar regions, hot springs)
  • Digestive: Complete (first phylum with mouth and anus at opposite ends); triradiate pharynx (muscular pump); intestine with brush border for absorption
  • Circulatory: Absent (no heart or blood vessels; pseudocoel fluid serves for transport)
  • Respiratory: Absent (no specialized organs; diffusion through cuticle; anaerobic respiration in parasitic forms)
  • Excretory: Renette cells (glandular) / Excretory canal system (H-shaped); lateral canals in some
  • Nervous: Circumpharyngeal nerve ring + dorsal and ventral nerve cords; few sensory organs (amphids, phasmids)
  • Muscular: Only longitudinal muscles (no circular muscles); body wall musculature is separate, not continuous; hydrostatic skeleton (pseudocoel pressure); thrashing whip-like movement
  • Reproduction: Sexually dimorphic (males smaller, with curved tail and copulatory spicules; females larger, straight tail). Internal fertilization. Male has 1 testis, female has 2 ovaries. Mostly oviparous (lay eggs); eggs are resistant to environmental extremes.
  • Economic importance: Massive agricultural pests (root-knot nematodes); cause billions in crop losses. Beneficial: Caenorhabditis elegans (model organism in genetics and developmental biology).
Detailed Examples with Medical Importance

Ascaris lumbricoides — Human roundworm

Largest and most common human helminth infection (~800 million infected globally). Female up to 40 cm (vs. male ~25 cm). Lives in small intestine. Life cycle: Eggs passed in feces → develop in soil (embryonate) → ingested via contaminated food/water → hatch in small intestine → larvae penetrate intestinal wall → migrate to liver → heart → lungs (causes pneumonitis) → coughed up, swallowed → mature in small intestine. Disease: Ascariasis. Symptoms: abdominal pain, malnutrition (steals nutrients), intestinal blockage (heavy infection), growth retardation in children. Prevention: handwashing, proper sanitation.

Wuchereria bancrofti — Filarial worm (Elephantiasis)

Thread-like (3-10 cm). Causes Lymphatic Filariasis (Elephantiasis). Adult worms live in lymphatic vessels and lymph nodes. Female releases microfilariae (sheathed larvae) into blood; transmitted by female Culex mosquito (intermediate host). Chronic infection causes massive swelling of lower limbs, scrotum, and breasts due to lymphatic obstruction. Over 120 million people affected in tropical regions. Control: Mass drug administration (DEC + albendazole), mosquito control.

Ancylostoma duodenale — Hookworm

Small (1 cm). Buccal capsule with teeth (A. duodenale) or cutting plates (Necator americanus). Attaches to intestinal wall and feeds on blood (0.2 ml/worm/day). Causes Hookworm anemia (iron-deficiency anemia). Infection: Larvae penetrate skin (usually bare feet) → migrate through blood → lungs → trachea → swallowed → small intestine. Symptoms: ground itch (skin at entry site), pneumonitis (lung migration), anemia, fatigue, protein malnutrition. Common in tropical regions with poor sanitation and barefoot walking.

Enterobius vermicularis — Pinworm

Most common helminth infection in children in developed countries (up to 50% of school children). Small (female ~1 cm, male ~0.5 cm). Lives in cecum and appendix. Gravid female migrates to perianal region at night to deposit eggs (up to 15,000 eggs). Causes intense perianal itching (pruritus ani). Scratching leads to egg contamination under fingernails and autoinfection or transmission to others. Eggs are infectious within 4-6 hours. Diagnosis: “scotch tape test” (sticky tape applied to perianal skin in morning). Treatment: mebendazole/albendazole (entire family must be treated).

Trichinella spiralis — Trichina worm

Smallest nematode parasite of humans. Unique: same individual serves as definitive and intermediate host. Infection: eating undercooked pork containing encysted larvae (in striated muscle). Larvae excyst in stomach → develop to adults in small intestine → produce newborn larvae → migrate through blood to striated muscle → encyst (calcify). Disease: Trichinosis / Trichinellosis. Symptoms: diarrhea, muscle pain, fever, periorbital edema, eosinophilia. Heavy infection can be fatal (myocarditis, encephalitis). Prevention: cook pork thoroughly (to 71°C internal temperature).

Dracunculus medinensis — Guinea worm

Dracunculiasis (Guinea worm disease). Female worm can be 60-100 cm long. Infection: drinking water containing copepods (Cyclops) infected with larvae. Larvae penetrate intestinal wall, develop in abdominal cavity, adults mate; gravid female migrates to subcutaneous tissue (usually lower limb). Causes painful blister (burning sensation), worm emerges when blister contacts water. Control: filtration of drinking water (nylon filter), vector control, health education. Targeted for eradication by WHO (near elimination — only ~15 cases/year in 2024, down from 3.5 million in 1986).

Key Distinctions
Nematodes are pseudocoelomates — this is the first phylum with a body cavity (pseudocoel), though not fully lined by mesoderm. They are the first phylum with a complete digestive tract (mouth + anus separate), first with sexual dimorphism, and first with cuticle. The model organism Caenorhabditis elegans was the first animal to have its complete genome sequenced (1998) and all 959 somatic cell fates mapped.
8 Phylum Annelida — Segmented Worms
General Characteristics
  • Level: Organ system level
  • Symmetry: Bilateral
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: True coelom (eucoelomate/schizocoelom) — body cavity completely lined by mesoderm (peritoneum). Annelids are the first phylum with a true coelom.
  • Segmentation: Metameric — body divided into similar segments (metameres) arranged linearly; segmentation visible externally (annulations) and internally (septa compartmentalize coelom)
  • Body: Elongated, cylindrical; head region with prostomium and peristomium; terminal anus on pygidium
  • Locomotion: Setae/Chaetae (chitinous bristles) in each segment; parapodia (fleshy appendages) in polychaetes; circular + longitudinal muscles for peristaltic movement
  • Digestive: Complete — mouth → pharynx → esophagus → crop (storage) → gizzard (grinding) → intestine → anus. Calciferous glands in earthworm neutralize soil acidity.
  • Circulatory: Closed — blood flows within vessels (dorsal vessel, ventral vessel, lateral hearts, capillaries). The first phylum with closed circulation. Blood contains hemoglobin (dissolved in plasma, not in cells).
  • Respiratory: Through moist skin (cutaneous respiration — earthworm); gills (parapodial — Nereis); no specialized organs in oligochaetes
  • Excretory: Nephridia (segmentally paired). Types: protonephridia, metanephridia (with nephrostome opening into coelom). Earthworm has 3 types: septal, integumentary, pharyngeal nephridia.
  • Nervous: Cerebral ganglia (brain) + circumpharyngeal connectives + ventral nerve cord with segmental ganglia (double, fused). Nerves from each ganglion innervate the segment.
  • Reproduction: Oligochaetes and leeches are hermaphrodites (protandrous); polychaetes have separate sexes; clitellum (saddle-like glandular swelling) secretes mucus for copulation and cocoon for eggs; direct development (no larval stage) in terrestrial forms; trochophore larva in marine forms.
  • Regeneration: High regenerative ability (especially in oligochaetes and some polychaetes)
Classes & Detailed Examples

Class Oligochaeta (Few setae; no parapodia; hermaphrodite; clitellum present; terrestrial/freshwater)

  • Pheretima posthuma — Earthworm. Key features: 100-150 segments; clitellum on segments 14-16; 4 pairs of setae per segment (except 1st and last); dorsal pores (open from coelom) for lubrication; prostomium + peristomium form mouth; typhlosole (internal fold in intestine) increases absorptive surface. Reproductive system: male (testes in 10-11, seminal vesicles, prostate glands) + female (ovaries in 12-13, oviducts, spermathecae for storing sperm from mate). Cross-fertilization occurs in cocoons. Economic importance: vermicomposting (converts organic waste into nutrient-rich castings), soil aeration and drainage improvement, soil fertility enhancement.

Class Polychaeta (Many setae; parapodia present; separate sexes; mostly marine)

  • Nereis virens — Clamworm / Ragworm. Distinct head with tentacles, palps, eyes; each body segment bears a pair of parapodia (dorsal = notopodium, ventral = neuropodium) with setae; parapodia function in swimming, crawling, and gas exchange; predatory withversible pharynx and jaws; used as bait in recreational fishing.
  • Aphrodite aculeata — Sea mouse. Oval, dorsoventrally flattened body covered with dense mat of iridescent setae (resembles mouse fur); not a true polychaete but an errant form; lives in muddy sea floors; feeds on small invertebrates.
  • Arenicola marina — Lugworm. Burrowing polychaete in intertidal sandy/muddy shores; U-shaped burrow with head shaft and tail shaft; casts (coiled fecal mounds) on surface at one end; feeds by ingesting sand and digesting organic matter; important in marine ecosystems as bioturbator.

Class Hirudinea (Leeches; no setae; no parapodia; 2 suckers; hermaphrodite; clitellum present)

  • Hirudinaria granulosa — Indian leech. Semi-aquatic, ectoparasitic on vertebrates. Body dorsoventrally flattened with 33 segments; anterior sucker (with mouth) and posterior sucker (for attachment). Moves by “lingering looping” (inchworm) movement. Saliva contains hirudin (anticoagulant), hyaluronidase (spreading factor), and anesthetic. Feeds on blood. Medical use: Hirudotherapy (medicinal leech therapy) — used in reconstructive surgery, microsurgery (venous congestion), and treatment of arthritis.
Evolutionary Significance
Annelids are the first true coelomates. The segmentation (metamerism) is serial homologous — repeated segments with similar structures. This body plan is highly successful and also appears in arthropods and chordates. The trochophore larva of marine annelids shows relationship with molluscs.
9 Phylum Arthropoda — The Largest Phylum
General Characteristics
  • Level: Organ system level
  • Symmetry: Bilateral
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: Coelomate (but coelom is greatly reduced; body cavity is hemocoel filled with hemolymph)
  • Largest phylum: ~1.2 million described species (over 80% of all animal species); estimated 5-10 million total species
  • Body plan: Segmented; jointed appendages (Arthropoda = “jointed feet”). Body regions: Head, thorax, abdomen (insects); Cephalothorax + abdomen (crustaceans, arachnids); Head + trunk (myriapods)
  • Exoskeleton: Chitinous cuticle (chitin + protein, sometimes calcified). Functions: protection, support, muscle attachment, prevention of water loss. Shed periodically by molting / ecdysis (controlled by hormone ecdysone)
  • Digestive: Complete with specialized mouthparts (adapted for chewing, piercing-sucking, siphoning, sponging). Foregut + midgut + hindgut. Hepatopancreas in crustaceans.
  • Circulatory: Open (hemolymph bathes organs directly; dorsal heart with ostia; hemocoel sinuses). Hemocyanin (copper-based) or hemoglobin (rare) as respiratory pigments.
  • Respiratory: Tracheae (insects) — network of air tubes; spiracles (openings) with valves for regulation; Book lungs (arachnids) — stacked plates for gas exchange; Gills (crustaceans) — feathery, external
  • Excretory: Malpighian tubules (insects, myriapods) — blind tubules at midgut-hindgut junction; remove nitrogenous wastes (uric acid) from hemolymph. Green glands / Antennal glands (crustaceans). Coxal glands (arachnids).
  • Nervous: Well-developed; dorsal brain (supraesophageal + subesophageal ganglia) + ventral nerve cord with paired ganglia. Compound eyes (ommatidia mosaic vision) and simple eyes (ocelli). Antennae for touch, smell, taste.
  • Reproduction: Mostly dioecious (separate sexes); internal fertilization; many show metamorphosis (complete/incomplete); oviparous (lay eggs) or viviparous (some). Complex courtship behaviors.
Classes & Detailed Examples

Class Insecta (3 body parts: head, thorax, abdomen; 3 pairs of legs; 2 pairs of wings or reduced; 1 pair antennae)

  • Periplaneta americana — Cockroach. 3 body regions; 2 pairs of wings; 3 pairs of legs with coxa, trochanter, femur, tibia, tarsus; cursorial (running) legs. Mouthparts: biting-chewing type. Excretion: Malpighian tubules. Respiration: tracheal system with 10 pairs of spiracles. Nervous system: 6 ganglia in ventral nerve cord. Composite structure: head + thorax (pro, meso, metathorax) + abdomen with 10 segments and anal cerci. Used extensively in experimental biology.
  • Locusta migratoria — Migratory locust. Swarming behavior; causes massive crop damage (biblical plagues); phase polymorphism (solitary vs gregarious phases).
  • Apis cerana indica — Indian honeybee. Social insect (queen, workers, drones); honey and beeswax production; essential pollinator of crops; waggle dance communication.
  • Bombyx mori — Silkworm (sericulture). Larva produces silk thread from salivary glands (sericulture). Domesticated, cannot fly; completely dependent on humans.
  • Musca domestica — Housefly. Vector of typhoid, cholera, dysentery, polio; sponging mouthparts; disease transmission through regurgitation and defecation.
  • Anopheles — Malaria mosquito. Vector of Plasmodium (malaria parasite). Female is blood-feeder; rests at angle to surface (45°).
  • Culex — Filariasis mosquito. Vector of Wuchereria bancrofti (elephantiasis). Rests parallel to surface.
  • Aedes — Vector of dengue, chikungunya, Zika, yellow fever. White stripes on legs and thorax. Day-biting.
  • Cimex lectularius — Bedbug. Ectoparasite; nocturnal; feeds on blood; causes skin irritation, allergic reactions.
  • Pediculus humanus capitis — Head louse. Ectoparasite on human scalp; causes pediculosis (itching, secondary infections).
  • Lepisma saccharina — Silverfish. Primitive wingless insect; scales on body; feeds on starchy materials, paper, glue.
  • Xenopsylla cheopis — Rat flea. Vector of Yersinia pestis (bubonic plague); responsible for the Black Death pandemic (14th century) that killed 25-50 million people.
  • Laccifer lacca — Lac insect. Produces lac (resinous secretion) on host trees (palas, banyan). Used to make shellac (varnish, sealing wax, food coating, electrical insulation).

Class Crustacea (2 body parts: cephalothorax + abdomen; 2 pairs of antennae; mostly aquatic; biramous appendages; gills)

  • Cancer pagurus — Crab. Broad carapace; 10 walking legs (1 pair modified as claws/chelae); sideways walking; exoskeleton calcified.
  • Palaemon malcolmsonii — Indian river prawn. Important aquaculture species; rostrum with teeth; stalked compound eyes.
  • Penaeus monodon — Giant tiger prawn. Largest commercial shrimp; extensively farmed in coastal aquaculture.
  • Daphnia — Water flea. Freshwater zooplankton; carapace encloses body; filter-feeder; used as fish food and in toxicity testing.
  • Cyclops — Copepod. Freshwater/zooplankton; single median eye; serves as intermediate host for guinea worm (Dracunculus).

Class Arachnida (2 body parts: cephalothorax + abdomen; 4 pairs of legs; no antennae; book lungs or tracheae)

  • Spider (Araneae) — Web-builders; pedipalps (sensory); chelicerae (fangs) with venom glands; spinnerets produce silk from abdominal glands; book lungs for respiration.
  • Scorpion — Pedipalps as large pincers; long metasoma (tail) ending in telson with venomous sting; nocturnal; courtship dance (promenade a deux).
  • Limulus polyphemus — King crab / Horseshoe crab. Living fossil (unchanged for 450 million years). Blue blood (hemocyanin contains copper); blood used in Limulus amebocyte lysate (LAL) test to detect bacterial endotoxins in medical devices. Not a true crab (more related to arachnids than crustaceans). Book gills for respiration.

Class Myriapoda (Many-legged; head + trunk; 1 pair antennae; tracheae)

  • Scolopendra — Centipede (up to 30 cm). Carnivorous; 1 pair of legs per segment; poison claws (maxillipeds) for capturing prey; painful bite.
  • Julus / Millipede — Millipede (up to 2000 legs). Herbivorous; 2 pairs of legs per segment (diplosegment); defense: coiling into spiral and secreting repugnatorial fluid (hydrogen cyanide).
Connecting Link: Peripatus
Peripatus (phylum Onychophora) is a connecting link between Annelida and Arthropoda. Annelid features: segmented body, nephridia, smooth muscles, ciliated skin. Arthropod features: tracheae, chitinous jaws, open circulatory system, hemocoel. It has unjointed legs and a thin flexible cuticle. Provides evidence of evolutionary transition from annelids to arthropods.
10 Phylum Mollusca — Second Largest Phylum
General Characteristics
  • Level: Organ system level
  • Symmetry: Bilateral (many gastropods show torsion — asymmetrical due to twisting)
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: Coelomate (reduced to small cavities around heart, gonads, nephridia; hemocoel present)
  • Body: Soft, unsegmented; divided into Head + Foot + Visceral hump + Mantle
  • Mantle (Pallium) — Fold of skin covering visceral hump; secretes the calcareous shell; encloses the mantle cavity
  • Mantle cavity — Space between mantle and body; houses gills (ctenidia), osphradium, openings of digestive, excretory, and reproductive systems
  • Shell: Calcareous (CaCO₃, mainly aragonite/calcite) exoskeleton; 1 (gastropods), 2 (bivalves), 8 (polyplacophorans), or internal/reduced (cephalopods, slugs)
  • Radula — Unique rasping organ (chitinous ribbon with teeth) in mouth; used for scraping food; present in all molluscs except bivalves
  • Foot — Muscular ventral structure for locomotion (creeping, burrowing, swimming); modified into tentacles/arms in cephalopods
  • Digestive: Complete; buccal cavity with radula; salivary glands; stomach with style/sorting area; digestive gland (hepatopancreas); anus empties into mantle cavity
  • Circulatory: Open (heart with ventricle + 1-2 auricles; hemolymph in sinuses). Cephalopods (squid, octopus) have closed circulatory system (exception).
  • Respiratory: Ctenidia (feathery gills) in mantle cavity; some have lung (pulmonary sac) for air-breathing (terrestrial snails)
  • Excretory: 1-2 metanephridia (kidneys); open into mantle cavity
  • Nervous: Circumesophageal nerve ring + paired ganglia (cerebral, pedal, pleural, visceral, buccal); sense organs: eyes, tentacles, statocysts, osphradium
  • Reproduction: Separate sexes or hermaphrodite; internal/external fertilization; trochophore or veliger larva; direct development in some
  • Size: Microscopic (1 mm) to giant squid (13+ m, the largest invertebrates)
Detailed Examples

Pila globosa — Apple snail (Gastropoda)

Freshwater; spiral shell; operculum closes shell opening to prevent drying; has both ctenidia (gills) and a pulmonary sac (lung) — amphibious respiratory adaptation; herbivorous; pest in paddy fields; aestivates in mud during dry season.

Pinctada vulgaris / Pinctada margaritifera — Pearl oyster (Bivalvia)

Marine bivalve; produces pearls (nacreous layer of shell secreted around an irritant, usually a parasite or grain of sand). Cultured pearl industry (Mikimoto). Pearl layers: nacre (mother of pearl) composed of aragonite platelets + conchiolin protein. Important in jewelry.

Sepia officinalis — Cuttlefish (Cephalopoda)

Marine; 8 arms + 2 retractile tentacles; internal calcareous shell (cuttlebone) used as calcium supplement for birds; ink sac releases sepia ink (used in old ink and as food coloring); jet propulsion (siphon); color-changing chromatophores; highly intelligent.

Loligo vulgaris — Squid (Cephalopoda)

Streamlined body; 8 arms + 2 tentacles (with suckers on stalks); internal pen (remnant of shell); jet propulsion is fastest invertebrate movement (up to 40 km/h). Giant squid (Architeuthis) up to 13m — largest invertebrate. Squid are important in marine food webs and as seafood (calamari).

Octopus vulgaris — Devilfish (Cephalopoda)

8 arms with suckers; no shell; highly intelligent (largest brain/invertebrate, problem-solving, tool use); camouflage (chromatophores, papillae); can change color, texture, and shape; venomous (blue-ringed octopus); solitary, nocturnal; den-living.

Aplysia californica — Sea hare (Gastropoda)

Marine; reduced internal shell; named for rabbit-ear-like rhinophores; produces purple ink when disturbed. Critical in neuroscience — has giant neurons (up to 1 mm diameter) that enabled mapping of neural circuits for learning and memory (Eric Kandel, Nobel Prize 2000).

Dentalium — Tusk shell (Scaphopoda)

Marine; tubular, tusk-like shell open at both ends; burrows in sand with foot; feeds on foraminifera using captacula (thread-like tentacles).

Chaetopleura / Chiton — Chiton (Polyplacophora)

Marine; 8 overlapping dorsal shell plates (valves) surrounded by girdle; clings tightly to rocks; grazes algae with radula; uses wide, muscular foot for adhesion and slow movement.

Unio — Freshwater mussel (Bivalvia)

Burrows in mud/sand of ponds and rivers; filter-feeder; siphons for water intake and outflow; parasitic larval stage (glochidium) attaches to fish gills for dispersal.

Mytilus edulis — Sea mussel (Bivalvia)

Marine; attached to hard surfaces by byssus threads (proteinaceous); filter-feeder; widely cultivated as seafood (mussel farming); important in aquaculture.

Helix pomatia — Garden snail (Gastropoda)

Terrestrial; spiral shell; pulmonary lung (no gills); hermaphrodite; aestivates and hibernates by sealing shell opening with mucus plug (epiphragm); eaten as escargot (delicacy in French cuisine).

11 Phylum Echinodermata
General Characteristics
  • Level: Organ system level
  • Symmetry: Pentamerous radial (adult); bilateral (larva). Larvae must be bilateral (indicates evolutionary relationship to chordates).
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: Coelomate (enterocoelom — coelom forms from pouches of archenteron)
  • Habitat: Strictly marine (all species; none in freshwater or terrestrial). Found from intertidal to abyssal depths.
  • Etymology: Echinoderm = “spiny-skinned” (Greek: echinos = hedgehog, derma = skin)
  • Endoskeleton: Calcareous ossicles (plates of CaCO₃, calcite form) embedded in dermis; covered by epidermis. Ossicles have mesh-like structure (stereom). Spines project from ossicles in many species.
  • Water Vascular System (WVS) — Unique to this phylum! Functions in locomotion, feeding, respiration, excretion, sensory perception. Components: Madreporite (sieve plate) → Stone canal → Ring canal → Radial canals → Tube feet (podia). Tube feet are extensions of coelom; operate by hydraulic pressure; have ampulla and podium.
  • Digestive: Complete (mouth on oral side, anus on aboral side). Stomach (cardiac + pyloric); digestive glands. Sea cucumbers have respiratory trees (also excretory).
  • Circulatory: Open (hemal system — reduced). Perihaemal sinuses accompany hemal vessels.
  • Respiratory: Tube feet, dermal branchiae (papillae), respiratory trees (holothurians), bursae (brittle stars).
  • Excretory: No specialized excretory organs (diffusion through tube feet and body surfaces).
  • Nervous: Oral nerve ring + radial nerves; no prominent brain; ectoneural and hyponeural systems. Sensory: eyespots (starfish), pedicellariae, sensory tube feet.
  • Reproduction: Separate sexes (rarely hermaphroditic). External fertilization (shedding gametes into water). Free-swimming bilateral larvae (bipinnaria, brachiolaria, pluteus, auricularia) undergo metamorphosis.
  • Regeneration: Exceptional regenerative ability. Starfish can regenerate entire body from a single arm (with part of central disk).
  • Autotomy: Self-amputation of arms (brittle stars, starfish) as defense mechanism.
  • Pedicellariae: Small pincer-like structures on starfish and sea urchin surfaces; keep body clean of debris and small predators.
Detailed Examples

Asterias rubens — Common starfish / Sea star

5 arms radiating from central disk; madreporite on aboral surface; tube feet in 2-4 rows along ambulacral grooves; mouth on oral side (center). Eversible cardiac stomach (can evert through mouth to digest prey externally). Carnivorous, feeding on bivalves (oysters, clams) — pries shells open with tube feet. Pests in oyster beds. Remarkable regeneration: one arm + small disk piece can regenerate full starfish.

Echinus esculentus — Sea urchin

Globular, no arms; rigid test (fused ossicles) with long movable spines (articulated at base). Five rows of tube feet. Complex chewing apparatus: Aristotle's lantern (5 teeth arranged pyramidally, operated by muscles). Herbivorous (grazes on algae using lantern). Important in controlling algal overgrowth on reefs. Gonads (“uni”) eaten as delicacy in Japanese, Mediterranean, and Chilean cuisines.

Antedon bifida — Sea lily / Feather star

Sessile (stalked) in deep water; free-swimming as adult (feather stars); 5 arms branching into 10-200 feathery pinnules; mouth on oral side (upward); suspension feeder (captures plankton with tube feet). Most primitive living echinoderm. Fossil crinoids (“sea lilies”) were abundant in Paleozoic seas; common as fossils.

Cucumaria / Holothuria — Sea cucumber

Soft-bodied, cucumber-shaped; reduced ossicles (microscopic); no spines. Anus at one end, mouth at other (bilateral tendency). Calcareous ring around mouth. Subclass: Holothuroidea. Respiration: respiratory trees (paired, branched outgrowths from cloaca). Defense: evisceration (expels internal organs, including respiratory trees and gonads, through anus) to confuse predators; organs regenerate. Some produce toxic saponins (holothurin). Many harvested for “beche-de-mer” (trepang) in Asian cuisine and traditional medicine.

Ophiura ophiura — Brittle star

5 slender, flexible arms sharply demarcated from central disk; arms move side-to-side (serpentine). No arms grooves (ambulacral grooves covered). Fast-moving; rapid serpentine locomotion. Arms break off easily (autotomy). Lacks an anus. Filter-feeder / scavenger / predator of small invertebrates. Abundant in marine benthic communities.

Echinocardium cordatum — Heart urchin

Irregular echinoid (heart-shaped); burrowing in sand; spines modified for digging; tube feet long, specialized for building respiratory channels to surface.

Why Echinoderms Are Unique
Echinoderms are the only phylum with pentamerous radial symmetry in adults but bilateral larvae. The water vascular system is found nowhere else in the animal kingdom. Molecular evidence places echinoderms within the deuterostomes (closely related to chordates). Larvae (bipinnaria, pluteus) are bilaterally symmetrical and free-swimming, undergoing dramatic metamorphosis to become radial adults.
12 Phylum Hemichordata
General Characteristics
  • Level: Organ system level
  • Symmetry: Bilateral
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: Coelomate (enterocoelom; three coelomic compartments corresponding to 3 body parts)
  • Habitat: Exclusively marine; shallow coastal waters; burrowing in sand or mud
  • Body: Soft, worm-like; divided into 3 distinct regions: Proboscis (anterior, conical), Collar (short, around proboscis base), Trunk (elongated, posterior, containing most organs). Body wall: ciliated epidermis + muscles.
  • Stomochord — Short structure in proboscis; arises from roof of buccal cavity; NOT homologous to chordate notochord (considered analogous/convergent). Some consider it a pre-chordal structure.
  • Pharyngeal gill slits — 1 to many pairs in trunk. Open from pharynx to exterior (like chordates). Used for filter-feeding (water enters mouth, passes through gill slits, food particles trapped).
  • Digestive: Complete; straight tube from mouth (between proboscis and collar) → pharynx (with gill slits) → esophagus → intestine → anus at posterior end of trunk
  • Circulatory: Open (heart + dorsal and ventral blood vessels; heart is dorsal, unlike chordates). Heart vesicle in proboscis.
  • Excretory: Glomerulus / Proboscis gland — tuft of blood vessels projecting into coelom; filters waste
  • Nervous: Dorsal nerve cord in collar (may be hollow in some) + ventral nerve cord in trunk. Dorsal cord is a partial tube (not completely hollow like true chordates).
  • Reproduction: Separate sexes; external fertilization; free-swimming ciliated larva: Tornaria (resembles echinoderm larva — suggests affinity). Some have direct development.
  • Significance: Considered a connecting link between non-chordates (invertebrates) and chordates because they share features with both groups.
Examples

Balanoglossus gigas — Acorn worm / Tongue worm

Most well-known hemichordate. Body size up to 1.5 m in some species. Burrows in U-shaped burrows in intertidal sandy/muddy sediments. Proboscis is firm, acorn-shaped (hence “acorn worm”). Collar has mouth on ventral side. Trunk has numerous gill slits (100+ pairs) and hepatic caeca. Produces characteristic “iodoform” smell (due to brominated compounds). Feeds on organic matter in sediment. Tornaria larva closely resembles bipinnaria larva of starfish, providing strong evidence for deuterostome relationships.

Saccoglossus kowalevskii

Smaller acorn worm (5-15 cm). Important in developmental biology and evolutionary studies. Used in genomic studies of chordate origins. Has been key in understanding the evolution of the deuterostome body plan.

Phylogenetic Position
Hemichordates share with echinoderms: enterocoely, tripartite coelom, tornaria larva similar to bipinnaria, and biochemical similarities (phosphocreatine). Share with chordates: pharyngeal gill slits, dorsal nerve cord (at least partly hollow), and stomochord (precursor to notochord?). This places hemichordates as the sister group to echinoderms, and together they are sister to chordates within Deuterostomia.
13 Phylum Chordata
Four Fundamental Characteristics (present at some stage in life)
  • Notochord — Flexible, rod-like structure along the back (dorsal). Derived from mesoderm. Provides skeletal support. In vertebrates, replaced by vertebral column (backbone) during development.
  • Dorsal hollow nerve cord — Nerve cord positioned dorsal (above) to the notochord. Hollow, fluid-filled (vs. solid ventral cord in non-chordates). Develops into central nervous system: brain + spinal cord.
  • Pharyngeal gill slits — Paired openings from pharynx to exterior. In aquatic chordates, bear gills for respiration. In terrestrial vertebrates, present only in embryonic stages; modified into ear (Eustachian tube), tonsils, parathyroids, thymus.
  • Post-anal tail — Muscular tail extending posterior to the anus. Provides propulsion in aquatic species. Reduced or absent in adult humans (coccyx).
Additional Chordate Features
Bilateral symmetry, triploblastic, coelomate, closed circulatory system, complete digestive system, ventral heart (heart positioned below the digestive tract), hemoglobin in RBCs (most), endoskeleton (bone/cartilage in most).
Three Subphyla of Chordata

Subphylum 1: Urochordata (Tunicata) — Notochord only in larval tail

  • Larva is free-swimming tadpole-like (with notochord in tail). Adult is sessile, filter-feeding, enclosed in tunic
  • Ascidia — Sea squirt. Solitary or colonial; tunic composed of tunicin (cellulose-like substance, unique in animals!). Sessile adult; larva uses notochord and tail to swim, settle, and metamorphose into sessile form. Larva loses notochord and tail during metamorphosis.
  • Salpa — Free-swimming pelagic tunicate; barrel-shaped; transparent; alternation of generations (solitary sexual and aggregate asexual).
  • Doliolum — Transparent, barrel-shaped; muscle bands for water pumping; alternation of generations.

Subphylum 2: Cephalochordata — Notochord persists throughout life, extends into head

  • Branchiostoma (Amphioxus) — Lancelet. Small (5-8 cm), fish-like, translucent body; no distinct head (no eyes, no brain). Notochord extends from tip of tail to tip of rostrum. Has all 4 chordate features throughout life. Segmental muscle blocks (myotomes). Filter-feeds with oral hood and cirri. Important model for understanding chordate origins and vertebrate evolution. Considered the closest living relative of vertebrates.

Subphylum 3: Vertebrata (Craniata) — Notochord replaced by vertebral column; well-developed brain in cranium

  • All vertebrates belong here. Includes 7 classes: Cyclostomata, Chondrichthyes, Osteichthyes, Amphibia, Reptilia, Aves, Mammalia.

14 Vertebrates — All 7 Classes
14.1 Class Cyclostomata — Jawless Fishes
  • Jawless (Agnatha) — most primitive living vertebrates; no jaws or paired fins
  • Mouth: Round, suctorial, without jaws; horny teeth in buccal funnel
  • Body: Elongated, eel-like; no scales; no paired appendages
  • Gills: 6-15 pairs of gill slits (unlike other fish which have 5 or fewer)
  • Skeleton: Entirely cartilaginous; persistent notochord (no vertebrae)
  • Ectoparasitic (Petromyzon) or scavengers (Myxine)
  • Marine but some anadromous (migrate to freshwater to spawn)
  • No stomach — esophagus connects directly to intestine
  • Heart: 2-chambered; brain with distinct regions

Petromyzon marinus — Sea lamprey

Marine, anadromous (spawns in freshwater rivers). Buccal funnel with horny teeth for attachment to fish. Secretes anticoagulant. After spawning, adults die. Larvae (ammocoetes) live in freshwater for 3-7 years, filter-feeding, resemble Amphioxus. Invasive in Great Lakes (damaged fishing industry).

Myxine glutinosa — Atlantic hagfish

Marine, deep-water (100-1000 m). Scavenger on dead/dying fish. Has 4 pairs of tentacles around mouth. Produces copious slime (up to 20 liters per individual) from slime glands along body. Slime clogs predators' gills. Knotting behavior — ties body in knot to gain leverage for tearing prey or to escape predators. Has a partial skull but no vertebrae. Primitive immune system.

14.2 Class Chondrichthyes — Cartilaginous Fish
  • Skeleton: Entirely cartilaginous (no true bone; cartilage may be calcified)
  • Scales: Placoid scales (dermal denticles — tooth-like structures with enamel + dentine + pulp)
  • Mouth: Ventral (on underside of head)
  • Tail fin: Heterocercal (upper lobe larger than lower)
  • Air bladder: Absent — must swim constantly to avoid sinking (buoyancy from large oil-filled liver)
  • Heart: 2-chambered (1 atrium + 1 ventricle)
  • Excretion: Ureotelic — retain high urea concentration (2-2.5%) in blood for osmoregulation (osmotic balance with seawater)
  • Fertilization: Internal (males have claspers, modified pelvic fins)
  • Viviparous/ovoviviparous (most); some are oviparous (egg cases “Mermaid's purses”)
  • No operculum; 5-7 pairs of exposed gill slits
  • Spiracles — small openings behind eyes for water intake (important for benthic species)

Important Examples:

  • Scoliodon sorrakowah — Indian dogfish / Shark. Common small coastal shark; used in fish markets and shark liver oil.
  • Pristis pristis — Sawfish. Long, flat snout (rostrum) with lateral teeth (looks like a saw); uses it to slash/stun prey; critically endangered.
  • Carcharodon carcharias — Great white shark. Largest predatory fish (up to 6m, 2,000 kg). Apex predator; can detect blood from 5 km away; warm-blooded regionally.
  • Rhiniodon typus — Whale shark. Largest fish (up to 12-18 m, 20+ tons). Filter feeder (plankton, small fish). Endangered. Docile. Found in tropical oceans.
  • Torpedo torpedo — Electric ray. Disc-shaped body; electric organs (modified muscles) in disc produce up to 220 volts for stunning prey and defense.
  • Trygon (Dasyatis) — Stingray. Flat body; whiplike tail with serrated venomous spine (causes extreme pain, tissue necrosis).
  • Sphyrna lewini — Hammerhead shark. T-shaped head (cephalofoil) with eyes at lateral ends; enhanced 360° vision and electroreception (ampullae of Lorenzini).
14.3 Class Osteichthyes — Bony Fish
  • Skeleton: Bony (bone = calcium phosphate in collagen matrix)
  • Scales: Cycloid (smooth, overlapping) or Ctenoid (with comb-like ridges on posterior edge)
  • Mouth: Terminal (at front of head)
  • Tail fin: Homocercal (upper and lower lobes equal)
  • Air bladder (Swim bladder) — Present; gas-filled sac that regulates buoyancy (allows fish to stay at desired depth without swimming). Derived from lung.
  • Operculum: Bony plate covering gills (4 pairs of gills)
  • Heart: 2-chambered (sinus venosus + atrium + ventricle + conus arteriosus)
  • Excretion: Ammonotelic (excrete ammonia as dilute urine, losing water)
  • Fertilization: Mostly external (spawning); oviparous (lay eggs); some are ovoviviparous
  • Habitat: Marine (~58%) & Freshwater (~41%); some are diadromous (migrate between fresh and saltwater)
  • Lateral line system — sensory organ detecting water vibrations and pressure changes

Important Examples:

  • Labeo rohita — Rohu. Indian major carp; freshwater; important aquaculture species in South Asia; herbivorous; grows to 60+ kg.
  • Catla catla — Catla. Indian major carp; surface feeder (zooplankton); large upturned mouth; fast-growing, up to 70 kg.
  • Cirrhinus mrigala — Mrigal. Indian major carp; bottom feeder; detritivore.
  • Clarias batrachus — Magur. Air-breathing catfish; accessory respiratory organs (suprabranchial chamber / labyrinth organ); can survive out of water for extended periods; freshwater.
  • Exocoetus volitans — Flying fish. Enlarged pectoral fins allow gliding above water surface (up to 200m); escapes predators.
  • Hippocampus kuda — Seahorse. Unique upright posture; prehensile tail; horse-like head; male has brood pouch (male gives birth); monogamous courtship dance.
  • Betta splendens — Siamese fighting fish. Labyrinth organ (air-breathing); brilliant colors and long fins (selectively bred); highly aggressive males.
  • Pterophyllum scalare — Angelfish. Freshwater; disc-shaped; tall dorsal and anal fins; popular aquarium fish; Amazon basin.
  • Anabas testudineus — Climbing perch. Labyrinth organ; can move short distances on land using opercular spines and tail; survives in low-oxygen water.
  • Channa striata — Murrel / Snakehead. Predatory; air-breathing (suprabranchial chamber); important food fish in Asia.
  • Echeneis naucrates — Sucker fish / Remora. Dorsal fin modified into oval adhesive sucker (sucking disc) on top of head; attaches to sharks, rays, turtles, boats for transport and food scraps.
  • Anguilla anguilla — European eel. Catadromous (lives in freshwater, migrates to sea to spawn); remarkable migration to Sargasso Sea.
14.4 Class Amphibia
  • Dual life (Amphibian = “double life”) — aquatic larval stage + terrestrial/semi-terrestrial adult stage
  • Heart: 3-chambered (2 atria + 1 ventricle; partial interventricular septum reduces mixing of oxygenated/deoxygenated blood)
  • Respiration: Gills (larva) → Lungs (adult, but simple, sac-like + buccopharyngeal respiration) + Cutaneous respiration (moist skin)
  • Metamorphosis: Dramatic transformation from aquatic tadpole (herbivorous, with tail and gills) to adult (carnivorous, with legs and lungs)
  • Skin: Moist, glandular (mucous glands for moisture; poison glands in some), highly vascular (cutaneous respiration); no scales
  • Cold-blooded (Poikilothermic / Ectothermic) — body temperature varies with environment
  • External fertilization (most); eggs laid in water; eggs are anamniotic (no shell, no amnion); jelly coat protects eggs; gelatinous masses or strings
  • Hibernation (winter sleep, in cold climates) & Aestivation (summer sleep, in dry/hot climates)
  • Eardrum (Tympanum) visible externally; middle ear with columella (stapes) for hearing
  • Limbs: 4 limbs (tetrapods); feet webbed in aquatic forms; no claws
  • No ribs (or very short ribs) — breathing by buccal pumping

Important Examples:

  • Rana tigrina — Indian bullfrog / Tiger frog. Largest Indian frog (up to 15 cm). Green/brown with dark spots. Active during monsoon; breeds in rain pools. Eaten in some cultures.
  • Bufo melanostictus — Common Indian toad. Dry, warty skin; prominent parotid glands behind eyes (secrete bufotoxin, a cardiac toxin). Nocturnal. Crawls (doesn't jump).
  • Salamandra salamandra — Fire salamander. Lizard-like with bright yellow/black warning coloration. Lives in moist forests of Europe. Secretes poison from skin glands. Tail persists in adults.
  • Ichthyophis glutinosus — Limbless amphibian (Caecilian / Apoda). Burrowing, worm-like (body with annular folds). Vestigial eyes. Tentacle between eye and nostril (chemosensory). Found in moist soil in tropics (India, Sri Lanka).
  • Hyla arborea — European tree frog. Adhesive discs (toe pads) on digits for climbing. Green with dark lateral stripe. Nocturnal. Males have vocal sac.
  • Ambystoma mexicanum — Axolotl. Mexican salamander. Neoteny (paedomorphosis) — retains larval features (external gills, tail fin, aquatic) throughout life even when sexually mature. Critically important in developmental biology and regeneration research. Can regenerate limbs, tail, spinal cord, heart, and parts of brain.
  • Alytes obstetricans — Midwife toad. Male carries fertilized eggs wrapped around his hind legs for protection until hatching.
  • Rhacophorus malabaricus — Malabar flying frog / Gliding frog. Enlarged webbed feet and skin flaps on limbs allow gliding/parachuting between trees. Arboreal; breeds in tree holes. Found in Western Ghats (India).
Amphibian Decline
Amphibians are the most threatened vertebrate class (~41% of species threatened). Causes: habitat loss, climate change, pollution, chytrid fungus (Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis) causing chytridiomycosis, UV radiation, invasive species. Frogs are bioindicators of environmental health.
14.5 Class Reptilia
  • Crawling (Reptilia = “creeping”) — most move by belly crawling or on short legs
  • Skin: Dry, keratinized scales/scutes (epidermal or dermal); no glands; prevents water loss (first truly terrestrial adaptation). Ecdysis (shedding of skin) in snakes.
  • Heart: 3-chambered (2 atria + 1 ventricle with an incomplete septum). Exception: Crocodiles have 4-chambered heart (2 atria + 2 completely separated ventricles) — convergent evolution with birds/mammals.
  • Poikilothermic (Ectothermic / cold-blooded) — bask in sun to raise body temperature; seek shade to cool
  • Respiration: Lungs only throughout life (no gills at any stage); better developed lungs than amphibians
  • Fertilization: Internal (males have copulatory organs: hemipenes in squamates)
  • Amniotic egg — First truly terrestrial egg: shell (calcareous/leathery) prevents desiccation; extraembryonic membranes (amnion, chorion, allantois, yolk sac). Amniotes: reptiles, birds, mammals. This innovation freed vertebrates from needing water for reproduction.
  • Most are oviparous (lay eggs); some are ovoviviparous or viviparous (e.g., some snakes, lizards)
  • First vertebrates to complete entire life cycle on dry land

Important Examples:

  • Testudo elegans — Indian star tortoise. Domed shell with yellow star-like pattern; terrestrial; herbivorous; slow-moving; popular in pet trade.
  • Chelone mydas — Green sea turtle. Marine; flippers for swimming; herbivorous (adult); nests on beaches; endangered.
  • Trionyx gangeticus — Indian softshell turtle. Flat, leathery shell (no scutes); aquatic; long snout for breathing at surface; Ganges river system.
  • Crocodilus palustris — Mugger / Marsh crocodile. Broad snout; 4-chambered heart; powerful jaws; estuarine and freshwater; found in Indian subcontinent.
  • Alligator mississippiensis — American alligator. U-shaped snout; freshwater; less aggressive than crocodiles.
  • Gavialis gangeticus — Gharial. Long, narrow snout with bulbous tip (ghara — used by males to produce sounds); fish-eating; critically endangered; found only in rivers of northern India and Nepal.
  • Hemidactylus frenatus — Common house lizard. Adhesive toe pads (lamellae) for climbing; tail autotomy (can detach tail); nocturnal; insectivorous.
  • Chamaeleo zeylanicus — Indian chameleon. Prehensile tail; grasping feet (zygodactylous); projectile tongue (up to body length); color change (chromatophores); independently moving eyes; slow-moving arboreal.
  • Calotes versicolor — Garden lizard / Oriental garden lizard. Can change color (green to brown); prominent dorsal crest (males); diurnal; insectivorous.
  • Varanus salvator — Water monitor. Large lizard (up to 2.5 m); forked tongue; powerful limbs with claws; good swimmer; carnivorous.
  • Naja naja — Indian cobra (Spectacled cobra). Hood (cervical ribs spread when threatened); neurotoxic venom; iconic snake in Indian culture; responsible for most snakebite deaths in India.
  • Vipera russelii (Daboia russelii) — Russell's viper. Triangular head with heat-sensing pit; hemotoxic venom; highly dangerous; causes coagulopathy and renal failure.
  • Python molurus — Indian python. Large non-venomous constrictor (up to 8 m); kills prey by suffocation; heat-sensing labial pits.
  • Bungarus caeruleus — Common krait. Blue-black with white bands; highly neurotoxic venom (paralytic); nocturnal; often found in houses.
  • Typhlops braminus — Blind snake / Worm snake. Burrowing; vestigial eyes covered by scales; harmless; feeds on ants and termites.
  • Eryx conicus — Rough-scaled sand boa. Thick body; blunt tail resembles head (defense); burrowing; non-venomous constrictor.
  • Hydrophis spiralis — Yellow sea snake. Marine; paddle-like tail; highly venomous (neurotoxin); air-breather; found in Indian Ocean.
14.6 Class Aves — Birds
  • Feathers — Body covering of feathers (contour, down, filoplumes, flight feathers); unique to birds; provide insulation, flight, display. Derived from reptilian scales.
  • Forelimbs modified into wings (for flight). Some flightless: ostrich, emu, penguin (wings modified as flippers), kiwi.
  • Heart: 4-chambered (2 atria + 2 ventricles); complete double circulation; powerful (largest relative heart size). High heart rate (e.g., hummingbird ~1,200 bpm).
  • Homeothermic (Endothermic / warm-blooded) — maintain constant body temperature (~40-42°C, highest of all animals).
  • Bones: Pneumatic (hollow, air-filled, with internal struts for strength); light yet strong; fused bones (synsacrum, pygostyle, carpometacarpus, tarsometatarsus) for rigidity.
  • Respiratory: Lungs + air sacs (9 interconnected). Unidirectional airflow (air moves through lungs in one direction, unlike mammalian tidal flow); double respiration (oxygen absorbed during both inhalation and exhalation) — highly efficient oxygen extraction.
  • Beak (Bill) — Toothless, keratin-covered jaws; shape reflects diet (hooks = carnivore, flat = filter-feeder, conical = seed-eater, curved = nectar-feeder).
  • Digestive: Beak + Crop (storage) + Proventriculus (glandular stomach) + Gizzard (muscular, grinds food with grit/stones) + Intestine + Cloaca. No urinary bladder (excrete semisolid uric acid).
  • Vision: Excellent (largest relative eye size of any vertebrate, up to 15% of body mass); color vision (ultraviolet-sensitive), high acuity, nictitating membrane.
  • Reproduction: Internal fertilization; lay amniotic eggs with hard calcareous shell (incubated by parents); no copulatory organ (most); many build nests, care for young.
  • Migration: Many species undertake long-distance seasonal migrations (Arctic tern: ~70,000 km/year, pole-to-pole).

Important Examples:

  • Corvus splendens — House crow. Highly adaptable, omnivorous; intelligent (tool use); associated with human settlements across Asia and East Africa.
  • Columba livia — Rock pigeon / Domestic pigeon. Crop milk (produced by both parents, rich in protein/fat). Homing ability (magnetic field + sun compass + landmarks).
  • Pavo cristatus — Indian peafowl / Peacock. National bird of India. Male has spectacular iridescent train (elongated upper tail coverts) with eye-spots used in courtship display. Females choose males with more eyespots.
  • Psittacula krameri — Rose-ringed parakeet. Green; red beak; mimics human speech; zygodactyl feet (2 toes forward, 2 backward).
  • Neophron percnopterus — Egyptian vulture. Scavenger; uses stones to break ostrich eggs (tool use!); declining due to diclofenac poisoning.
  • Struthio camelus — Ostrich. Largest living bird (2.5 m, 150 kg). Flightless; 2-toed feet; powerful kick; fastest bird on land (70 km/h).
  • Apus apus — Common swift. Fastest flyer (horizontal: 110 km/h, record 170 km/h). Spends almost entire life in air (eats, sleeps, mates, drinks in flight).
  • Tyto alba — Barn owl. Heart-shaped facial disk; silent flight (special feather fringes); exceptional hearing (asymmetrical ears); nocturnal. Pellet regurgitation.
  • Aquila chrysaetos — Golden eagle. Large raptor; powerful hooked beak; sharp talons; keen eyesight (can spot rabbit from 2 km).
  • Passer domesticus — House sparrow. Common urban bird; declining in many cities (pollution, lack of insects, loss of nesting sites).
  • Anas platyrhynchos — Mallard duck. Dabbling duck; webbed feet; broad flat bill with lamellae for filter-feeding; migratory.
  • Gallus gallus domesticus — Domestic chicken. Descended from Red junglefowl (Gallus gallus). Most abundant bird (over 25 billion).
  • Coracias benghalensis — Indian roller. State bird of several Indian states. Brilliant blue plumage; aerial acrobatics during courtship.
  • Hirundo rustica — Barn swallow. Forked tail; graceful flight; builds mud nests under eaves; long-distance migrant.
  • Nectarinia asiatica — Purple sunbird. Hovering flight (like hummingbird); curved beak for nectar; iridescent purple breeding plumage.
  • Bubo bubo — Eurasian eagle-owl. Large (60 cm); ear tufts (horns); powerful predator of mammals, birds, reptiles.
14.7 Class Mammalia
  • Mammary glands — Produce milk to nourish young (defining characteristic, name derived from Latin: mamma = breast). All female mammals produce milk.
  • Hair / Fur — Body covered with hair (keratin filaments) at some stage. Functions: insulation, camouflage, sensory (vibrissae/whiskers). Reduced in aquatic mammals.
  • Heart: 4-chambered (2 atria + 2 ventricles); complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood
  • Homeothermic (Endothermic / warm-blooded) — maintain constant body temperature (~36-38°C) with high metabolic rate
  • Diaphragm: Muscular sheet separating thoracic and abdominal cavities; crucial for efficient respiration
  • Fertilization: Internal. Viviparous (most) — give birth to live young. Exceptions: Monotremes (platypus, echidna) lay eggs.
  • Brain: Highly developed neocortex (largest brain relative to body size among vertebrates); complex behavior, learning, memory.
  • Teeth: Heterodont (4 types: incisors, canines, premolars, molars) and Diphyodont (two sets: milk teeth + permanent teeth). Thecodont (teeth in sockets/Jaw bone).
  • Middle ear: 3 bones (Malleus, Incus, Stapes) — modified from reptilian jaw bones. Transmit sound from eardrum to inner ear.
  • Pinna (external ear): Present in most (absent in whales, some seals). Collects and focuses sound.
  • Orbit: Bony plate (postorbital bar) separating eye socket from temporal fossa (in most).
  • Lower jaw: Single bone (dentary) on each side, directly articulates with skull (squamosal-dentary jaw joint).

Subclasses:

  • Prototheria (Egg-laying mammals / Monotremes): Ornithorhynchus anatinus — Duck-billed platypus. Lays eggs; has duck-like bill (electroreceptors), webbed feet, beaver-like tail, and venomous spur (males). Echidna (Tachyglossus) — Spiny anteater. Egg laid into pouch; long snout for ants/termites.
  • Metatheria (Marsupials / Pouched mammals): Macropus giganteus — Eastern grey kangaroo. Powerful hind legs for hopping; pouch (marsupium) for developing young; birth at very early stage (2-3 cm) → crawls to pouch. Phascolarctos cinereus — Koala. Eucalyptus leaves specialist; pouch opens backward; long cecum for leaf digestion. Didelphis virginiana — Virginia opossum. Only marsupial in North America; “playing possum” (thanatosis).
  • Eutheria (Placental mammals — most diverse and successful): Complex placenta provides nutrients/gas exchange for developing fetus; longer gestation; fully developed young at birth (compared to marsupials).

Placental Mammals — Representative Examples:

  • Homo sapiens — Human. Most advanced brain (neocortex); bipedal; opposable thumbs; language; tool use; ~8 billion population.
  • Panthera leo — Lion. Social (prides); mane in males; apex predator; grassland habitats.
  • Panthera tigris — Tiger. National animal of India; striped coat (unique pattern); solitary; critically endangered (subspecies).
  • Panthera pardus — Leopard. Spotted (rosettes); highly adaptable; climbs trees with prey.
  • Elephas maximus — Indian elephant. Largest land animal in Asia (up to 5 tons). Trunk (fusion of nose + upper lip); tusks (elongated incisors); matriarchal herds.
  • Rhinoceros unicornis — Indian rhinoceros. Single horn; folded skin (armored look); Kaziranga National Park; endangered.
  • Balaenoptera musculus — Blue whale. Largest animal ever to exist (up to 33 m, 200 tons). Baleen plates (filter-feeds on krill). Endangered.
  • Delphinus delphis — Common dolphin. Echolocation (sonar); intelligent; social (pods up to 1000); acrobatic.
  • Pteropus giganteus — Indian flying fox. Largest bat; fruit-eating; roosts in large colonies (banyan trees); nocturnal; pollinator and seed disperser.
  • Canis lupus familiaris — Domestic dog. First domesticated animal (~15,000 years ago); descended from gray wolf; hundreds of breeds.
  • Felis catus — Domestic cat. Retractile claws; crepuscular; solitary hunter; descended from African wildcat.
  • Equus ferus caballus — Horse. Hoofed (odd-toed ungulate); used for transport, agriculture, sport.
  • Camelus dromedarius — One-humped camel. Hump stores fat (not water); adaptation to desert: water conservation, thick fur, long eyelashes, closable nostrils.
  • Bos indicus — Zebu (Indian humped cow). Sacred in Hinduism; high heat tolerance; hump is muscle/fat deposit.
  • Bubalus bubalis — Water buffalo. Important milk and draft animal in Asia; large horns; wallows in water.
  • Capra hircus — Goat. Agile climber; important for milk (cheese), meat, and fiber (cashmere, mohair, pashmina).
  • Ovis aries — Sheep. Wool production; flerd behavior; important livestock species.
  • Sus scrofa domesticus — Pig. Omnivorous; high intelligence; important meat source (pork, bacon, ham).
  • Rattus rattus — Black rat. Cosmopolitan; commensal with humans; historical vector of bubonic plague.
  • Mus musculus — House mouse. Most important laboratory animal in biomedical research; bred in many strains/genetic models.
  • Manis pentadactyla — Chinese pangolin. Covered in overlapping keratin scales; feeds on ants/termites; endangered (most trafficked mammal globally).
  • Macaca mulatta — Rhesus macaque. Widely used in biomedical research (Rh factor discovery, polio vaccine, AIDS research). Temple monkeys in India.
  • Loris tardigradus — Red slender loris. Nocturnal; large eyes; slow, deliberate movements; venomous brachial gland (elbow licking).
  • Semnopithecus entellus — Hanuman langur. Sacred in Indian mythology; silver fur, black face; leaf-eating (colobine).
  • Hylobates lar — White-handed gibbon. Lesser ape; brachiation (arm-swinging); no tail; monogamous; territorial singing.
  • Pongo pygmaeus — Bornean orangutan. Great ape; red hair; solitary; arboreal; critically endangered (deforestation).
  • Pan troglodytes — Chimpanzee. Closest living relative of humans (~98.8% DNA similarity). Tool use; complex social structure; hunting.
  • Phoca vitulina — Harbor seal. Marine mammal (Pinnipedia); flippers; blubber for insulation; whiskers (vibrissae) for prey detection.
  • Trichechus manatus — West Indian manatee. Sea cow; herbivorous; slow-moving; found in coastal waters, rivers, springs; vulnerable.
