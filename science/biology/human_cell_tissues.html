1. Discovery of Cell

The term "cell" was first coined by Robert Hooke in 1665. He observed a thin slice of cork (dead plant cells) under his self-made microscope. He saw small compartments like rooms in a honeycomb and named them "cellulae" (Latin for small rooms).

  • 1665 — Robert Hooke discovered cell in cork
  • 1674 — Antonie van Leeuwenhoek first observed living cells (bacteria, protozoa) — called them "animalcules"
  • 1831 — Robert Brown discovered the nucleus in orchid cells
  • 1839 — Purkinje coined the term "protoplasm" for the living fluid inside cells
Key Fact
Robert Hooke's microscope was a 30x magnification simple microscope. His observations were published in the book "Micrographia" (1665) — the first book about microscopic observations.

2. Cell Theory

The Cell Theory was formulated by Matthias Schleiden (botanist) and Theodor Schwann (zoologist) in 1838-1839.

  • Schleiden (1838): All plants are composed of cells
  • Schwann (1839): All animals are composed of cells; cells have a cell membrane
  • Rudolf Virchow (1855): "Omnis cellula e cellula" — all cells arise from pre-existing cells

Modern Cell Theory includes:

  • All living organisms are composed of cells
  • The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life
  • All cells arise from pre-existing cells (biogenesis)
  • Energy flow occurs within cells (metabolism)
  • Hereditary information (DNA) is passed from cell to cell
Mnemonic: "SSV"
Schleiden = Plants | Schwann = Animals | Virchow = Pre-existing cells

3. Cell Structure — Overview

A typical cell has 3 main parts: Cell Membrane (outer covering), Cytoplasm (jelly-like substance), and Nucleus (control center).

PartLocationFunction
Cell MembraneOutermost in animal cellsSelectively permeable; regulates entry/exit
CytoplasmBetween membrane & nucleusJelly-like matrix; site of metabolic reactions
NucleusCenter (usually)Controls cell activities; contains DNA
Cell WallOutside cell membrane (plants only)Structural support; protection
Detailed Parts
A. Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)
  • Made of lipids and proteins (fluid mosaic model — Singer & Nicolson, 1972)
  • Selectively permeable — allows some substances to pass through but not others
  • Structure: Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins
  • Functions: Protection, transport, cell-cell recognition, communication
B. Cell Wall (Only in Plants, Bacteria, Fungi)
  • Plant cell wall: made of cellulose (a carbohydrate)
  • Functions: Provides rigidity, maintains shape, prevents bursting
  • Fully permeable — allows all substances to pass through
  • Primary wall (thin, flexible) + Middle lamella (glues cells together)
C. Cytoplasm
  • Jelly-like protoplasm between cell membrane and nucleus
  • Contains organelles — small structures with specific functions
  • Cytosol = fluid portion; Cytoplasm = cytosol + organelles (excluding nucleus)
  • Site of many metabolic reactions (glycolysis, protein synthesis)
D. Nucleus — The Control Center
  • Discovered by Robert Brown (1831) in orchid root cells
  • Structure: Nuclear membrane (double-layered, porous), Nucleoplasm, Chromatin (DNA + proteins), Nucleolus (ribosome synthesis)
  • Functions: Controls cell division, growth, metabolism, heredity
  • Cells without nucleus: RBCs (mammalian) — enucleated; Platelets — anucleate
  • Cells with multiple nuclei: Skeletal muscle cells (syncytium), Osteoclasts

4. Cell Organelles — Structure & Functions

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
  • Network of membranous tubes & sacs called cisternae
  • Two types: Rough ER (RER) — has ribosomes on surface → protein synthesis
  • Smooth ER (SER) — no ribosomes → lipid synthesis, detoxification
  • Functions: Transport of materials, synthesis of proteins/lipids, detoxification
  • Discovered by: Porter, Claude & Thompson (1945)
Golgi Apparatus (Golgi Body)
  • Stack of flattened membrane sacs called cisternae
  • Discovered by: Camillo Golgi (1898)
  • Functions: Packaging, modification, sorting of proteins; lysosome formation
  • Secretory vesicles bud off from trans-face
Lysosomes — "Suicide Bags"
  • Membrane-bound sacs containing digestive enzymes (hydrolases)
  • Functions: Intracellular digestion, phagocytosis, autophagy, autolysis
  • Called "suicide bags" because if the lysosome bursts, it digests the entire cell
  • Discovered by: Christian de Duve (1955)
Lysosomal Storage Diseases
Deficiency of lysosomal enzymes causes diseases: Tay-Sachs (lipid accumulation), Pompe disease (glycogen accumulation), Gaucher disease
Mitochondria — "Powerhouse of the Cell"
  • Double-membrane organelle: outer membrane smooth, inner membrane folded into cristae
  • Inner space: Matrix — contains DNA, ribosomes, enzymes for Krebs cycle
  • Functions: ATP synthesis (cellular respiration), regulation of metabolism
  • Has its own DNA (mtDNA) and ribosomes (70S) — semi-autonomous organelle
  • Number per cell: Liver cells ~1000-2000, Sperm ~100-200
  • Maternal inheritance — mtDNA comes only from mother
Ribosomes — "Protein Factory"
  • Non-membrane bound organelles made of rRNA + proteins
  • Two subunits: 70S (prokaryotes, mitochondria) or 80S (eukaryotes)
  • Location: Free in cytoplasm OR attached to RER
  • Function: Protein synthesis (translation)
Centrosome & Centrioles
  • Found only in animal cells
  • Centrosome contains 2 centrioles arranged perpendicularly
  • Each centriole: 9+0 arrangement of microtubule triplets
  • Functions: Spindle fiber formation during cell division, cilia/flagella formation
Vacuoles
  • Fluid-filled sacs bound by tonoplast membrane (in plants)
  • Plant cells: Large central vacuole — provides turgidity and stores nutrients
  • Animal cells: Small vacuoles — food vacuole, contractile vacuole (in protozoa)
  • Functions: Storage, waste disposal, osmoregulation, maintaining turgor pressure
Plastids (Plant Cells Only)
  • Types: Leucoplasts (colorless — storage), Chromoplasts (colored — pigments), Chloroplasts (green — photosynthesis)
  • Chloroplast: Double membrane + internal thylakoids (stacked as grana) + stroma
  • Chloroplast contains its own DNA and ribosomes — semi-autonomous
  • Green pigment: Chlorophyll (absorbs sunlight for photosynthesis)

NCERT Exam Point
Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) lack membrane-bound organelles: no nucleus, no mitochondria, no ER, no Golgi. They have 70S ribosomes and nucleoid (circular DNA). Eukaryotic cells have all membrane-bound organelles with 80S ribosomes.

6. Cell Division

Two main types: Mitosis (somatic cells) and Meiosis (germ cells). Interphase is the preparatory phase before both.

Cell Cycle
  • Interphase (90% of cell cycle): G1 phase (cell growth), S phase (DNA replication), G2 phase (preparation for division)
  • M phase (Mitosis/Meiosis): actual division
  • Cytokinesis: division of cytoplasm
Mitosis — Equational Division
  • Occurs in somatic (body) cells
  • Divides: 1 parent cell → 2 identical daughter cells
  • Chromosome number: remains same (2n → 2n)
  • Phases: Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase
  • PMAT — Mnemonic: "People Meet At Tea"
  • Importance: Growth, repair, regeneration, asexual reproduction
Meiosis — Reductional Division
  • Occurs in germ cells (gametes)
  • Divides: 1 parent cell → 4 non-identical daughter cells
  • Chromosome number: halved (2n → n)
  • Two stages: Meiosis I (reductional) + Meiosis II (equational)
  • Crossing over occurs in Prophase I — exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes → genetic variation
  • Importance: Sexual reproduction, genetic diversity, evolution

Cell typeSomatic cellsGerm cells
Daughter cells2 identical4 non-identical
Chromosome #Same (2n)Half (n)

Key Distinction
Mitosis = Growth & Repair | Meiosis = Gamete (sperm/egg) formation. If mitosis goes wrong → Cancer. If meiosis goes wrong → Down syndrome, Turner syndrome, Klinefelter syndrome.

7. Introduction to Tissues

A tissue is a group of similar cells performing a specific function. The study of tissues is called Histology.

  • Meristematic tissue — dividing cells (plants) — at root/shoot tips
  • Permanent tissue — differentiated, non-dividing

Animal Tissues — 4 main types:

  • Epithelial Tissue — covering, lining, protection
  • Connective Tissue — support, connection, transport
  • Muscular Tissue — movement & locomotion
  • Nervous Tissue — conduction of nerve impulses
Mnemonic: "ECMN — Every Cell Makes New"
Epithelial — Connective — Muscular — Nervous → "Every Cell Must Nourish"

8. Epithelial Tissue

Epithelial tissue covers body surfaces, lines cavities, and forms glands. It is avascular (no blood vessels) — gets nutrients by diffusion from underlying connective tissue.

Simple Epithelium (single layer)
  • Squamous — flat cells; in lung alveoli, blood vessels (endothelium); function: diffusion, filtration
  • Cuboidal — cube-shaped; in kidney tubules, glands; function: secretion, absorption
  • Columnar — tall column-like; in stomach, intestine; function: secretion, absorption
  • Ciliated columnar — with hair-like cilia; in respiratory tract, fallopian tubes; function: movement of mucus/egg
Compound Epithelium (multiple layers — stratified)
  • Stratified squamous — in skin (epidermis), mouth, esophagus; provides protection against wear & tear
  • Transitional epithelium — in urinary bladder, ureters; stretches to accommodate urine
Glandular Epithelium
  • Glands formed from epithelial tissue
  • Exocrine glands — with ducts (e.g., sweat glands, salivary glands, liver)
  • Endocrine glands — ductless, secrete hormones into blood (e.g., pituitary, thyroid)

9. Connective Tissue

Most abundant and widely distributed tissue. Characterized by cells scattered in an extracellular matrix (ECM — ground substance + fibers).

Loose Connective Tissue
  • Areolar tissue — most widely distributed; contains fibroblasts, collagen & elastic fibers; fills spaces, supports organs
  • Adipose tissue — stores fat (adipocytes); under skin, around kidneys; insulation, energy storage, cushioning
Dense Connective Tissue
  • Tendons — connect muscle to bone; strong, inelastic (collagen fibers)
  • Ligaments — connect bone to bone; elastic (elastic fibers)
Specialized Connective Tissues
Cartilage
  • Cells: Chondrocytes (in lacunae); matrix: chondrin
  • Avascular — no blood supply
  • Types: Hyaline cartilage (nose, trachea, joints — most common), Fibrous cartilage (intervertebral discs), Elastic cartilage (ear pinna)
Bone
  • Cells: Osteocytes (in lacunae); matrix: ossein (collagen) + calcium phosphate (hardness)
  • Vascular — has blood supply
  • Functions: Support, protection (skull, ribs), movement (levers), mineral storage (Ca, P), blood cell formation (bone marrow)
  • Compact bone → Haversian system (osteon)
Blood — Fluid Connective Tissue
  • Matrix: Plasma (55%) = water (92%) + proteins (7%: albumin, globulin, fibrinogen) + nutrients, hormones, wastes
  • Cells (45%): RBCs (erythrocytes) — 4.5-5.5 million/mm³; WBCs (leukocytes) — 4000-11000/mm³; Platelets (thrombocytes) — 1.5-4 lakh/mm³
  • RBC absence of nucleus — more space for hemoglobin (Hb: 12-16 g/dL in males, 11-14 g/dL in females)
  • Phagocytic WBCs: Neutrophils (60-65%), Monocytes (6-8%) → first line defense
  • Lymphocytes (20-25%) → B-cells (antibodies) & T-cells (cellular immunity)
Blood Disorders
Anemia — low Hb/RBCs | Leukemia — blood cancer (↑ WBCs) | Thrombocytopenia — low platelets | Hemophilia — genetic, blood doesn't clot
Lymph — A Fluid Connective Tissue
  • Similar to plasma but less proteins; contains lymphocytes
  • Carries: absorbed fats (lacteals in intestine), drains excess tissue fluid back to blood
  • Lymph nodes — filter lymph; site of immune response

11. Nervous Tissue

Specialized for conducting nerve impulses. The structural & functional unit is the neuron (nerve cell).

Neuron Structure
  • Cell body (Soma/Cytom) — contains nucleus, Nissl granules (RER), neurofibrils
  • Dendrites — short, branched; receive impulses from other neurons
  • Axon — long, single; carries impulses away from cell body
  • Myelin sheath — Schwann cells (PNS) / Oligodendrocytes (CNS); gaps = Nodes of Ranvier
  • Synapse — junction between two neurons; neurotransmitters (acetylcholine, dopamine) transfer impulse
Types of Neurons
  • Sensory (afferent) neurons — carry impulses from receptors → CNS
  • Motor (efferent) neurons — carry impulses from CNS → effectors (muscles/glands)
  • Interneurons (association) — connect sensory & motor neurons within CNS
Neuroglia (Glial Cells)
  • Non-neuronal supporting cells in CNS — 10x more abundant than neurons
  • Types: Astrocytes (support, blood-brain barrier), Microglia (immune defense), Oligodendrocytes (myelin in CNS), Ependymal cells (line ventricles)
  • In PNS: Schwann cells (myelin), Satellite cells (support)

12. Practice MCQs

1. Who discovered the cell?
A) Leeuwenhoek
B) Robert Hooke
C) Robert Brown
D) Rudolf Virchow
Answer: B) Robert Hooke (1665)
2. Which organelle is called the "powerhouse of the cell"?
A) Golgi apparatus
B) Ribosome
C) Mitochondria
D) Lysosome
Answer: C) Mitochondria — site of ATP synthesis
3. Which tissue connects muscle to bone?
A) Ligament
B) Tendon
C) Cartilage
D) Areolar
Answer: B) Tendon (muscle→bone); Ligament = bone→bone
4. Which of the following is NOT found in animal cells?
A) Centriole
B) Cell wall
C) Lysosome
D) Mitochondria
Answer: B) Cell wall — present only in plants
5. "Omnis cellula e cellula" was given by:
A) Schleiden
B) Schwann
C) Virchow
D) Hooke
Answer: C) Rudolf Virchow — all cells arise from pre-existing cells
6. Which blood cells help in blood clotting?
A) RBCs
B) WBCs
C) Platelets
D) Plasma
Answer: C) Platelets (thrombocytes) — normal count: 1.5-4 lakh/mm³
7. Cardiac muscle is characterized by:
A) Voluntary control
B) Intercalated discs
C) Spindle-shaped cells
D) Single nucleus per cell
Answer: B) Intercalated discs — unique to cardiac muscle for rapid impulse transmission
8. During mitosis, chromosome number in daughter cells:
A) Doubles
B) Remains same
C) Halves
D) Becomes zero
Answer: B) Remains same (2n→2n) — equational division
9. Which epithelial tissue lines the respiratory tract?
A) Squamous
B) Cuboidal
C) Ciliated columnar
D) Stratified squamous
Answer: C) Ciliated columnar — cilia move mucus upward
10. Which organelle contains digestive enzymes?
A) Peroxisome
B) Lysosome
C) Vacuole
D) Golgi body
Answer: B) Lysosome — contains hydrolytic enzymes, called "suicide bags"
