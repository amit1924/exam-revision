1. Types of Nutrition

Nutrition is the process of obtaining and utilizing food for growth, repair, and energy. Types:

  • Autotrophic nutrition — organisms synthesize their own food (photosynthesis). Eg: plants, algae, cyanobacteria
  • Heterotrophic nutrition — organisms obtain food from others. Subtypes:
    • Holozoic — ingest solid food (humans, animals) — ingestion → digestion → absorption → assimilation → egestion
    • Saprotrophic — feed on dead/decaying matter (fungi, bacteria)
    • Parasitic — live on/in host, derive nutrition (tapeworm, roundworm, lice)
Digestive System Steps
Ingestion → Digestion → Absorption → Assimilation → Egestion. Mnemonic: "I D A A E — I Did A Awesome Exam"

2. Food Components (Nutrients)

Our food contains 6 major nutrients: Carbohydrates, Proteins, Fats, Vitamins, Minerals, Water. Plus Roughage (Fiber).

A. Carbohydrates — Energy Providers
  • Elements: C, H, O (ratio H:O = 2:1 — like water, hence "carbo-hydrates")
  • Types: Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose — single sugar), Disaccharides (sucrose, maltose, lactose — two sugars), Polysaccharides (starch, cellulose, glycogen — many sugars)
  • Energy: 4 kcal/g — primary energy source
  • Sources: Rice, wheat, potato, sugar, fruits, honey
  • Storage: Glycogen (in liver & muscles — animals), Starch (in plants)
  • Cellulose: Roughage/fiber — humans cannot digest (lack cellulase enzyme); helps in bowel movement
B. Proteins — Body Builders
  • Elements: C, H, O, N (+ S in some)
  • Made of amino acids (20 types: 9 essential — must get from food, 11 non-essential — body can synthesize)
  • Energy: 4 kcal/g
  • Sources: Milk, eggs, meat, fish, pulses, soyabean, nuts
  • Functions: Growth, repair, enzymes, hormones, antibodies, structure (collagen)
  • Kwashiorkor & Marasmus — protein deficiency diseases (see diseases section)
C. Fats — Energy Reserves
  • Elements: C, H, O (less O than carbs)
  • Energy: 9 kcal/g — highest energy per gram
  • Types: Saturated fats (solid at RT — butter, ghee), Unsaturated fats (liquid — oils)
  • Sources: Butter, ghee, oil, meat, egg yolk, cheese, nuts
  • Functions: Energy reserve, insulation, protection of organs, vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K)
D. Water
  • Body composition: 60-70% water
  • Functions: Universal solvent, transport, temperature regulation, lubrication, metabolic reactions
  • Daily requirement: ~2-2.5 L (8 glasses)
E. Roughage (Dietary Fiber)
  • Cannot be digested — no enzymes for cellulose
  • Functions: Adds bulk, prevents constipation, absorbs toxins, reduces cholesterol
  • Sources: Whole grains, vegetables, fruits, nuts

Think: Fat is 9 (more than double of Carbs/Proteins). Fat keeps you full longer!

5. Human Digestive System — Overview

The human digestive system consists of the alimentary canal (digestive tract) and digestive glands.

Alimentary Canal (Length: ~9 meters)
  • Mouth (Oral Cavity) → Pharynx → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine → Large Intestine → Rectum → Anus
Digestive Glands
  • Salivary Glands (3 pairs: parotid, submandibular, sublingual)
  • Gastric Glands (in stomach wall)
  • Liver (largest gland — produces bile) + Gall Bladder (stores bile)
  • Pancreas (mixed gland — exocrine + endocrine)
  • Intestinal Glands (in intestinal wall)
Length Facts
Total alimentary canal length: ~9 m (30 ft). Small intestine: ~6-7 m (largest part). Large intestine: ~1.5 m. Oesophagus: ~25 cm. Stomach capacity: ~1-2 L

6. Mouth, Teeth & Salivary Digestion

Buccal Cavity (Mouth)
  • Bounded by lips (front), cheeks (sides), palate (roof), tongue (floor)
  • Palate: hard palate (front — bony) + soft palate (back — muscular) with uvula
Teeth — Types & Structure

An adult human has 32 teeth (permanent). Children have 20 milk teeth (deciduous) which fall between ages 6-12.

Tooth TypeNumberFunction
Incisors4 × 2 = 8Cutting/Biting
Canines2 × 2 = 4Tearing/Piercing
Premolars4 × 2 = 8Chewing/Grinding
Molars6 × 2 = 12Grinding (main chewing)

Dental formula (one half of jaw): 2123 (2 incisors, 1 canine, 2 premolars, 3 molars). Total: 2123 × 2 × 2 = 32. Milk teeth formula: 2102

Tooth Structure
  • Crown (visible part — covered by enamel — hardest substance in body)
  • Neck (between crown & root; surrounded by gums/gingiva)
  • Root (embedded in jawbone — socket)
  • Inner: Dentine (hard, bonelike) → Pulp cavity (contains nerves, blood vessels)
Dental Diseases
Dental caries (tooth decay) — caused by bacteria (Streptococcus mutans) that produce acid from sugar, damaging enamel. Gingivitis — gum inflammation. Periodontitis — advanced gum disease.
Tongue
  • Muscular organ attached to floor by frenulum
  • Covered with papillae (bumps) containing taste buds (~10,000)
  • Taste areas: Sweet (tip), Salty (front sides), Sour (middle sides), Bitter (back), Umami (savory — all over)
  • Functions: Taste, mixing food (bolus formation), swallowing, speech
Salivary Glands & Saliva
  • 3 pairs of salivary glands: Parotid (largest — near ear), Submandibular (under jaw), Sublingual (under tongue)
  • Saliva produced daily: 1-1.5 L
  • pH: 6.8-7.0 (slightly acidic to neutral)
  • Composition: 99% water + Salivary amylase (ptyalin) + Lysozyme (antibacterial) + Mucus + electrolytes
  • Function of saliva: Moistens food, begins starch digestion (amylase → maltose), cleanses mouth, antibacterial (lysozyme), taste facilitation
Dental Formula Mnemonic: "I Can Put Molars in — 2123"
2 Incisors, 1 Canine, 2 Premolars, 3 Molars = "2123" (half jaw; multiply by 4 for total = 32)

7. Stomach — Digestion & Churning

  • J-shaped muscular bag located in upper left abdomen (below diaphragm)
  • Capacity: 1-2 L
  • Parts: Cardia (upper, near oesophagus) → Fundus (dome-shaped) → Body (main) → Pylorus (lower, leads to duodenum)
  • Oesophageal sphincter (gastro-oesophageal) — prevents backflow of acid → heartburn/acid reflux
  • Pyloric sphincter — regulates release of chyme into duodenum
Gastric Glands & Secretions
  • Gastric juice: ~2-3 L/day, pH 1-3 (highly acidic)
  • Cells:
    • Chief cells — secrete Pepsinogen (inactive form → activated by HCl to Pepsin)
    • Parietal cells — secrete HCl (kills bacteria, activates pepsinogen) & Intrinsic factor (B12 absorption)
    • Mucus neck cells — secrete Mucus (protects stomach lining from acid)
    • G cells — secrete Gastrin (hormone that stimulates HCl secretion)
  • Digestion in stomach: Proteins → Peptides (pepsin), partial fat digestion (lingual lipase continues), no carb digestion (amylase inactivated by acid)
  • Food stays in stomach: 2-4 hours. Semi-digested food = Chyme
Stomach Disorders
Acidity/GERD — acid reflux into oesophagus | Gastritis — inflammation of stomach lining | Peptic ulcer — caused by H. pylori bacteria or excessive NSAIDs | Indigestion (Dyspepsia)

8. Small Intestine — Main Absorption Site

  • Length: ~6-7 m (longest part of alimentary canal)
  • Parts: Duodenum (25 cm — C-shaped), Jejunum (~2.5 m), Ileum (~3.5 m)
  • Duodenum receives bile (from liver) and pancreatic juice (from pancreas) via hepatopancreatic ampulla (opening controlled by Sphincter of Oddi)
Wall Structure — Villi & Microvilli
  • Inner wall has finger-like projections called Villi (increase surface area 30x)
  • Each villus cell has Microvilli (brush border) — further increase area 600x
  • Total surface area: ~200-300 m² (size of a tennis court!)
  • Each villus contains: Blood capillaries (absorb glucose, amino acids, water-soluble vitamins) and Lacteal (lymph vessel — absorbs fatty acids & glycerol)
Intestinal Glands (Crypts of Lieberkuhn)
  • Secrete Intestinal juice (Succus Entericus): ~1.5 L/day, pH 7.8-8.0 (alkaline)
  • Contains enzymes: Peptidases, Disaccharidases (maltase, sucrase, lactase), Nucleotidases, Lipases
  • Lactose intolerance — deficiency of lactase enzyme → unable to digest milk sugar (lactose)
Absorption Sites
Duodenum — Iron, Calcium | Jejunum — Glucose, amino acids, fat-soluble vitamins | Ileum — Vitamin B12, bile salts, remaining nutrients

9. Large Intestine — Water Absorption

  • Length: ~1.5 m; diameter: ~6-7 cm (wider than small intestine)
  • Parts: Caecum (with Appendix — vestigial organ in humans), Colon (ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid), Rectum, Anal canal
  • Functions: Absorption of water & electrolytes (from chyme → semi-solid feces), bacterial synthesis (vitamin K, B12), storage of feces
  • Gut bacteria (Gut microbiome): ~100 trillion bacteria in colon — essential for health
  • Defecation: Egestion of undigested food (feces) via anus (controlled by anal sphincters)
Intestinal Disorders
Appendicitis — inflammation of appendix | Constipation — insufficient fiber/water | Diarrhoea — frequent loose stools (dehydration risk) | Dysentery — diarrhoea with blood & mucus | Colon cancer

10. Digestive Glands — Liver & Pancreas

Liver — The Largest Gland
  • Largest gland in human body: ~1.2-1.5 kg
  • Located in upper right abdomen, protected by rib cage
  • Secretes Bile (600-1000 mL/day) — stored in Gall Bladder
  • Bile composition: Water + Bile salts (emulsify fats) + Bile pigments (bilirubin, biliverdin — from Hb breakdown) + Cholesterol + Electrolytes
  • Bile is alkaline (pH 7.8-8.6) — neutralizes acid chyme from stomach
  • Bile function: Emulsification of fats (physical breakdown) — no enzymes in bile
  • Other liver functions:
    • Metabolism of carbohydrates (glycogen storage, gluconeogenesis)
    • Protein synthesis (albumin, clotting factors, fibrinogen)
    • Detoxification of drugs, alcohol, toxins
    • Storage of vitamins (A, D, E, K, B12), iron, glycogen
    • Kupffer cells — phagocytic immune cells
Liver Disorders
Jaundice — buildup of bilirubin (yellowing of skin/eyes) | Cirrhosis — liver scarring (alcohol, hepatitis) | Hepatitis — liver inflammation (A, B, C, D, E viruses) | Gallstones — hardened cholesterol deposits in gall bladder
Pancreas — Mixed Gland
  • Located behind stomach (retroperitoneal)
  • Exocrine part (acini cells) — secretes Pancreatic juice (1-1.5 L/day, pH 7.8-8.4) into duodenum
  • Endocrine part (Islets of Langerhans) — secretes Insulin (beta cells ↓ blood glucose) & Glucagon (alpha cells ↑ blood glucose)

11. Digestion Process — Complete Summary

OrganSecretion/EnzymeSubstrate DigestedProduct Formed
MouthSalivary amylase (ptyalin)StarchMaltose
StomachHCl + PepsinProteinsPeptides
DuodenumPancreatic juice + BileCarbs, proteins, fatsMaltose, peptides, FA + glycerol
Jejunum/IleumIntestinal juiceMaltose, peptides, etc.Glucose, amino acids
Carbohydrate Digestion
  • Mouth: Salivary amylase → starch → maltose
  • Small Intestine: Pancreatic amylase → remaining starch → maltose
  • Intestinal wall: Maltase → maltose → glucose; Sucrase → sucrose → glucose + fructose; Lactase → lactose → glucose + galactose
  • Final product: Glucose (absorbed into blood)
Protein Digestion
  • Stomach: Pepsin (activated by HCl) → proteins → peptides
  • Duodenum: Trypsin (activated by enterokinase) + Chymotrypsin → proteins → peptides
  • Intestinal wall: Peptidases (aminopeptidase, dipeptidase) → peptides → Amino acids
Fat Digestion
  • Duodenum: Bile salts (from liver) emulsify large fat globules into tiny droplets (↑ surface area)
  • Duodenum: Pancreatic lipase → emulsified fats → Fatty acids + Glycerol
  • Bile also activates lipase
  • Absorption: Fatty acids & glycerol enter lacteals (lymph vessels in villi) → transported via lymphatic system
NCERT Connect — Class 10: Life Processes
Digestion in humans is covered in Class 10 Life Processes chapter. Key takeaway: All carbohydrates → glucose, proteins → amino acids, fats → fatty acid + glycerol. These end products are absorbed and used for energy, growth, and repair.

13. Practice MCQs

1. Which enzyme digests starch in the mouth?
A) Pepsin
B) Trypsin
C) Salivary amylase
D) Lipase
Answer: C) Salivary amylase (ptyalin) — starch → maltose
2. Which vitamin helps in blood clotting?
A) Vitamin A
B) Vitamin C
C) Vitamin K
D) Vitamin E
Answer: C) Vitamin K (Phylloquinone) — helps in prothrombin formation
3. The largest gland in the human body is:
A) Pancreas
B) Salivary gland
C) Liver
D) Thyroid
Answer: C) Liver (1.2-1.5 kg) — produces bile
4. Bile is stored in:
A) Liver
B) Pancreas
C) Gall bladder
D) Duodenum
Answer: C) Gall bladder — stores & concentrates bile
5. Kwashiorkor is caused by deficiency of:
A) Carbohydrates
B) Proteins
C) Fats
D) Vitamins
Answer: B) Protein deficiency (with adequate calories) — swollen belly due to edema
6. Where is most water absorbed from digested food?
A) Stomach
B) Small intestine
C) Large intestine
D) Mouth
Answer: C) Large intestine (colon) — absorbs water & electrolytes
7. Which hormone regulates blood sugar level?
A) Gastrin
B) Insulin
C) Thyroxine
D) Adrenaline
Answer: B) Insulin — secreted by beta cells of pancreas (Islets of Langerhans)
8. The finger-like projections in the small intestine are called:
A) Villi
B) Alveoli
C) Nephron
D) Neurons
Answer: A) Villi — increase surface area for absorption (200-300 m²)
9. Total number of permanent teeth in humans is:
A) 20
B) 28
C) 32
D) 36
Answer: C) 32 teeth (dental formula: 2123 × 4)
10. Which gland is both exocrine and endocrine?
A) Liver
B) Pancreas
C) Thyroid
D) Salivary gland
Answer: B) Pancreas — exocrine (pancreatic juice) + endocrine (insulin, glucagon)
11. Pepsinogen is activated to pepsin by:
A) Bile
B) Enterokinase
C) HCl
D) Trypsin
Answer: C) HCl (hydrochloric acid) in stomach activates pepsinogen → pepsin
12. Which nutrient provides the highest energy per gram?
A) Carbohydrates
B) Proteins
C) Fats
D) Vitamins
Answer: C) Fats — 9 kcal/g (Carbs & Proteins = 4 kcal/g)
