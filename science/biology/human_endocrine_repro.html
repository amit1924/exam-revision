1. Endocrine Glands — Overview

Endocrine glands are ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the blood. Study of endocrine system: Endocrinology. Hormones are chemical messengers that travel through blood to target organs and regulate body functions.

Endocrine vs Exocrine Glands
FeatureEndocrine GlandsExocrine Glands
DuctsDuctless (secrete directly into blood)Have ducts (secrete onto surface or into cavity)
SecretionHormones (chemical messengers)Enzymes, sweat, saliva, mucus, sebum, milk, etc.
TransportVia bloodstream to distant target organsVia ducts to specific location
ExamplesPituitary, Thyroid, Adrenal, Pancreas (islets), Ovaries, TestesSalivary glands, Sweat glands, Liver, Mammary glands, Sebaceous glands, Lachrymal glands
Speed of responseSlow but prolonged effectVaries (enzymes act quickly)
Pancreas — A Mixed Gland
The pancreas is both endocrine (islets of Langerhans → insulin & glucagon into blood) and exocrine (acinar cells → digestive enzymes via duct to duodenum). This makes it a heterocrine (mixed) gland.

Key Hormones in Detail (NCERT Class 10 — Control & Coordination)
  • Adrenaline (Epinephrine) — "Emergency/Fight or Flight hormone". Secreted by adrenal medulla in response to stress/fear/excitement. Effects: ↑ Heart rate & BP, dilates airways (bronchodilation), ↑ blood glucose (glycogen → glucose), dilates pupils, diverts blood to muscles. Prepares body for intense physical action
  • Insulin — Secreted by β-cells of pancreas. ↓ Blood glucose (promotes glucose uptake by cells, glycogenesis in liver). Deficiency → Diabetes Mellitus (high blood sugar, glucosuria, polyuria, polydipsia)
  • Glucagon — Secreted by α-cells of pancreas. ↑ Blood glucose (stimulates glycogenolysis — glycogen → glucose in liver)
  • Thyroxine (T4) — Regulates metabolism of carbs, fats, proteins. Iodine is essential for synthesis (iodine deficiency → goiter). T3 is 3-5x more potent than T4
  • Growth Hormone (GH) — Stimulates growth of bones and tissues (protein synthesis, cell division). Dwarfism (↓ in childhood), Gigantism (↑ in childhood), Acromegaly (↑ in adults — enlarged hands, feet, face)
  • Estrogen — Main female hormone; develops breasts, uterine lining growth, menstrual cycle regulation, secondary sexual characteristics
  • Progesterone — Maintains pregnancy; prepares uterus for implantation; inhibits contractions during pregnancy
  • Testosterone — Main male hormone; sperm production, muscle/bone growth, voice deepening, facial/body hair, libido
  • Oxytocin — "Love/Birth hormone". Uterine contractions during childbirth, milk ejection (let-down reflex), social bonding, trust
  • ADH (Vasopressin) — Increases water reabsorption in kidneys (↓ urine output). Deficiency → Diabetes Insipidus (excessive urination, thirst)

3. Feedback Mechanism of Hormones (NCERT Class 10)

The secretion of hormones is regulated by feedback mechanisms — the body maintains homeostasis by controlling hormone levels. This is a key concept from NCERT Class 10 (Control & Coordination).

Negative Feedback (Most Common)
  • An increase in hormone level triggers a response that DECREASES its production → maintains balance
  • Example 1: Thyroid — Low T3/T4 → Hypothalamus releases TRH → Pituitary releases TSH → Thyroid releases T3/T4 → When T3/T4 levels rise → inhibits TRH & TSH (negative feedback)
  • Example 2: Blood Glucose — High blood glucose → Pancreas releases insulin → Cells absorb glucose → Blood glucose drops → Insulin secretion stops
  • Example 3: Calcium — Low blood Ca²⁺ → Parathyroid releases PTH → ↑ Ca²⁺ from bones, kidneys, gut → Blood Ca²⁺ rises → PTH secretion stops
Positive Feedback (Amplifies change)
  • An increase triggers MORE production → creates a cycle that amplifies the change
  • Example 1: Childbirth — Baby's head stretches cervix → Oxytocin released → Uterine contractions → Baby pushes more → More oxytocin released (cycle continues until birth)
  • Example 2: Ovulation (LH surge) — Rising estrogen → Pituitary releases more LH → LH surge triggers ovulation
  • Example 3: Blood clotting — Initial clotting factors activate more clotting factors (cascade)
NCERT Exam Tip
Negative feedback = most hormone regulation (like a thermostat) | Positive feedback = rare, used for childbirth & ovulation. Exam question: "Which feedback mechanism controls TSH secretion?" Answer: Negative feedback.

5. Human Reproductive System

The reproductive system produces offspring through the union of male (sperm) and female (egg) gametes. It consists of primary sex organs (gonads) and accessory reproductive structures.

Male Reproductive System (NCERT Class 8 & 10)
  • Primary sex organ: Testes (a pair) — located in scrotum (outside body, 2-3°C lower temp needed for spermatogenesis)
  • Functions: (1) Spermatogenesis (sperm production, ~1000 sperm/sec), (2) Testosterone secretion
  • Internal structure: Each testis has ~250 lobules containing seminiferous tubules (site of sperm production) → Leydig cells (interstitial, produce testosterone) + Sertoli cells (nourish developing sperm)
  • Duct system: Epididymis (storage & maturation of sperm, ~20 days) → Vas deferens (carries sperm during ejaculation) → Ejaculatory duct → Urethra (common passage for semen & urine)
  • Accessory glands: Seminal vesicles (2) — secrete fructose-rich fluid (energy for sperm), prostaglandins | Prostate gland — secretes alkaline fluid (neutralizes vaginal acidity) | Bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands (2) — secrete pre-ejaculate lubricant
  • Semen = Sperm + Seminal fluid + Prostate fluid (total ~3-5 ml per ejaculation, containing 200-500 million sperm)
  • Penis — copulatory organ; contains erectile tissue (corpus cavernosum & corpus spongiosum) that fills with blood during erection
Female Reproductive System (NCERT Class 8 & 10)
  • Primary sex organ: Ovaries (a pair) — in pelvic cavity; size ~3 cm
  • Functions: (1) Oogenesis (egg/ovum production, 1 per month ~400 in lifetime), (2) Secretion of estrogen & progesterone
  • Duct system: Fallopian tubes (Oviducts) — ~10-12 cm long; site of fertilization (ampulla region); lined with cilia to move egg | Uterus (womb) — pear-shaped, ~7.5 cm; site of fetal development | Cervix — lower narrow part of uterus | Vagina — birth canal, ~8-10 cm; also receives penis during intercourse
  • Fimbriae — finger-like projections at infundibulum of fallopian tube that sweep the released egg
  • Uterus wall: Endometrium (inner lining — sheds during menstruation, site of implantation) → Myometrium (smooth muscle layer — contracts during childbirth) → Perimetrium (outer covering)
  • Vulva (external genitalia) — Mons pubis, Labia majora/minora, Clitoris (erectile, homologous to penis), Vestibular glands (Bartholin's — lubricate vagina)

6. Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a monthly series of changes in the female reproductive system to prepare for pregnancy. Controlled by hormones (FSH, LH, estrogen, progesterone).

Phases of Menstrual Cycle (~28 days)
PhaseDaysOvarian EventsUterine EventsHormonal Changes
Menstrual1-5No dominant activityEndometrium breaks down & sheds → menstrual bleeding (30-80 ml blood lost)Low estrogen & progesterone (both drop at end of previous cycle)
Follicular (Proliferative)6-13FSH stimulates follicle growth; one follicle becomes dominant; egg matures insideEstrogen from follicle → endometrium thickens (proliferates) — blood vessels & glands developFSH ↑ (stimulates follicle), Estrogen ↑ ↑ (from growing follicle)
Ovulation~14LH surge → mature follicle bursts → egg released (lives 12-24 hrs)Endometrium continues thickeningLH surge (peak), FSH peak, Estrogen peak → triggers LH surge
Luteal (Secretory)15-28Empty follicle → Corpus luteum (yellow body); if no pregnancy → degenerates into Corpus albicansProgesterone from corpus luteum → endometrium secretes nutrients (glycogen, lipids) — ready for implantationProgesterone ↑ ↑, Estrogen moderate; FSH & LH ↓ (negative feedback)
  • Ovulation — Most fertile period (Day ~14 in 28-day cycle); egg survives 12-24 hrs; sperm survives 3-5 days in female tract
  • If fertilization occurs: Corpus luteum persists (maintained by hCG from embryo) → continues secreting progesterone to maintain pregnancy
  • If no fertilization: Corpus luteum degenerates → progesterone & estrogen drop → endometrium sheds → new cycle begins
  • Dysmenorrhea — painful periods (cramps due to prostaglandins) | Amenorrhea — absence of menstruation (pregnancy, stress, hormonal imbalance, excessive exercise) | Menorrhagia — heavy/prolonged bleeding
Key Terms (NCERT Class 10)
Menarche — first menstruation (age 10-16) | Menopause — permanent cessation of menstruation (age 45-50) | Ovum — largest human cell (~150 µm) | Sperm — smallest human cell (~50 µm) | Fertile period — 6 days ending on day of ovulation (Day 10-16 approx)

7. Fertilization, Pregnancy & Childbirth

Fertilization (NCERT Class 8 & 10)
  • Fertilization — fusion of sperm (n=23) & egg (n=23) → Zygote (2n=46). Occurs in ampulla of fallopian tube
  • Only 1 sperm fertilizes the egg (polyspermy blocked by cortical reaction → zona pellucida hardens)
  • Cleavage (Cell division) — Zygote divides mitotically → Morula (16 cells, 3-4 days) → Blastocyst (hollow ball with inner cell mass) → Implantation into endometrium (Day 6-7 after fertilization)
  • Ectopic pregnancy — embryo implants outside uterus (often in fallopian tube) — medical emergency
Pregnancy (Gestation)
  • Gestation period: ~280 days (40 weeks/9 months) — counted from last menstrual period (LMP)
  • Trimesters: First (Week 1-12) — organ formation (most vulnerable); Second (13-27) — growth & movement felt; Third (28-40) — rapid growth, baby positions for birth
  • Placenta — Disc-shaped organ connecting mother & fetus through umbilical cord. Functions: (1) O₂ & nutrients to fetus, (2) CO₂ & wastes from fetus, (3) Secretes hormones: hCG, hPL, estrogen, progesterone, (4) Barrier against some pathogens (not all — e.g., Rubella, HIV cross placenta)
  • Amniotic sac — fluid-filled (amniotic fluid) cushions fetus, allows movement, maintains temperature
Childbirth (Parturition) & Lactation
  • Parturition — childbirth; triggered by Oxytocin (from posterior pituitary) → uterine contractions (positive feedback)
  • Stages: (1) Dilation — cervix opens (up to 10 cm), (2) Expulsion — baby delivered through vagina, (3) Placental stage — placenta expelled (afterbirth)
  • Lactation — Prolactin stimulates milk production in mammary glands + Oxytocin causes milk ejection (let-down reflex)
  • Colostrum — first milk (yellowish, rich in antibodies — especially IgA); important for newborn's immunity
hCG — Pregnancy Hormone
Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG) — secreted by placenta (by syncytiotrophoblast). Maintains corpus luteum (continues progesterone). Detected in pregnancy tests (urine/blood) from ~Day 10 after conception. Peaks at Week 10, then declines.

8. Adolescence & Reaching Puberty

Complete coverage from NCERT Class 8 Chapter 10: Reaching the Age of Adolescence

What is Adolescence?
  • Adolescence — transitional period from childhood to adulthood. WHO definition: 10-19 years.
  • Puberty — phase when the body undergoes sexual maturation and becomes capable of reproduction
  • Age of puberty: Girls: 10-14 years (typically starts ~2 years earlier than boys) | Boys: 12-16 years
  • Puberty onset triggered by hypothalamus → GnRH → FSH/LH from pituitary → gonads produce sex hormones

Secondary Sexual Characteristics
  • Secondary sexual characteristics — features that distinguish males & females but are NOT directly involved in reproduction
  • Boys: Broad shoulders, narrow hips, facial/chest hair, prominent larynx (Adam's apple), deep voice, muscular build
  • Girls: Developed breasts, wider hips, less body hair, higher-pitched voice, more subcutaneous fat (curvy shape)
  • Primary sexual characteristics: Presence of gonads (testes/ovaries) and reproductive organs at birth
Changes at Puberty Mnemonic: "Hair, Height, Hormones, Hips, Voice, Skin, Sweat, Sex Organs"
8 key changes: Hair growth | Height spurt | Hormonal changes | Hip widening (♀) | Voice change | Skin (acne) | Sweat & body odor | Sex organ maturation
Nutritional Needs During Adolescence
  • Increased need for: Protein (body growth), Calcium (bone growth), Iron (blood volume & menstruation in girls), Iodine (thyroid function)
  • Girls need more iron due to menstrual blood loss (iron-rich foods: spinach, jaggery, dates, leafy greens, meat)
  • Both need more calcium for bone density (milk, curd, cheese, ragi, leafy greens)
  • A balanced diet with all nutrients is essential; avoid junk food (empty calories)
Personal Hygiene During Adolescence
  • Regular bathing — sweat glands active, body odor; use mild soap, wear clean clothes
  • Dental care — brush twice daily; avoid excess sugar; visit dentist regularly
  • Menstrual hygiene (girls) — Use sanitary pads/tampons/menstrual cups; change every 4-6 hrs; wash hands; dispose properly; maintain cleanliness
  • Skin care — Wash face regularly; avoid touching/picking acne; use non-comedogenic products; consult dermatologist for severe acne
  • Emotional health — Talk to parents/teachers/counselors about changes; don't hesitate to ask questions

9. Reproductive Health & Contraception

Sexually Transmitted Diseases (STDs)
  • Gonorrhea (bacterial — Neisseria gonorrhoeae; causes painful urination, discharge)
  • Syphilis (bacterial — Treponema pallidum; stages: chancre → rash → organ damage)
  • HIV/AIDS (viral — Human Immunodeficiency Virus; weakens immune system; transmitted via unprotected sex, blood, needle-sharing, mother-to-child)
  • HPV (Genital warts) (viral — Human Papillomavirus; some strains cause cervical cancer)
  • Chlamydia (bacterial — often asymptomatic; can cause infertility)
  • Herpes genitalis (viral — HSV-2; painful blisters; recurrent outbreaks)
  • Prevention: Use condoms, mutual monogamy, vaccination (HPV, HBV), regular testing, avoid sharing needles

Reproductive Health Issues
PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) — hormonal disorder; enlarged ovaries with small cysts; irregular periods, acne, weight gain, hirsutism (excess hair), infertility. Treatment: lifestyle changes, metformin, hormonal pills.
Endometriosis — uterine tissue grows outside uterus → severe pelvic pain, infertility.
Infertility — inability to conceive after 1 year of unprotected sex. Causes: male factors (low sperm count/motility), female factors (ovulation disorders, blocked tubes), or unexplained. Treatments: IVF (In Vitro Fertilization — egg & sperm combined in lab), ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection), IUI (Intrauterine Insemination), Surrogacy.

10. Practice MCQs

1. Which gland is called the "master gland" of the endocrine system?
A) Thyroid
B) Adrenal
C) Pituitary
D) Hypothalamus
Answer: C) Pituitary gland — controls other endocrine glands via tropic hormones
2. The hormone that regulates blood sugar level is:
A) Thyroxine
B) Insulin
C) Adrenaline
D) Estrogen
Answer: B) Insulin — secreted by beta cells of pancreas; lowers blood glucose
3. The site of fertilization in humans is:
A) Uterus
B) Fallopian tube (ampulla)
C) Ovary
D) Vagina
Answer: B) Fallopian tube (ampulla region)
4. Which of the following is an endocrine gland?
A) Salivary gland
B) Sweat gland
C) Thyroid
D) Liver
Answer: C) Thyroid — ductless gland secreting T3, T4, calcitonin
5. The "fight or flight" hormone is:
A) Insulin
B) Adrenaline
C) Estrogen
D) Thyroxine
Answer: B) Adrenaline (Epinephrine) — from adrenal medulla
6. The first menstruation is called:
A) Menopause
B) Menarche
C) Menstruation
D) Ovulation
Answer: B) Menarche — first menstrual period (usually 10-16 years)
7. Which hormone is detected in pregnancy tests?
A) Estrogen
B) Progesterone
C) hCG
D) Oxytocin
Answer: C) hCG (Human Chorionic Gonadotropin) — secreted by placenta
8. Which contraceptive method also protects against STDs?
A) Oral pills
B) IUD (Copper-T)
C) Condom
D) Tubectomy
Answer: C) Condom — barrier method that prevents both pregnancy and STDs
9. Iodine deficiency can cause which disorder?
A) Diabetes
B) Goiter
C) Rickets
D) Anemia
Answer: B) Goiter — enlarged thyroid due to insufficient iodine for thyroxine synthesis
10. The feedback mechanism that controls childbirth is:
A) Negative feedback
B) Positive feedback
C) Neutral feedback
D) No feedback
Answer: B) Positive feedback — oxytocin release → contractions → more oxytocin release → until birth
11. Which part of the brain links the nervous and endocrine systems?
A) Cerebrum
B) Cerebellum
C) Hypothalamus
D) Medulla
Answer: C) Hypothalamus — controls pituitary gland via releasing hormones
12. The hormone responsible for milk ejection during breastfeeding is:
A) Prolactin
B) Oxytocin
C) Estrogen
D) Progesterone
Answer: B) Oxytocin (Prolactin stimulates milk PRODUCTION; Oxytocin causes milk EJECTION)
