1. Health & Its Significance

Health is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being (WHO definition). It is NOT merely the absence of disease. NCERT Class 9 — Why Do We Fall Ill

Factors Affecting Health
  • Balanced diet — adequate nutrients (carbs, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, fiber, water)
  • Personal hygiene — cleanliness of body, clothes, surroundings
  • Clean environment — safe drinking water, fresh air, proper waste disposal
  • Regular exercise & sleep — physical fitness, 7-9 hrs sleep, stress reduction
  • Mental well-being — stress management, positive relationships, emotional balance
  • Social connections — family & community support, sense of belonging
  • Regular check-ups — early detection of diseases, vaccinations
Disease — Types & Classification (NCERT Class 9)
  • Disease — any disturbance in the normal functioning of the body
  • Acute — short duration, sudden onset, severe symptoms (e.g., common cold, flu, food poisoning). Usually self-limiting or quickly treated
  • Chronic — long duration (months to years), slow progression (e.g., tuberculosis, diabetes, arthritis, cancer). Require long-term management
  • Communicable (Infectious) — spread from person to person; caused by pathogens (e.g., cold, COVID-19, TB, malaria, HIV)
  • Non-communicable (Non-infectious) — not transmitted; caused by genetic, lifestyle, or environmental factors (e.g., diabetes, cancer, heart disease, hypertension, asthma)
  • Congenital — present from birth (e.g., heart defects, Down syndrome)
  • Acquired — develops after birth (most diseases)
  • Deficiency diseases — caused by lack of nutrients (e.g., scurvy ← Vit C, rickets ← Vit D, anemia ← iron, goiter ← iodine)
NCERT Connect — Class 9 Ch 13
"Why Do We Fall Ill" teaches that health depends on individual (diet, hygiene, exercise) and social factors (clean water, sanitation, healthcare access). Disease causes = immediate (pathogen) + contributory (poor nutrition, stress, weak immunity).

Means of Transmission (NCERT Class 9)
  • Airborne — coughing, sneezing, talking (TB, common cold, flu, COVID-19, chickenpox)
  • Waterborne/Foodborne — contaminated water/food (cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A, amoebiasis)
  • Vector-borne — insects (malaria ← Anopheles mosquito, dengue ← Aedes mosquito, plague ← rat flea, kala-azar ← sandfly)
  • Blood/body fluids — unprotected sex, needle-sharing, blood transfusion (HIV, hepatitis B, syphilis)
  • Direct contact — skin-to-skin (ringworm, scabies), sexual contact (STDs)
  • Mother to child — during pregnancy/birth/breastfeeding (HIV, syphilis, rubella)

3. Immune System — Innate Immunity

Immunity is the body's ability to recognize and defend against pathogens. The immune system has two main branches: Innate (non-specific) and Adaptive (specific).

First Line of Defense — Physical & Chemical Barriers
  • Skin — Tough, dry, keratinized outer layer; sheds dead cells carrying microbes; intact skin prevents pathogen entry
  • Mucous membranes — Line respiratory, digestive, urinary tracts; secrete sticky mucus that traps microbes
  • Cilia — Tiny hair-like projections in airways; sweep mucus + trapped microbes upward → coughed out or swallowed (mucociliary escalator)
  • Stomach acid (HCl) — pH ~2 kills most ingested bacteria
  • Lysozyme — Antibacterial enzyme in tears, saliva, sweat, mucus; destroys bacterial cell walls
  • Interferons — Proteins released by virus-infected cells; warn neighboring cells, inhibit viral replication
  • Defensins — Antimicrobial peptides on skin & mucous membranes
  • Normal flora (commensal bacteria) — Compete with pathogens for space & nutrients (e.g., gut flora, vaginal lactobacilli)
Second Line of Defense — Cellular & Inflammatory Response
  • Phagocytes — White blood cells that engulf & digest pathogens (phagocytosis)
  • Neutrophils — Most abundant WBC (60-70%); first responders; short-lived; form pus
  • Macrophages — "Big eaters"; reside in tissues; also present antigens to T-cells (APC)
  • Natural Killer (NK) cells — Kill virus-infected cells & tumor cells
  • Inflammatory response — Signs: Redness, Heat, Swelling, Pain, Loss of function. Triggered by damaged cells releasing histamine (from mast cells) → vasodilation → increased blood flow → WBCs arrive
  • Complement system — ~30 proteins in blood; cascade that opsonizes (marks) pathogens, forms membrane attack complex, attracts phagocytes
  • Fever — Elevated body temperature inhibits pathogen growth & enhances immune activity. Pyrogens (from pathogens or immune cells) reset hypothalamus thermostat
Why Do We Get Pus?
Pus = dead neutrophils + dead bacteria + tissue debris + fluid. Formation of pus indicates the body is fighting a bacterial infection. White blood cells rush to the site, engulf bacteria, and die — accumulating as pus.

Immune System Disorders
Allergies — Overreaction of immune system to harmless substances (allergens like pollen, dust, peanuts). Mast cells release histamine → symptoms (sneezing, itching, hives, anaphylaxis). Treatment: antihistamines, epinephrine (for anaphylaxis).
Autoimmune diseases — Immune system attacks body's own cells: Rheumatoid arthritis (joints), Type 1 diabetes (pancreas β-cells), Multiple sclerosis (myelin sheath), Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) (multiple organs), Hashimoto's thyroiditis (thyroid).
Immunodeficiency — Weak immune system: HIV/AIDS (acquired), SCID (severe combined immunodeficiency — genetic, "bubble boy disease").

5. Principles of Treatment (NCERT Class 9)

Treatment aims to (1) reduce symptoms, (2) kill the pathogen, and (3) strengthen the immune system.

Treatment Approaches
  • Symptomatic treatment — Reduces symptoms without killing pathogen. Eg: paracetamol (fever), cough syrup, antihistamines (cold), ORS (dehydration)
  • Specific (Antimicrobial) treatment — Targets the pathogen directly
Antimicrobial Drugs
  • Antibiotics — Kill or inhibit bacteria. Examples: Penicillin (discovered by Alexander Fleming, 1928), Streptomycin, Tetracycline, Ciprofloxacin. Antibiotics do NOT work against viruses
  • Antivirals — Inhibit viral replication. Eg: Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) for flu, Acyclovir for herpes, Zidovudine (AZT) for HIV, Sofosbuvir for hepatitis C
  • Antifungals — Treat fungal infections. Eg: Clotrimazole (topical), Fluconazole (oral), Amphotericin B (IV for serious infections)
  • Antiprotozoals — Eg: Chloroquine (malaria), Metronidazole (amoebiasis)
  • Anthelmintics — Kill parasitic worms. Eg: Albendazole, Mebendazole
Antibiotic Resistance — A Global Threat
  • Misuse/overuse of antibiotics → bacteria evolve resistance (MRSA — Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus, XDR-TB — Extensively drug-resistant TB)
  • Even when viruses cause symptoms (cold, flu), antibiotics don't help — yet many people misuse them
  • Prevention: Complete full course of antibiotics as prescribed; don't use leftover antibiotics; don't demand antibiotics for viral infections; doctors should avoid unnecessary prescribing
Antibiotics vs Viruses
Why don't antibiotics work on viruses? Viruses use host cell machinery to replicate. Antibiotics target bacterial structures (cell wall, ribosomes) that viruses don't have. That's why the common cold and flu are treated symptomatically, not with antibiotics!

6. Principles of Prevention & Hygiene

NCERT Class 9 — Why Do We Fall Ill: Three levels of prevention.

Three Levels of Prevention (NCERT Class 9)
LevelGoalExamples
Primary preventionPrevent disease from occurringVaccination, hand washing, safe drinking water, mosquito control, hygiene education, healthy diet, exercise, avoiding smoking
Secondary preventionEarly detection & treatment to stop progressionScreening tests (mammography, Pap smear), regular check-ups, early diagnosis & treatment of infections
Tertiary preventionReduce complications & disabilityRehabilitation after stroke, physiotherapy, insulin for diabetes, dialysis for kidney failure, antiretroviral therapy for HIV
Personal Hygiene
  • Hand washing — Before eating, after toilet, after coughing/sneezing, after touching animals. Use soap & water for 20 sec
  • Oral hygiene — Brush twice daily, floss, dental check-ups. Prevents cavities (dental caries), gum disease (gingivitis, periodontitis)
  • Bathing & skin care — Regular bathing removes sweat, dirt, microbes. Clean clothes daily
  • Nail care — Short, clean nails prevent dirt & microbe accumulation
  • Safe food handling — Wash raw food, cook thoroughly, separate raw & cooked, refrigerate properly, check expiry
Community Hygiene & Sanitation
  • Safe drinking water — Boiling, filtration (reverse osmosis, UV), chlorination. Essential to prevent waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A)
  • Proper sewage & waste disposal — Sewage treatment plants, septic tanks, proper garbage collection. Open defecation → disease spread
  • Vector control — Mosquito nets (LLINs), insecticide spraying (DDT, malathion), eliminating stagnant water (mosquito breeding), neem oil, larvivorous fish (Gambusia)
  • Air pollution control — Indoor smoke (cooking with biomass), outdoor pollution → respiratory diseases. Use clean fuels (LPG), masks, air purifiers
  • WHO's 5 Keys to Safer Food: (1) Keep clean, (2) Separate raw & cooked, (3) Cook thoroughly, (4) Keep food at safe temperature, (5) Use safe water & raw materials

7. Vaccination & Immunization

Vaccination is the process of administering a vaccine (weakened/killed pathogen or antigen) to stimulate the immune system to produce antibodies and memory cells WITHOUT causing the disease. NCERT Class 8 — Microorganisms, Class 9 — Why Do We Fall Ill

How Vaccines Work
  • Vaccine contains antigens (weakened/killed pathogen, toxin, or mRNA/protein fragment)
  • Body's immune system: B-cells produce antibodies + T-cells respond + Memory cells created
  • On subsequent exposure to real pathogen → immune system "remembers" and responds rapidly → prevents disease or reduces severity
  • This is active artificial immunity
  • Herd immunity — when enough people are vaccinated → disease cannot spread easily → protects vulnerable (those who cannot be vaccinated)

8. First Aid Basics

  • ABC of First Aid — Airway (clear airway), Breathing (check for breathing), Circulation (chest compressions if needed)
  • CPR — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation: 30 chest compressions (at least 5 cm deep, 100-120/min) → 2 rescue breaths → repeat. Continue until help arrives or person recovers
  • Burns — Cool under running water for 10-20 min; cover with sterile non-stick cloth; do NOT apply ice, butter, or toothpaste; seek medical help for serious burns
  • Fracture/Sprain — Immobilize with splint (extend beyond adjacent joints); apply ice for swelling; do NOT move or realign; transport to hospital
  • Bleeding — Apply direct pressure with sterile gauze/cloth; elevate injured part; if wound is deep, seek medical attention; apply tourniquet only if severe hemorrhage
  • Snake/Insect bite — Keep victim calm (reduces venom spread); immobilize bitten limb; remove tight clothing/jewelry; rush to hospital; do NOT cut/suck the wound or apply tourniquet
  • Choking — Heimlich maneuver (abdominal thrusts): stand behind victim, fist above navel, thrust inward & upward (for adults/children >1 yr). For infants: back blows + chest thrusts
  • Heat stroke — Move to shade, cool body with water/fan, give water if conscious. High body temp (>40°C), confusion, hot dry skin
  • Drowning — Remove from water, check ABC, start CPR immediately, call emergency services
First Aid Kit Essentials
Sterile gauze & bandages, adhesive bandages (band-aids), antiseptic solution (betadine), scissors, tweezers, gloves, pain relievers (paracetamol/ibuprofen), thermometer, ORS packets, burn cream, antihistamines (for allergies), elastic bandage (for sprains).

9. Drugs, Alcohol & Substance Abuse

  • Drug abuse — Harmful use of psychoactive substances. Common drugs: Opioids (heroin, morphine — pain relief, euphoria, addiction), Cocaine (stimulant — euphoria, then crash), Cannabis (Marijuana, Hashish) (hallucinogen — altered perception, impaired coordination), Inhalants (glue, paint thinner — cheap, accessible, dangerous), Hallucinogens (LSD, psilocybin — altered reality, bad trips)
  • Alcohol abuse — Short-term: impaired judgment, slurred speech, loss of coordination, vomiting, blackouts. Long-term: Liver cirrhosis, pancreatitis, brain damage (Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome), heart disease, addiction, malnutrition, social problems
  • Smoking/Tobacco — Contains Nicotine (highly addictive), Tar (carcinogen — causes lung, oral, throat, esophageal cancer), Carbon monoxide (CO) (binds hemoglobin → reduces O₂ carrying capacity). Effects: Lung cancer (90% of cases linked to smoking), COPD, emphysema, chronic bronchitis, heart disease, stroke, oral cancer, stained teeth, bad breath, premature aging
  • Passive smoking (second-hand smoke) — Non-smokers inhaling smoke from others' cigarettes → same health risks (lung cancer, asthma in children)
  • Effects of addiction — Physical dependency (withdrawal symptoms), psychological dependency (cravings), financial loss, family breakdown, job loss, criminal behavior, overdose → death
  • Prevention: Education & awareness (school programs, media campaigns), counseling, healthy hobbies & sports, peer support groups (AA, NA), learning to say NO assertively, parental guidance, strict laws (no tobacco sales to minors, smoking bans)
NCERT Connect — Class 9 Ch 13
"Why Do We Fall Ill" emphasizes that prevention is better than cure. Maintaining good health through hygiene, nutrition, exercise, and avoiding substance abuse is far easier than treating disease. Community health depends on public health measures — sanitation, clean water, vector control, and immunization programs.

10. Cancer & Other Major Non-Communicable Diseases

Cancer
  • Cancer — Uncontrolled, abnormal cell division. Cells lose ability to differentiate & undergo apoptosis (programmed cell death)
  • Benign — Localized, does not spread, usually not life-threatening (can be removed surgically)
  • Malignant (Cancerous) — Invades nearby tissues; spreads via blood/lymph to other parts (metastasis)
  • Common types: Carcinoma (epithelial — lung, breast, prostate, colon, skin), Sarcoma (connective tissue — bone, muscle), Leukemia (blood — WBC cancer), Lymphoma (lymphatic system), Melanoma (skin — dangerous)
  • Risk factors: Smoking (lung, oral, bladder), UV radiation (skin), HPV (cervical), Hepatitis B (liver), asbestos (mesothelioma), dietary factors (processed meat → colorectal), genetic mutations (BRCA → breast/ovarian), obesity, alcohol, pollution
  • Warning signs (CAUTION): Change in bowel/bladder habits, A sore that doesn't heal, Unusual bleeding/discharge, Thickening/lump in breast or elsewhere, Indigestion/difficulty swallowing, Obvious change in wart/mole, Nagging cough/hoarseness
  • Treatment: Surgery (remove tumor), Radiotherapy (radiation kills cancer cells), Chemotherapy (drugs kill rapidly dividing cells), Immunotherapy, Targeted therapy, Hormone therapy, Bone marrow transplant
Other Major Non-Communicable Diseases
  • Diabetes Mellitus — High blood glucose. Type 1 (autoimmune — insulin dependent), Type 2 (insulin resistance — lifestyle related). Symptoms: polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia, weight loss
  • Hypertension (High BP) — BP >140/90 mmHg. "Silent killer" — often asymptomatic. Risk factor for heart attack, stroke, kidney failure. Management: low salt diet, exercise, medications
  • Cardiovascular diseases — Heart attack (myocardial infarction), angina, stroke, atherosclerosis. Risk factors: smoking, high cholesterol, hypertension, diabetes, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, stress
  • Asthma — Chronic airway inflammation → wheezing, breathlessness, cough. Triggers: allergens, cold air, exercise, stress. Treatment: inhalers (bronchodilators, corticosteroids)
  • Obesity — Excess body fat (BMI ≥30). Causes: overeating, sedentary lifestyle, genetics. Complications: diabetes, heart disease, joint problems, sleep apnea, cancer
Lifestyle & Non-communicable Diseases
Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) cause ~71% of deaths worldwide. Key preventable risk factors: Tobacco use, unhealthy diet, physical inactivity, harmful alcohol use. Prevention: healthy diet (more fruits/vegetables, less salt/sugar/fat), regular exercise (30 min/day, 5 days/week), no smoking, limited alcohol, stress management.
