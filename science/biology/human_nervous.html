1. Overview — Nervous System

The nervous system controls and coordinates all body activities. It detects stimuli (changes in environment) and produces responses.

  • Two main divisions: Central Nervous System (CNS) — Brain + Spinal Cord | Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) — Cranial nerves + Spinal nerves
  • PNS divided into: Somatic nervous system (voluntary — control skeletal muscles) | Autonomic nervous system (involuntary — controls organs/glands)
  • Autonomic further: Sympathetic (fight or flight) | Parasympathetic (rest and digest)

2. Neuron — Structure & Function

The neuron is the structural and functional unit of the nervous system. It is specialized for conducting nerve impulses.

  • Cell body (Soma) — contains nucleus, Nissl granules, neurofibrils. Also called perikaryon
  • Dendrites — short, highly branched; receive signals from other neurons/receptors
  • Axon — single, long; carries impulse away from cell body. Ends at synaptic knobs (boutons)
  • Myelin sheath — insulating layer; made by Schwann cells (PNS) or Oligodendrocytes (CNS)
  • Nodes of Ranvier — gaps in myelin; saltatory conduction (impulse jumps → faster transmission)
Types of Neurons
  • Sensory (afferent) — carry impulses from receptors → CNS
  • Motor (efferent) — carry impulses from CNS → effectors (muscles, glands)
  • Interneurons (association) — connect sensory & motor neurons within CNS (most numerous)
Nerve Impulse Transmission
  • Resting membrane potential: -70 mV (inside negative relative to outside — Na⁺ outside, K⁺ inside)
  • Na⁺/K⁺ pump maintains gradient: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in (active transport, uses ATP)
  • Depolarization: Stimulus opens Na⁺ channels → Na⁺ rushes in → potential becomes +40 mV (action potential)
  • Repolarization: K⁺ channels open → K⁺ rushes out → returns to -70 mV
  • Refractory period — brief period after impulse when neuron cannot fire again
  • Speed: 1-120 m/s (myelinated = faster, unmyelinated = slower)
Mnemonic: "RIP — Resting -70, Inside negative, Polarized"
Resting -70 | Depolarization = Na⁺ in | Repolarization = K⁺ out | Hyperpolarization = more negative

3. Brain — The Control Center

The brain is protected by cranium (skull) and 3 layers of meninges (dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater). Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) cushions the brain.

Forebrain (Prosencephalon)
  • Cerebrum — largest part (80% of brain). Divided into 2 hemispheres (left & right) connected by corpus callosum
  • Outer layer: Cerebral cortex (grey matter — cell bodies); inner: white matter (axons)
  • Lobes: Frontal (thinking, planning, personality), Parietal (sensory processing), Temporal (hearing, memory), Occipital (vision)
  • Thalamus — relay station for sensory information
  • Hypothalamus — controls body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, circadian rhythm, pituitary gland
Midbrain (Mesencephalon)
  • Connects forebrain to hindbrain; contains corpora quadrigemina (visual & auditory reflexes)
Hindbrain (Rhombencephalon)
  • Cerebellum — Balance, posture, coordination of voluntary movements. Second largest part
  • Pons — connects cerebellum to medulla; controls breathing
  • Medulla Oblongata — Vital centers: breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, vomiting, coughing, sneezing

4. Spinal Cord

  • Long, cylindrical structure: ~42-45 cm (adult); extends from medulla to L1-L2 vertebra
  • Protected by vertebral column + meninges + CSF
  • Contains grey matter (H-shaped, center — cell bodies) surrounded by white matter (axons)
  • 31 pairs of spinal nerves: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal
  • Functions: Conducts impulses between brain & body; center for reflex actions

5. Peripheral Nervous System

  • Cranial nerves — 12 pairs from brain (I-XII). Eg: Optic (II — vision), Vagus (X — visceral organs)
  • Spinal nerves — 31 pairs from spinal cord
  • Autonomic Nervous System (involuntary):
    • Sympathetic — "Fight or Flight": ↑ HR, ↑ BP, dilated pupil, bronchodilation, ↓ digestion. Neurotransmitter: Noradrenaline
    • Parasympathetic — "Rest and Digest": ↓ HR, ↓ BP, constricted pupil, ↑ digestion. Neurotransmitter: Acetylcholine

6. Reflex Arc

Reflex action is a rapid, automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus. It occurs via the reflex arc — the neural pathway.

  • Pathway: Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory neuron → Spinal cord (interneuron) → Motor neuron → Effector (muscle/gland) → Response
  • Examples: Knee jerk (patellar reflex), Withdrawing hand from hot object, Blinking
  • Advantage: Fast response — doesn't wait for brain to process (saves time, prevents injury)
  • Reflexes: Simple (monosynaptic — knee jerk) vs Complex (polysynaptic — withdrawal)

7. Sense Organs

Eye — Vision
  • Wall layers: Sclera (white outer) → Choroid (blood vessels) → Retina (photoreceptors — rods & cones)
  • Rods: Scotopic vision (dim light, black & white) — pigment: Rhodopsin
  • Cones: Photopic vision (bright light, color) — pigment: Photopsin (3 types for red, green, blue)
  • Cornea — transparent front → Iris (colored, controls pupil size) → Lens → Retina
  • Fovea centralis — highest concentration of cones (sharpest vision)
  • Blind spot — where optic nerve leaves retina (no photoreceptors)
  • Myopia (near-sighted) — eyeball too long → image falls in front of retina; corrected by concave lens
  • Hypermetropia (far-sighted) — eyeball too short → image falls behind retina; corrected by convex lens
  • Cataract — lens becomes opaque (cloudy) → vision loss; treated by lens replacement surgery
Ear — Hearing & Balance
  • Outer ear — Pinna + Ear canal → collects sound
  • Middle ear — Tympanic membrane (eardrum) → 3 ossicles: Malleus (hammer), Incus (anvil), Stapes (stirrup) — amplify vibrations
  • Inner ear — Cochlea (hearing — hair cells convert vibrations to nerve impulses) + Vestibular apparatus (balance — semicircular canals)
  • Eustachian tube — connects middle ear to pharynx; equalizes pressure
Nose — Smell
  • Olfactory epithelium on roof of nasal cavity → contains olfactory receptor cells for smell
  • Signals go to olfactory bulb → brain
Tongue — Taste
  • Taste buds on papillae of tongue — ~10,000 taste buds
  • Five basic tastes: Sweet (sugar), Sour (acid), Salty (NaCl), Bitter (alkaloids), Umami (savory — glutamate)
Skin — Touch
  • Receptors in skin: Meissner's corpuscles (light touch), Pacinian corpuscles (deep pressure), Thermoreceptors (temperature), Nociceptors (pain)

8. Neurotransmitters & Synapses

  • Synapse — junction between two neurons where signals are transmitted
  • Types: Electrical synapse (direct, fast, rare in humans) | Chemical synapse (common, slower, uses neurotransmitters)
  • Process: Impulse arrives at synaptic knob → Ca²⁺ influx → vesicles release neurotransmitter into synaptic cleft → binds to receptors on postsynaptic membrane → new impulse generated

10. Practice MCQs

1. The largest part of the brain is:
A) Cerebellum
B) Medulla
C) Cerebrum
D) Pons
Answer: C) Cerebrum — 80% of brain, controls voluntary actions, thought, memory
2. Resting membrane potential of neuron is:
A) +40 mV
B) -70 mV
C) -90 mV
D) 0 mV
Answer: B) -70 mV (inside negative, outside positive)
3. Which part of the brain controls balance & coordination?
A) Cerebrum
B) Cerebellum
C) Medulla
D) Thalamus
Answer: B) Cerebellum — coordinates voluntary movements and maintains posture
4. The reflex arc does NOT involve:
A) Sensory neuron
B) Motor neuron
C) Brain
D) Effector
Answer: C) Brain — reflexes are processed in spinal cord for faster response
5. Myopia is corrected by which lens?
A) Convex
B) Concave
C) Bifocal
D) Cylindrical
Answer: B) Concave lens (near-sightedness — eyeball too long)
6. Which neurotransmitter is involved in muscle contraction?
A) Dopamine
B) Acetylcholine
C) Serotonin
D) GABA
Answer: B) Acetylcholine (ACh) — at neuromuscular junction
7. How many pairs of cranial nerves are there?
A) 8
B) 10
C) 12
D) 31
Answer: C) 12 pairs (31 pairs of spinal nerves)
8. The vital centers for heart rate and breathing are in:
A) Cerebrum
B) Cerebellum
C) Medulla oblongata
D) Hypothalamus
Answer: C) Medulla oblongata — controls breathing, heart rate, blood pressure
