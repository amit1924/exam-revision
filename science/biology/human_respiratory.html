1. Types of Respiration

Respiration is the process of releasing energy from food (glucose) using oxygen (aerobic) or without oxygen (anaerobic). The overall equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (ATP)

  • Aerobic respiration — occurs in mitochondria, requires O₂, produces 36-38 ATP per glucose — complete breakdown
  • Anaerobic respiration — occurs in cytoplasm, no O₂ required, produces only 2 ATP per glucose — partial breakdown
  • In humans: Aerobic (most tissues) + Anaerobic (muscles during intense exercise → lactic acid buildup causes cramps)
  • In yeast: Anaerobic → Ethanol + CO₂ (fermentation — used in baking, brewing)

2. Human Respiratory System — Organs

The human respiratory system consists of air passages and lungs for exchange of O₂ and CO₂ between body and environment.

  • Pathway: Nostrils (Nasal Cavity) → Pharynx → Larynx (Voice Box) → Trachea (Windpipe) → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli (air sacs)
Upper Respiratory Tract
  • Nasal Cavity — lined with ciliated epithelium + mucus → filters, warms, moistens air. Has hairs (vibrissae) to trap dust
  • Pharynx — common passage for air & food (nasopharynx → oropharynx → laryngopharynx)
  • Larynx — contains vocal cords (sound production). Epiglottis — flap that prevents food from entering trachea during swallowing
Lower Respiratory Tract
  • Trachea — 10-12 cm long; supported by 16-20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings (prevents collapse)
  • Divides into Right bronchus (wider, shorter, vertical → aspirated objects more likely here) and Left bronchus
  • Bronchi → Bronchioles (no cartilage) → Terminal bronchioles → Respiratory bronchioles → Alveolar ducts → Alveoli
Lungs & Pleura
  • Pair of lungs in thoracic cavity, covered by pleural membranes (visceral + parietal pleura)
  • Pleural fluid — reduces friction during breathing
  • Right lung — 3 lobes (superior, middle, inferior); Left lung — 2 lobes (superior, inferior) + cardiac notch (for heart)
  • Total alveoli in both lungs: ~300-500 million → surface area ~80-100 m² (tennis court!)
Alveoli — Site of Gas Exchange
  • Thin wall: Simple squamous epithelium (one cell thick)
  • Surrounded by blood capillaries
  • Gas exchange by simple diffusion (O₂ → blood, CO₂ → alveoli)
  • Surfactant — phospholipid layer lining alveoli → reduces surface tension, prevents collapse

3. Mechanism of Breathing

Breathing (Pulmonary ventilation) involves Inspiration (inhalation) and Expiration (exhalation). It is controlled by diaphragm and intercostal muscles.

Inspiration (Active process)
  • Diaphragm contracts → flattens (↓)
  • External intercostal muscles contract → ribs move up & out
  • ↑ Thoracic volume → ↓ Intrapulmonary pressure (-3 mmHg relative to atmosphere) → air rushes in
Expiration (Passive normally, Active during forced)
  • Diaphragm relaxes → moves up (↑)
  • External intercostals relax → ribs move down & in
  • ↓ Thoracic volume → ↑ Intrapulmonary pressure → air pushed out
  • Forced expiration uses internal intercostals & abdominal muscles

4. Gas Exchange & Transport

Exchange of Gases (At Alveoli)
  • Gases move by simple diffusion based on concentration/pressure gradients
  • Alveoli: O₂ (high) → Blood, CO₂ (low in alveoli but high in blood) ← Blood → exhaled
  • Partial pressure concept: Each gas exerts its own pressure in a mixture (Dalton's law)

Oxygen Transport
  • O₂ is transported mainly by Hemoglobin (Hb) in RBCs — 97% of O₂ bound to Hb as oxyhemoglobin
  • Each Hb molecule carries 4 O₂ molecules (one per heme group with Fe²⁺)
  • Remaining 3% dissolved in plasma
  • Oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve — sigmoid shape (cooperative binding)
  • Bohr effect: ↑ CO₂ / ↓ pH → Hb releases more O₂ (in tissues)
Carbon Dioxide Transport
  • CO₂ transported in 3 ways: As bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) — 70%, Bound to Hb (Carbaminohemoglobin) — 20-25%, Dissolved in plasma — 5-7%
  • Carbonic anhydrase enzyme (in RBCs) catalyzes: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
  • Chloride shift (Hamburger phenomenon) — HCO₃⁻ moves out of RBC, Cl⁻ moves in to maintain ionic balance
Key Numbers
97% O₂ carried by Hb | 70% CO₂ as bicarbonate | 20-25% CO₂ as carbaminohemoglobin | Normal Hb: 12-16 g/dL (♂), 11-14 g/dL (♀)

6. Cellular Respiration — Energy Release

The breakdown of glucose to produce ATP occurs in 3 stages:

Stage 1: Glycolysis (Cytoplasm)
  • Anaerobic — no O₂ required
  • Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvate (3C) + 2 ATP + 2 NADH
  • Enzyme: Hexokinase, PFK, Pyruvate kinase
  • Occurs in cytoplasm
Stage 2: Krebs Cycle (Mitochondrial Matrix)
  • Aerobic — requires O₂
  • Pyruvate → Acetyl CoA → enters cycle → produces 2 ATP + 6 NADH + 2 FADH₂ + CO₂ (per glucose)
  • Also called TCA cycle or Citric acid cycle
  • Discovered by: Hans Krebs (1937)
Stage 3: Electron Transport Chain (ETC) — Inner Mitochondrial Membrane
  • NADH & FADH₂ donate electrons → Oxidative phosphorylation
  • Produces ~32-34 ATP + H₂O
  • Final electron acceptor: O₂ (that's why we need oxygen!)
  • ATP synthase — enzyme that makes ATP
Total ATP Count
Glycolysis (2) + Krebs (2) + ETC (32-34) = 36-38 ATP per glucose molecule. "The energy currency of life!"

Remember: Vital Capacity = TV + IRV + ERV — it's the maximum you can exhale after a deep breath. RV keeps lungs from collapsing.

8. Control of Breathing

  • Breathing is controlled by the Respiratory Center in the Medulla Oblongata (brainstem)
  • Dorsal respiratory group (DRG) — initiates inspiration
  • Ventral respiratory group (VRG) — active during forced breathing
  • Pneumotaxic center (Pons) — limits inspiration, prevents over-inflation
  • Rate: ~12-16 breaths/min at rest; 500 mL/breath → total ~6-8 L/min
  • Chemoreceptors — in carotid & aortic bodies, medulla — sense CO₂/pH levels (primary driver for breathing)< /li>
  • Hyperventilation — rapid breathing → ↓ CO₂ → dizziness
  • Hypoxia — low O₂ in tissues → blue skin (cyanosis)

Smoking Effects
Tobacco smoking damages cilia (→ smoker's cough), destroys alveoli (→ emphysema), contains carcinogens (→ lung cancer). Nicotine is addictive, CO (carbon monoxide) in smoke binds Hb 200x stronger than O₂ → reduces O₂ carrying capacity.

10. Practice MCQs

1. The site of gaseous exchange in the lungs is:
A) Bronchi
B) Bronchioles
C) Alveoli
D) Trachea
Answer: C) Alveoli — thin-walled air sacs surrounded by capillaries
2. The respiratory pigment in human blood is:
A) Chlorophyll
B) Hemoglobin
C) Myoglobin
D) Melanin
Answer: B) Hemoglobin — Fe²⁺ containing protein in RBCs
3. During inhalation, the diaphragm:
A) Relaxes
B) Contracts
C) Moves up
D) No change
Answer: B) Contracts and flattens → increases thoracic volume
4. Anaerobic respiration in muscles produces:
A) Ethanol
B) Lactic acid
C) CO₂
D) Oxygen
Answer: B) Lactic acid — causes muscle cramps during intense exercise
5. Vital capacity is:
A) TV + IRV
B) TV + ERV
C) TV + IRV + ERV
D) TV + IRV + ERV + RV
Answer: C) TV + IRV + ERV = ~4000-4600 mL
6. Which enzyme in RBCs helps in CO₂ transport?
A) Pepsin
B) Carbonic anhydrase
C) Trypsin
D) Lipase
Answer: B) Carbonic anhydrase — catalyzes CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃
7. The structure that prevents food from entering the trachea is:
A) Pharynx
B) Epiglottis
C) Larynx
D) Uvula
Answer: B) Epiglottis — flap-like cartilage covering larynx during swallowing
8. Total ATP produced from one glucose molecule is:
A) 2
B) 8
C) 24
D) 36-38
Answer: D) 36-38 ATP (Glycolysis 2 + Krebs 2 + ETC 32-34)
