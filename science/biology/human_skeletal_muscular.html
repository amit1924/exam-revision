1. Skeletal System — Overview

The skeletal system is the framework of bones and cartilage that supports and protects the body. Study of bones: Osteology. Total bones in adult human: 206. Newborn: ~300 bones that fuse during growth.

Functions of Skeleton (NCERT Class 6 — Body Movements)
  • Support — Provides structural framework for the body, holds organs in place
  • Protection — Skull protects brain; ribs protect heart & lungs; vertebral column protects spinal cord
  • Movement — Bones act as levers; muscles pull on bones to produce movement
  • Mineral storage — Stores calcium (Ca) and phosphorus (P); releases into blood as needed
  • Blood cell formation — Red bone marrow produces RBC, WBC, platelets (Hematopoiesis)
  • Energy storage — Yellow bone marrow stores fat (adipose tissue)
NCERT Connect — Class 6
Chapter: Body Movements — You learned that the skeleton gives shape to the body and helps in movement. Bones are hard while cartilage is flexible. The skull protects the brain, ribs protect the heart and lungs, and the backbone protects the spinal cord.

Bone Cells (NCERT Class 9 — Tissues: Connective Tissue)
  • Osteocytes — Mature bone cells; maintain bone matrix (most numerous)
  • Osteoblasts — Bone-forming cells; synthesize collagen & deposit minerals (build bone)
  • Osteoclasts — Bone-resorbing cells; break down bone to release Ca²⁺ into blood (large, multinucleate)
  • Osteoprogenitor cells — Stem cells that differentiate into osteoblasts
Microscopic Structure of Bone
  • Compact bone (Cortical) — Dense outer layer; provides strength; contains Haversian systems (osteons)
  • Spongy bone (Cancellous/Trabecular) — Porous inner layer; contains red bone marrow; reduces weight
  • Periosteum — Outer fibrous membrane covering bone (except joints); contains blood vessels & nerves
  • Endosteum — Inner membrane lining medullary cavity
  • Medullary cavity — Central cavity in long bones; contains yellow bone marrow (fat)

3. Division of Skeleton

Human skeleton divided into: Axial Skeleton (80 bones) + Appendicular Skeleton (126 bones)

Axial Skeleton (80 bones) — Core of the body
  • Skull — 22 bones (8 cranial + 14 facial). Cranial bones: Frontal, Parietal (2), Temporal (2), Occipital, Sphenoid, Ethmoid. Facial: Maxilla (2), Zygomatic (2), Mandible, Nasal (2), etc. Bones joined by sutures (immovable joints). Only movable bone: Mandible (lower jaw)
  • Hyoid bone — U-shaped; only bone not connected to any other bone; in neck; supports tongue
  • Ear ossicles — 6 bones (3 each ear): Malleus (hammer), Incus (anvil), Stapes (stirrup) — smallest bones in body
  • Vertebral column (Spine) — 26 bones: 7 cervical (C1-7), 12 thoracic (T1-12), 5 lumbar (L1-5), 1 sacrum (5 fused), 1 coccyx (4 fused). Curves: cervical (forward), thoracic (backward), lumbar (forward), sacral (backward). Functions: protects spinal cord, supports head, allows movement
  • Rib cage — 12 pairs ribs + Sternum (breastbone). 7 true ribs (directly attached to sternum), 3 false ribs (attached via cartilage), 2 floating ribs (no anterior attachment)
  • Note: Atlas (C1) — supports skull; Axis (C2) — allows head rotation ("no" movement)
Appendicular Skeleton (126 bones) — Limbs & girdles
  • Pectoral girdle (Shoulder) — 4 bones: Clavicle (collar bone, 2) + Scapula (shoulder blade, 2). Connects arms to axial skeleton
  • Upper limbs — 60 bones (30 each side): Humerus (upper arm) → Radius (thumb side) + Ulna (little finger side) → Carpals (wrist, 8) → Metacarpals (palm, 5) → Phalanges (fingers, 14)
  • Pelvic girdle (Hip) — 2 coxal bones (each: Ilium + Ischium + Pubis fused). Connects legs to axial skeleton. Transmits body weight to legs
  • Lower limbs — 60 bones (30 each side): Femur (thigh — longest bone, ~50 cm) → Patella (kneecap) → Tibia (shin — weight bearing) + Fibula (thin, lateral) → Tarsals (ankle, 7) → Metatarsals (foot, 5) → Phalanges (toes, 14)

4. Cartilage

Cartilage is a flexible, semi-rigid connective tissue. Study: Chondrology. It is avascular (no blood vessels) — nutrients diffuse through matrix. Cells: Chondrocytes (in spaces called lacunae).

Types of Cartilage
TypeFeaturesLocation
Hyaline cartilageMost common; glassy appearance; provides smooth surfaces for jointsNose tip, trachea, bronchi, larynx, articular surfaces of bones, costal cartilage (ribs to sternum), fetal skeleton
Elastic cartilageContains elastic fibers; more flexibleExternal ear (pinna), epiglottis, eustachian tube
FibrocartilageTough; contains collagen fibers; resists compression & tensionIntervertebral discs, menisci of knee, pubic symphysis, temporomandibular joint
Bone vs Cartilage (NCERT Class 9 — Tissues)
Bone: Hard, calcified matrix, has blood supply, can repair quickly, provides structural support | Cartilage: Flexible, non-calcified matrix, avascular, repairs slowly, provides cushioning & flexibility

Synovial Joint — Structure
  • Articular cartilage — smooth hyaline cartilage covering bone ends
  • Joint capsule — fibrous connective tissue enclosing the joint
  • Synovial membrane — inner lining that secretes synovial fluid (lubrication + nutrition)
  • Ligaments — connect bone to bone; provide stability
  • Bursae — fluid-filled sacs reduce friction

6. Muscular System — Overview

The muscular system enables body movement, maintains posture, and generates heat. Study: Myology. Muscles constitute ~40-50% of body weight. ~600+ muscles in human body.

Properties of Muscles
  • Excitability (Irritability) — ability to respond to stimuli (nerve impulses)
  • Contractility — ability to shorten and generate force
  • Extensibility — ability to stretch without damage
  • Elasticity — ability to return to original shape after contraction/extension
Muscle Naming Patterns
  • Location — Pectoralis (chest), Gluteus (buttock)
  • Shape — Deltoid (triangular), Trapezius (trapezoid)
  • Size — Gluteus maximus (largest), Gluteus minimus (smallest of group)
  • Number of origins — Biceps (2 heads), Triceps (3 heads), Quadriceps (4 heads)
  • Action — Flexor carpi (flexes wrist), Extensor digitorum (extends fingers)
Antagonistic Muscle Pairs
  • Muscles work in antagonistic pairs (one contracts while the other relaxes):
  • Biceps brachii (flexor — bends arm) ↔ Triceps brachii (extensor — straightens arm)
  • Quadriceps (extends leg) ↔ Hamstrings (flexes leg)
  • Deltoid (abducts arm) ↔ Latissimus dorsi/Pectoralis major (adduct arm)
Muscles & Bones Work Together (NCERT Class 6 — Body Movements)
Muscles are attached to bones by tendons. When muscle contracts, it pulls on the bone, causing movement at the joint. The origin is the fixed attachment, and the insertion is the movable attachment.

Mechanism of Muscle Contraction — Sliding Filament Theory (NCERT Class 9, 10)
  • Each muscle fiber contains myofibrils → made of sarcomeres (functional unit)
  • Sarcomere contains: Thin filaments (Actin) + Thick filaments (Myosin)
  • Process: Nerve impulse → Acetylcholine release → Ca²⁺ released from sarcoplasmic reticulum → Ca²⁺ binds Troponin → Tropomyosin moves exposing actin binding sites → Myosin heads bind actin (cross-bridge) → Power stroke (ATP → ADP + Pi) → Filaments slide → Sarcomere shortens → Muscle contracts
  • ATP required for: (1) Myosin head detachment, (2) Ca²⁺ pump back into SR, (3) Na⁺/K⁺ pump
  • Rigor mortis — after death, ATP depleted → myosin heads stuck to actin → body stiffens (2-6 hours after death, lasts ~24-72 hours)

8. Skeletal & Muscular Disorders

Skeletal Disorders
  • Osteoporosis — Decreased bone density (porous bones); common in postmenopausal women due to estrogen drop; ↑ risk of fractures (hip, wrist, spine). Prevention: Ca, Vitamin D, weight-bearing exercise
  • Rickets — Vitamin D deficiency in children → soft, weak bones → bowed legs, deformed pelvis. In adults: Osteomalacia
  • Scoliosis — Lateral (sideways) curvature of spine (C-shaped or S-shaped)
  • Kyphosis — Excessive outward curvature of thoracic spine (hunchback) — common in elderly
  • Lordosis — Excessive inward curvature of lumbar spine (swayback) — common in pregnancy, obesity
  • Fracture — Broken bone. Types: Simple (closed — skin intact), Compound (open — bone pierces skin), Greenstick (incomplete — common in children), Comminuted (bone shattered into pieces), Hairline (thin crack)
  • Paget's disease — Abnormal bone remodeling → enlarged, deformed bones
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta — "Brittle bone disease" — genetic disorder → fragile bones that break easily
Muscular Disorders
  • Muscular dystrophy — Genetic disorder → progressive muscle weakness & degeneration (Duchenne MD — most common, X-linked, affects boys)
  • Myasthenia gravis — Autoimmune → antibodies block acetylcholine receptors → muscle weakness (especially eyes, face, swallowing)
  • Tetanus — Bacterial infection (Clostridium tetani) → toxin causes continuous muscle contraction (lockjaw)
  • Muscle cramps — Sudden, involuntary muscle contraction (due to electrolyte imbalance, dehydration, fatigue)
  • Strain (pulled muscle) — Muscle/tendon overstretching or tearing
  • Atrophy — Muscle wasting due to disuse, denervation, or malnutrition
  • Fibromyalgia — Chronic widespread muscle pain, fatigue, tender points
First Aid for Fractures (NCERT Class 8 — First Aid)
Immobilize the injured part using a splint (board, stick, folded newspaper) — extend beyond adjacent joints. Apply ice to reduce swelling. Do NOT try to realign bones. Transport to hospital immediately. For open fractures, cover wound with sterile dressing before splinting.
Calcium & Vitamin D
Calcium — main mineral in bones (99% of body's Ca is in bones & teeth). Sources: Milk, curd, cheese, leafy greens, ragi. Vitamin D — needed for Ca absorption; synthesized in skin on sunlight exposure (morning sun). Deficiency → rickets (children) / osteomalacia (adults).

9. Practice MCQs

1. Total number of bones in adult human body:
A) 206
B) 300
C) 150
D) 250
Answer: A) 206 (newborn ~300, bones fuse during growth)
2. The longest bone in the human body is:
A) Tibia
B) Humerus
C) Femur
D) Fibula
Answer: C) Femur (thigh bone) — about 50 cm
3. Which type of joint is present in the shoulder?
A) Hinge
B) Ball & socket
C) Pivot
D) Gliding
Answer: B) Ball & socket — allows maximum movement
4. Biceps and triceps form which type of muscle pair?
A) Synergists
B) Antagonistic pair
C) Fixators
D) Prime movers
Answer: B) Antagonistic — biceps flexes arm, triceps extends arm
5. The smallest bone in the human body is:
A) Stapes
B) Malleus
C) Incus
D) Phalanx
Answer: A) Stapes (stirrup) in middle ear — about 3 mm
6. Which vitamin deficiency causes rickets?
A) Vitamin A
B) Vitamin C
C) Vitamin D
D) Vitamin E
Answer: C) Vitamin D — deficiency leads to soft, weak bones (rickets in children, osteomalacia in adults)
7. Which muscle is involuntary and found only in the heart?
A) Skeletal muscle
B) Smooth muscle
C) Cardiac muscle
D) Striated muscle
Answer: C) Cardiac muscle — involuntary, striated, branched, with intercalated discs
8. Which joint allows the "no" movement of the head?
A) Hinge
B) Ball & socket
C) Pivot
D) Gliding
Answer: C) Pivot joint — between Atlas (C1) and Axis (C2) vertebrae
9. Which muscle is the strongest for its size?
A) Gluteus maximus
B) Masseter
C) Deltoid
D) Quadriceps
Answer: B) Masseter (jaw muscle) — strongest for its size
10. The bone disorder characterized by decreased bone density is called:
A) Arthritis
B) Osteoporosis
C) Scoliosis
D) Rickets
Answer: B) Osteoporosis — porous bones; common in postmenopausal women
