1. The Plant Body — A Quick Tour

Teacher Says
Think of a plant as a building. The roots are the foundation (underground). The stem is the main pillar/ hallway. The leaves are the kitchen (where food is made). The flowers are the nursery (where babies/seeds are made).

A typical flowering plant has 2 main parts: the Root System (underground) and the Shoot System (above ground). Together they keep the plant alive!

Root System

Anchors the plant, absorbs water & minerals

Shoot System

Stem + Leaves + Flowers + Fruits — everything above ground

The Plant as a Factory
Roots = Raw material buyer (buys water & minerals from soil)
Stem = Conveyor belt & support structure (transports materials, holds everything up)
Leaves = Kitchen/food processing unit (makes food using sunlight)
Flowers = Marketing & sales (attracts pollinators, makes seeds for next generation)
Fruits = Packaging & shipping (protects and spreads seeds)

2. Root System — The Foundation

NCERT Class 6 — Getting to Know Plants
Roots are the underground part of the plant. They grow downward (toward gravity) and away from light. This is called positive geotropism and negative phototropism.
Functions of Roots
  • Anchoring — holds the plant firmly in the soil (prevents falling over in wind)
  • Absorption — absorbs water and minerals from the soil through root hairs (tiny hair-like projections)
  • Storage — some roots store food (carrot, radish, beetroot, sweet potato)
  • Conduction — conducts water & minerals upward to the stem
Two Types of Root Systems
FeatureTaprootFibrous Root
DescriptionOne main thick root going deep, with smaller side branchesMany thin roots spreading out like a bunch of hair
ExamplesMustard, Carrot, Radish, Neem, Mango, Rose, Sunflower, GramWheat, Rice, Maize, Grass, Sugarcane, Onion, Bamboo
DepthGoes deep into soil (can reach water far below)Spreads near the surface (covers wide area)
Which plants?Dicotyledons (dicots) — plants with 2 seed leavesMonocotyledons (monocots) — plants with 1 seed leaf
Taproot vs Fibrous Mnemonic
Taproot — Think "Tap" = one main tap (faucet) that goes deep. Plants: Dicots (2 leaves). "Mango has a TAProot"
Fibrous — Think "Fiber" = many thin threads like a mop. Plants: Monocots. "Wheat has FIBROUS roots like a FIBER mop"
Specialized Roots (Modifications)
  • Storage roots — store food (carrot = enlarged taproot, sweet potato = enlarged fibrous root)
  • Prop roots — grow from stem into soil for support (banyan tree — those hanging pillar-like roots)
  • Stilt roots — grow from lower stem, provide support in shallow water (sugarcane, maize)
  • Pneumatophores (Respiratory roots) — grow upward in swampy areas to get oxygen (mangroves like Sundarbans)
  • Parasitic roots (Haustoria) — penetrate host plant to suck nutrients (Cuscuta/Amarbel — the yellow twining parasite)
  • Nodulated roots — contain nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium) in root nodules (legumes — peas, beans, gram)
Root Hairs — The Tiny Heroes
Root hairs are tiny, thin extensions of root cells. They increase surface area for water absorption. A single plant can have billions of root hairs! They are so small you can barely see them — but without them, the plant would die of thirst.

3. Stem — The Main Pillar

NCERT Class 6 — Getting to Know Plants
The stem is the upward-growing part of the plant. It grows toward light (positive phototropism). Unlike roots, it has nodes and internodes.
Functions of Stem
  • Support — holds leaves, flowers, and fruits upright (so leaves get sunlight, flowers attract pollinators)
  • Transport — carries water & minerals up (from roots to leaves) and food down (from leaves to other parts)
  • Storage — stores food in some plants (potato = underground stem, sugarcane = stem stores sugar)
  • Propagation — some stems can grow into new plants (stem cuttings — rose, sugarcane, money plant)
Parts of a Stem
  • Node — the point where leaves and buds arise (like a knot on a bamboo stick)
  • Internode — the space between two nodes
  • Bud — a small swelling that can grow into a new shoot, leaf, or flower
  • Terminal (apical) bud — at the tip of stem, makes stem grow taller
  • Axillary (lateral) bud — in the angle between leaf and stem (axil), can grow into a branch
Types of Stems
  • Herbaceous stem — soft, green, flexible (grass, mint, tomato). No woody tissue
  • Woody stem — hard, brown, thick (tree trunks — mango, neem, banyan). Has bark and wood
  • Climbing stem — weak stem that climbs on support (money plant, grapevine, gourd)
  • Underground stem — grows underground, stores food, can grow into new plant (potato = tuber, ginger = rhizome, onion = bulb, grass = runner)
Stem Analogy — A Highway System
The stem is like a highway with two lanes:
Lane 1 (Xylem) — water & minerals moving UP from roots to leaves (like a water supply truck)
Lane 2 (Phloem) — food moving DOWN from leaves to roots & storage (like a food delivery truck)
The nodes are like highway exits where leaves (towns) are connected.

4. Leaf — The Food Factory

NCERT Class 6 — Getting to Know Plants
Leaves are the kitchen of the plant. They make food through photosynthesis. Without leaves, the plant would starve!
Parts of a Leaf
  • Lamina (leaf blade) — the flat, green, wide part of the leaf. This is where photosynthesis happens
  • Petiole (leaf stalk) — the thin stalk that connects the leaf to the stem (some leaves have no petiole — called sessile leaves)
  • Midrib — the thick central vein running down the middle of the leaf
  • Veins — thin branching lines on the leaf. They provide support and transport water & food
  • Margin — the edge of the leaf (can be smooth, serrated/toothed, or lobed)
  • Stomata — tiny pores (mostly on underside of leaf) for gas exchange. Each stoma is surrounded by two guard cells
Venation — The Pattern of Veins
TypePatternExamplesPlant Type
Reticulate venationVeins form a net-like pattern (branching irregularly, like a fishing net)Mango, Neem, Rose, Sunflower, Peepal, HibiscusDicots (taproot)
Parallel venationVeins run parallel to each other (like train tracks)Wheat, Rice, Maize, Grass, Sugarcane, Banana, BambooMonocots (fibrous root)
Easy Way to Remember
Look at the leaf veins to guess the root type! Net-like veins = taproot (dicot). Parallel veins = fibrous root (monocot). Example: If a mango leaf has net veins, it must have a taproot. Correct! If grass has parallel veins, it has fibrous roots. Correct!
Photosynthesis — How Leaves Make Food
  • Photosynthesis = "Photo" (light) + "Synthesis" (to make). Making food using sunlight!
  • Raw materials needed:
    • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — enters through stomata from air
    • Water (H₂O) — comes from roots through xylem
    • Sunlight — absorbed by chlorophyll (green pigment in leaves)
  • Products: Glucose (food — C₆H₁₂O₆) + Oxygen (O₂ — released into air)
  • Equation: CO₂ + H₂O →(sunlight, chlorophyll)→ C₆H₁₂O₆ + O₂
  • Chlorophyll — the green pigment in chloroplasts (tiny organelles inside leaf cells). It traps sunlight energy
  • Stomata — tiny pores (mostly on underside of leaf) through which CO₂ enters and O₂ & water vapor exit. Each stoma is controlled by guard cells (they open & close the pore)
Photosynthesis Mnemonic: "Plants Make Their Own Food Using COWS"
Carbon dioxide + Oxygen is released + Water + Sunlight = Food (Glucose)!
NCERT Class 7 — Nutrition in Plants
Autotrophs = plants that make their own food (using photosynthesis). They are called "producers" in the food chain. Heterotrophs = animals that depend on plants/other animals for food. Green plants are the only living things that can make their own food!
Leaf Modifications
  • Tendrils — leaves modified into thin, wiry coils for climbing (pea plant, cucumber)
  • Spines — leaves reduced to sharp points to reduce water loss & protect from animals (cactus, acacia)
  • Fleshy leaves — thick, juicy leaves that store water (aloe vera, agave)
  • Insectivorous leaves — modified to trap & digest insects (pitcher plant, Venus flytrap, sundew) — these grow in nitrogen-poor soil, so they get nitrogen from insects

5. Flower — The Reproductive Part

NCERT Class 6 — Getting to Know Plants
A flower is the reproductive organ of a plant. It's where seeds are made. Not all plants have flowers (non-flowering plants = ferns, mosses, gymnosperms like pine).
Parts of a Flower (4 Whorls)

Calyx (Sepals)

Green leaf-like parts at the bottom. Protect the bud before it opens.

Corolla (Petals)

Colorful, attractive parts. Attract pollinators (bees, butterflies).

Androecium (Stamens) — Male part

Each stamen has a filament (stalk) + anther (produces pollen — male gamete).

Gynoecium (Pistil) — Female part

Has Stigma (sticky top) + Style (tube) + Ovary (contains ovules — female gamete).

Flower Analogy — A Wedding Event
Sepals = Security guards (protect before event)
Petals = Decoration (attract guests)
Stamens (anther) = Groom's side (produces pollen = male guests)
Pistil (ovary) = Bride's side (contains ovules = female guests)
Pollination = The bride & groom meet (pollen reaches stigma)
Fertilization = The wedding (male & female gametes fuse) → seed = baby!
Pollination — Transfer of Pollen
  • Pollination = transfer of pollen from anther (male) to stigma (female)
  • Self-pollination — pollen from same flower (pea, tomato, wheat). Simpler but less genetic variety
  • Cross-pollination — pollen from one flower to another flower on a different plant (apple, mango, sunflower). Creates more variety. Agents: Wind (grass, wheat, pine — light, floaty pollen), Insects (bees, butterflies — flowers are colorful, smell nice, have nectar), Birds (hummingbirds, sunbirds), Water (aquatic plants)
Fertilization → Seed → Fruit
  • After pollination: Pollen tube grows down the style → reaches ovule → male gamete fuses with female gamete → Zygote formed
  • Ovule → develops into Seed
  • Ovary → develops into Fruit (the fruit protects the seeds and helps spread them)
  • Example: In a mango flower, after fertilization, the ovary becomes the mango fruit, and the ovule inside becomes the mango seed (the hard stone)
Flower Parts Mnemonic: "Some People See Flowers"
Sepals (Calyx) → Petals (Corolla) → Stamens (Androecium) → Flower center (Pistil/Gynoecium) — arranged from outer to inner!

6. Plant Tissues — What Plants Are Made Of

NCERT Class 9 — Tissues
Just like your body has different tissues (muscle, bone, blood), plants also have different tissues. But plant cells have cell walls — that's why plants stand upright even without bones!
Meristematic Tissues — "Growing Tissues"
  • Cells that keep dividing — found in growing regions
  • Apical meristem — at tips of roots & stems → plant grows taller/longer
  • Lateral meristem — in sides (cambium) → plant grows thicker (tree trunks widen)
  • Intercalary meristem — at base of leaves/nodes → grass grows back after cutting
Permanent Tissues — "Specialized Tissues"
TissueTypeFunctionFeaturesWhere Found
ParenchymaSimpleStorage of food, photosynthesis (if it has chlorophyll = chlorenchyma), buoyancy in water (air spaces = aerenchyma)Thin-walled, loosely packed, living cells with intercellular spacesAll plant parts — pith of stem, flesh of fruits, leaf mesophyll
CollenchymaSimpleProvides flexibility & mechanical support to growing stemsLiving cells with thickened corners (pectin & cellulose), no intercellular spacesBeneath epidermis of stem, leaf stalks (petioles), young stems — allows bending without breaking
SclerenchymaSimpleProvides strength & rigidity (makes plants hard)Thick lignified walls, dead cells, no protoplasm. Very strong! Cannot stretchSeed coats, nut shells (coconut husk), stem of jute/hemp, veins of leaf
XylemComplexConducts water & minerals UP from roots to leaves + mechanical supportMade of: Tracheids, Vessels (water-conducting), Xylem fibers (support), Xylem parenchyma (storage). Dead cells (except parenchyma)Throughout plant — from root hair → stem → leaf veins
PhloemComplexConducts food DOWN from leaves to all partsMade of: Sieve tubes (food transport — no nucleus!), Companion cells (help sieve tubes — have nucleus), Phloem fibers, Phloem parenchyma. Living cells (except fibers)Bark side (inner bark) of stem — all over plant
Xylem vs Phloem — The Two Highways
Xylem = One-way water pipe (water goes UP only). Like a drinking straw. Cells are dead — they're just hollow tubes.
Phloem = Two-way food pipe (food goes FROM leaves TO everywhere). Like a delivery truck. Cells are alive but the sieve tube cells lose their nucleus to make more space for food transport!
Xylem vs Phloem Mnemonic: "Xylem = Up (X = X-tra Up)"
Xylem transports water UP (remember: X = "X-tra Up"). Phloem transports food in Plants (P = "P-food"). Or: "Xylem = Water, Phloem = Food"

7. Transport & Nutrition in Plants

NCERT Class 7 — Transportation in Plants
How does water from the soil reach the top of a 100-meter tall tree? And how does food from leaves reach the roots? That's transport in plants!
Water Transport — Root → Stem → Leaf
  • Water enters through root hairs (by osmosis)
  • Moves through root cells → reaches xylem in root
  • Travels UP through xylem vessels (continuous hollow tubes)
  • How does water go up? Transpiration pull — water evaporates from leaves (through stomata), creating a suction force that pulls water up from roots. Like sucking on a straw!
  • Transpiration = loss of water vapor from leaves through stomata. It also cools the plant (like sweating)
Food Transport — Leaf → All Parts
  • Food (glucose/sucrose) made in leaves by photosynthesis
  • Moves through phloem sieve tubes to all parts of the plant
  • Can move both up and down — to growing tips (up) and to roots/storage (down)
  • This process is called Translocation
Other Types of Nutrition in Plants
  • Parasitic plants — suck nutrients from other plants. Example: Cuscuta (Amarbel) — yellow, leafless, twining stem with haustoria (sucking roots)
  • Insectivorous plants — catch and digest insects for nitrogen (since they grow in poor soil). Examples: Pitcher plant, Venus flytrap, Sundew, Bladderwort
  • Saprophytes — feed on dead decaying matter. Examples: Mushrooms, Fungi, Moulds
  • Symbiosis — two organisms live together for mutual benefit. Example: Lichens (algae + fungus). Algae makes food, fungus provides water & shelter. Another: Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules of legumes — bacteria fixes nitrogen, plant gives food
NCERT Class 7 — Nutrition in Plants
Quick summary of plant nutrition types:
Autotrophic = Make own food (green plants — photosynthesis)
Heterotrophic = Depend on others for food → Parasitic (Cuscuta), Insectivorous (Pitcher plant), Saprophytic (Mushroom), Symbiotic (Lichens, Rhizobium)

8. Reproduction in Plants

NCERT Class 7 & 10
Plants can make babies in two ways: Asexual (only one parent — exact copy) and Sexual (two parents — unique baby).
Asexual Reproduction — "Clone-making"
  • Vegetative propagation — new plant grows from a part of the parent (root, stem, leaf)
  • Stem cuttings — Rose, sugarcane, money plant (cut a stem, put in soil → grows new plant)
  • Tubers — Potato (underground stem with "eyes" = buds). Each eye can grow into a new plant
  • Runners — Grass, strawberry (horizontal stems above ground that root at nodes)
  • Rhizomes — Ginger, turmeric (underground stems that grow horizontally)
  • Bulbs — Onion, garlic (underground modified stem with fleshy leaves)
  • Root cuttings — Sweet potato, dahlia (adventitious buds on roots grow new plants)
  • Leaf cuttings — Bryophyllum (leaves have small buds at edges that fall off and grow into new plants!)
  • Budding — Yeast (a tiny bud grows on parent cell, then detaches)
  • Spore formation — Ferns, mosses, mushrooms (spores are tiny cells that grow into new plants in favorable conditions)
Sexual Reproduction — "Seed-making"
  • Happens through flowers (as explained in Section 5)
  • Process: Pollination (pollen reaches stigma) → Fertilization (male & female gametes fuse) → Zygote (baby cell) → Seed (baby + food + protection) → Fruit (protective cover)
  • Germination — when a seed gets water, air, and warmth, it sprouts. The radicle (tiny root) grows down, and the plumule (tiny shoot) grows up
  • Seed dispersal — seeds spread away from the parent plant to avoid overcrowding. Methods: Wind (dandelion, maple — light with wings/parachutes), Water (coconut — fibrous outer layer floats), Animals (mango, guava — fruit eaten, seed passes through; or burrs like Xanthium stick to fur), Explosion (balsam, pea — pods burst open and throw seeds)
Why Seed Dispersal Matters
If seeds all fall under the parent tree, they'd compete for water, sunlight, and minerals. Most would die. Dispersal helps seeds reach new places with space, sunlight, and fewer competitors. That's why coconuts can float across oceans and dandelion seeds fly for kilometers!

9. Practice MCQs

1. Which part of the plant absorbs water and minerals?
A) Leaf
B) Stem
C) Root
D) Flower
Answer: C) Root — root hairs absorb water & minerals from soil
2. Which type of root system does wheat have?
A) Taproot
B) Fibrous root
C) Prop root
D) Storage root
Answer: B) Fibrous root — wheat is a monocot with parallel venation and fibrous roots
3. The process of making food in plants is called:
A) Respiration
B) Photosynthesis
C) Transpiration
D) Germination
Answer: B) Photosynthesis — uses CO₂ + water + sunlight + chlorophyll to make glucose + O₂
4. The green pigment in leaves that traps sunlight is called:
A) Chloroplast
B) Chlorophyll
C) Xylem
D) Stomata
Answer: B) Chlorophyll — green pigment found in chloroplasts
5. Which part of the flower becomes the fruit?
A) Stigma
B) Ovary
C) Petal
D) Anther
Answer: B) Ovary — after fertilization, ovary develops into fruit, ovules become seeds
6. Which plant tissue conducts water upward?
A) Phloem
B) Xylem
C) Parenchyma
D) Collenchyma
Answer: B) Xylem — conducts water & minerals from roots upward (X = "X-tra Up")
7. Reticulate venation is found in which type of plants?
A) Monocots
B) Dicots
C) Both
D) Neither
Answer: B) Dicots — net-like veins, taproot system (e.g., mango, rose, sunflower)
8. Which of the following is NOT a method of seed dispersal?
A) Wind
B) Water
C) Photosynthesis
D) Animals
Answer: C) Photosynthesis is food-making — not seed dispersal. Others are: wind (dandelion), water (coconut), animals (mango), explosion (balsam)
9. The loss of water vapor from leaves is called:
A) Transportation
B) Transpiration
C) Translocation
D) Transformation
Answer: B) Transpiration — water evaporates through stomata; creates suction pull for water transport
10. Which of these is an insectivorous plant?
A) Cactus
B) Pitcher plant
C) Mango
D) Cuscuta
Answer: B) Pitcher plant — catches insects for nitrogen. Cuscuta is parasitic, cactus has spines, mango is normal
11. The male part of a flower (stamen) consists of:
A) Stigma + Style
B) Anther + Filament
C) Ovary + Ovules
D) Petals + Sepals
Answer: B) Anther (produces pollen) + Filament (stalk) = Stamen (male reproductive part)
12. Potato is an example of which modified stem?
A) Rhizome
B) Tuber
C) Bulb
D) Runner
Answer: B) Tuber — underground stem with "eyes" (buds). Ginger = rhizome, Onion = bulb, Grass = runner
