1. Basics of Matter – What is Everything Made Of?

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space (volume). Everything around you — air, water, your book, the food you eat — is matter. Ancient Indian philosophers (like Maharishi Kanada, ~600 BCE) proposed that matter is made of tiny indivisible particles called paramanu. John Dalton (1808) later gave the Atomic Theory — all matter is made of atoms.

Three States of Matter

Analogy: Particles in solids are like students in a classroom — tightly packed, can only vibrate. In liquids, like students in a hallway — can move around but stay close. In gases, like students in a playground — free to move anywhere!

VolumeFixedFixedNo fixed volume (expands to fill)
CompressibilityVery low (negligible)Low (slightly compressible)High (easily compressed)
DensityHighModerateVery low
Arrangement of particlesTightly packed, orderlyLoosely packed, disorderlyFar apart, random motion
Intermolecular forcesVery strongModerateVery weak
Kinetic energyLow (vibrational only)MediumVery high
ExamplesIron, wood, ice, stoneWater, milk, oil, juiceAir, oxygen, steam, CO₂
Interconversion of States

Matter can change from one state to another by changing temperature or pressure.

ProcessChangeDirection of HeatExample
Melting (Fusion)Solid → LiquidAbsorbs heatIce melts to water at 0°C
Freezing (Solidification)Liquid → SolidReleases heatWater freezes to ice at 0°C
Boiling (Vaporization)Liquid → GasAbsorbs heatWater boils to steam at 100°C
EvaporationLiquid → Gas (at surface)Absorbs heatPuddles drying, clothes drying

Effect of Temperature & Pressure
  • Temperature ↑ → particles gain kinetic energy → overcome interparticle forces → solid melts → liquid boils → gas.
  • Pressure ↑ → particles are forced closer → gas condenses to liquid, liquid freezes to solid. Example: LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas) is stored under high pressure as liquid, becomes gas when released.
  • Dry ice is solid CO₂ that sublimes directly to gas at -78°C. Used in cooling and stage effects.
Diffusion – Particles Moving Around

Diffusion is the spontaneous mixing of particles of two substances. It happens due to the constant motion of particles and the empty spaces between them.

Gas-LiquidO₂ and CO₂ dissolve in water (fish breathe dissolved oxygen)Gas particles diffuse into liquid surface
Liquid-LiquidInk drop in water — colour spreads evenlyLiquid particles intermingle slowly

Teacher Says:
Diffusion is faster in gases (because particles are far apart), slower in liquids, and extremely slow in solids. Why? Think about the hallway vs playground analogy — more space = easier to move!
Matter is Composed of Particles — Evidence
  • Ancient Indian view: Maharishi Kanada (6th century BCE) proposed paramanu (atoms) as indestructible particles of matter. He also proposed dvyanuka (molecules).
  • Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808): All matter is made of atoms. Atoms are indivisible. Atoms of same element are identical.
  • Experimental evidence: When KMnO₄ crystals are dissolved in water, a few crystals colour a large volume — proving particles are tiny and have spaces between them.
Mnemonic:
State Change Memory Trick: "S-L-G = Melting & Evaporation (heat goes IN, temperature rises). G-L-S = Condensation & Freezing (heat comes OUT, temperature drops)." Remember: "S-L-G = Shoes Let Go (gain energy)".

2. Physical & Chemical Changes

Physical Change

Analogy: Physical change is like rearranging furniture in a room — same room (same substance), different layout (different form).

  • Definition: A change where the chemical composition of the substance remains the same. Only physical properties (shape, size, state, colour) change.
  • Characteristics: Usually reversible, no new substance formed, no change in mass, minor energy change.
  • Examples:
    — Melting of ice (solid water → liquid water — same H₂O)
    — Boiling/evaporation of water (liquid → vapour — still H₂O)
    — Dissolving sugar in water (sugar crystals disappear but sugar is still sugar)
    — Cutting paper, cutting cloth, breaking glass, chopping wood
    — Crushing a can, folding a paper, mixing salt and pepper
    — Magnetizing an iron nail
Chemical Change

Analogy: Chemical change is like baking a cake — once baked, you cannot get back the raw eggs and flour separately. Something entirely new is formed!

  • Definition: A change where new substances with different chemical compositions are formed. Old bonds break, new bonds form.
  • Characteristics: Usually irreversible, new substance formed, energy change (heat/light), mass remains same (law of conservation).
  • Examples:
    — Rusting of iron (Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃·xH₂O — reddish-brown rust)
    — Burning of wood/paper (produces ash, CO₂, H₂O — cannot get wood back)
    — Cooking food (raw egg → boiled egg — irreversible)
    — Digestion of food (enzymes break down food into new substances)
    — Photosynthesis (CO₂ + H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + O₂, with sunlight)
    — Ripening of fruits, souring of milk, fermentation
    — Burning of LPG/CNG — combustion reaction

Rusting — Why Iron Rusts
  • Rusting: Iron reacts with oxygen and moisture to form hydrated iron(III) oxide — Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust). Condition: Oxygen + Water both needed.
  • 4Fe + 3O₂ + xH₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust is reddish-brown, flaky, porous — allows more rusting).
  • Corrosion: General term for deterioration of metals by reaction with environment. Rusting is corrosion of iron. Aluminium corrodes to form Al₂O₃ (protective layer — does not flake). Silver tarnishes (black Ag₂S). Copper turns green (CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂ — patina).
  • Prevention of rusting: Painting, oiling/greasing, galvanizing (coating with zinc), chrome plating, alloying (stainless steel = Fe + Cr + Ni).
Crystallization

Crystallization is a physical change where a solid forms from a solution as crystals. It is a purification technique — pure crystals of a substance are obtained from its impure solution.

  • Process: Make a saturated solution → filter → cool slowly → crystals form.
  • Example: Obtaining pure copper sulphate (CuSO₄·5H₂O) blue crystals from an impure sample. Also, alum (fitkari) crystals.
  • Crystallization is different from evaporation — evaporation removes water completely; crystallization allows slow formation of pure crystals, leaving impurities in solution.
Teacher Says:
Remember this trick — "Physical = Same stuff, different form. Chemical = Different stuff altogether!" Cutting an apple is physical. The apple turning brown after some time is chemical (oxidation).
Mnemonic:
Mnemonic — Signs of Chemical Change: "C-G-H-P-S-S" = Colour change, Gas evolution, Heat/Light, Precipitate, Smell, Sound. Think "Cats Generally Have Purple Socks Sometimes".
Real-Life Examples Explained
  • Why does iron rust? Iron reacts with atmospheric oxygen in the presence of water/moisture. Fe → Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ → hydrated Fe₂O₃ (rust). This is a chemical change — new substance forms and the iron weakens. Rust is porous, so it does not protect the inner metal (unlike aluminium oxide which forms a protective layer).
  • Burning vs Evaporation: Burning is chemical (wood → ash + CO₂ + H₂O — irreversible). Evaporation is physical (water → water vapour — reversible by condensation).
  • Crystallization: When a hot saturated solution of copper sulphate is cooled, blue CuSO₄·5H₂O crystals form. This is a physical change — the copper sulphate is still copper sulphate, just in crystalline form. Impurities remain in the solution.

3. Separation of Substances — 15 Techniques

We separate mixtures because individual components have different uses. Each technique uses a difference in physical properties (size, density, boiling point, solubility, magnetic property, etc.)

1. Handpicking
  • Principle: Difference in colour, shape, or size of components — visible to the naked eye.
  • Method: Simply pick out the unwanted component by hand.
  • Examples: Removing stones from rice/wheat, removing rotten potatoes/chillies, picking out coloured lentils from plain ones.
  • Diagram: A pile of rice with a few stones scattered — a hand reaching to pick a stone out.
2. Threshing
  • Principle: Difference in bonding strength between grain and stalk.
  • Method: Beating the harvested crop to separate grains from stalks. Traditionally done by bullocks trampling or manual beating; modern threshing machines.
  • Examples: Separating wheat/rice grains from their stalks.
  • Diagram: A bundle of wheat stalks being beaten against a wooden block — grains falling off.
3. Winnowing
  • Principle: Difference in weight/density — lighter husk particles are carried away by wind, heavier grains fall down.
  • Method: Drop the mixture from a height in a gentle breeze/air stream. Heavier grains fall straight, lighter husk blows away.
  • Examples: Separating husk from grain after threshing; separating lighter chaff from heavier rice.
  • Diagram: A farmer holding a winnowing basket (soup) at height, tossing the mixture — husk blows sideways, grains fall in a pile below.
4. Sieving
  • Principle: Difference in particle size. A sieve has holes of a specific size — smaller particles pass through, larger particles are retained.
  • Method: Pour mixture onto a sieve and shake. Fine particles pass through the holes; larger ones stay on top.
  • Examples: Removing stones/bran from sand at construction site; separating flour from impurities (bran) in kitchen; separating gravel from sand.
  • Diagram: A circular sieve with wooden rim — sand falling through holes, pebbles retained on the mesh.
5. Sedimentation & Decantation
  • Principle: Difference in density and solubility. Heavier insoluble solids settle at the bottom (sedimentation); the clear liquid above is poured out (decantation).
  • Method: Leave the mixture undisturbed → heavy particles settle as sediment → carefully pour out the supernatant liquid.
  • Examples: Cleaning muddy water (mud settles at bottom, pour clear water); settling of rice wash water; separating sand from water.
  • Diagram: A beaker with muddy water — mud layer at bottom (sediment), clear water above, being poured into another beaker.
6. Filtration
  • Principle: Difference in particle size relative to filter medium pores. Insoluble solid particles are too large to pass through filter paper pores; liquid (filtrate) passes through.
  • Method: Fold filter paper, place in funnel, pour mixture. Solid residue remains on filter paper; clear filtrate collects below.
  • Key terms: Residue = the solid left on filter paper. Filtrate = the clear liquid that passes through.
  • Examples: Making tea (strain out tea leaves); water purification at home (candle filter); separating chalk powder from water; separating sand from salt solution.
  • Diagram: A conical flask with a funnel on top — filter paper cone inside funnel — mixture being poured — solid (residue) on paper, clear filtrate in flask below.
7. Evaporation
  • Principle: Difference in volatility/boiling point. The liquid (solvent) evaporates on heating, leaving behind the dissolved solid (solute).
  • Method: Heat the solution in a shallow dish. Liquid turns to vapour and escapes; solid remains in the dish.
  • Examples: Obtaining common salt from sea water (sea water in salt pans → sun evaporates water → salt left behind); obtaining sugar from sugar cane juice.
  • Diagram: A shallow pan over a flame — water boiling and evaporating — salt crystals left in the pan.
8. Condensation
  • Principle: Gas/vapour cools and changes to liquid on a cold surface.
  • Method: Vapour is collected and cooled to convert back to liquid.
  • Examples: Water droplets on a cold glass of water (atmospheric moisture condenses); collecting distilled water from steam; cloud formation (water vapour in air → tiny water droplets).
  • Diagram: A boiling flask with steam going through a cold tube — water droplets forming on the cold surface and dripping into a collection beaker.
9. Distillation
  • Principle: Difference in boiling points. The liquid with lower boiling point evaporates first, then condenses back to liquid — separating it from dissolved solids or other liquids.
  • Method: Solution is heated in a distillation flask → vapour rises → passes through condenser (cold water circulation) → condenses → collected as pure liquid (distillate).
  • Examples: Obtaining pure (distilled) water from salt water/sea water; separating acetone and water; purifying alcohol; obtaining essential oils.
  • Diagram: Distillation flask on a heat source — thermometer at the top — Liebig condenser (cold water jacket) — distillate collecting in a beaker. Salt remains in the flask.
10. Fractional Distillation
  • Principle: Difference in boiling points of miscible liquids (close BPs). A fractionating column provides multiple condensation-evaporation cycles, separating the mixture into fractions.
  • Method: Heat in a fractionating column — vapours of lower BP rise higher, condense at different levels, and are collected separately.
  • Examples:
    — Petroleum refining: Crude oil heated → separated into petrol (gasoline), kerosene, diesel, lubricating oil, bitumen, etc. (different boiling ranges).
    — Air separation: Liquid air is fractionally distilled to get O₂ (b.p. -183°C), N₂ (b.p. -196°C), Ar, etc.
  • Diagram: A tall fractionating column with trays — crude oil heated at bottom — different fractions collected at different heights (top = gases/petrol, bottom = asphalt/bitumen).
11. Separating Funnel
  • Principle: Difference in density of immiscible liquids (liquids that do not mix). The denser liquid settles at the bottom, the lighter floats on top.
  • Method: Pour mixture into a separating funnel → let it settle → open the stopcock → drain the lower layer first → collect the upper layer separately.
  • Examples: Separating oil and water; separating kerosene and water; separating petrol and water; separating mercury and water.
  • Diagram: A pear-shaped glass funnel with a stopcock at the bottom — oil layer (top, yellow) and water layer (bottom, clear) — water being drained out.
12. Chromatography
  • Principle: Difference in solubility and adsorption rates of different components in a mixture.
  • Analogy: Like a race! Different pigments run at different speeds on the paper (stationary phase) because they have different solubilities in the solvent (mobile phase). So they separate out.
  • Method: A spot of the mixture is placed on chromatography paper (or filter paper). The paper is dipped in a solvent. The solvent rises by capillary action, carrying the components at different speeds — forming separate spots at different heights.
  • Rf value: (Distance moved by component) ÷ (Distance moved by solvent) — unique for each substance in a given solvent.
  • Examples:
    — Separating different coloured pigments in ink (black ink = mixture of many colours)
    — Separating chlorophyll pigments (chlorophyll-a, chlorophyll-b, xanthophyll, carotene)
    — Forensic analysis of inks, food colourings (detecting adulteration)
    — Blood alcohol testing, drug testing
  • Diagram: A glass jar with solvent at bottom — a strip of filter paper hanging — a spot of ink near the bottom — after rising, multiple coloured spots appear at different heights on the paper.
13. Centrifugation
  • Principle: Difference in density. When a mixture is spun rapidly (centrifugal force), denser particles move outward and settle at the bottom; lighter particles stay at the top.
  • Method: The mixture is placed in tubes inside a centrifuge machine → spun at high speed → components separate by density.
  • Examples:
    — Separating cream from milk (lighter cream rises to top when milk is churned/centrifuged)
    — Separating blood components (RBCs settle at bottom, plasma at top)
    — Urine analysis in medical labs
    — Drying salad leaves in a salad spinner
    — Washing machines — spin cycle removes water from clothes
  • Diagram: A centrifuge machine with tubes arranged radially — tubes before spinning (mixed) and after spinning (separated layers).
14. Magnetic Separation
  • Principle: If one component is magnetic and the other is not. A magnet attracts the magnetic component.
  • Method: Pass a magnet over the mixture. The magnetic material sticks to the magnet; the non-magnetic material is left behind.
  • Examples:
    — Separating iron filings from sand/sulphur powder
    — Recycling industry — removing iron/steel from waste
    — Separating iron ore from waste rock in mining
    — Removing iron nails/screws from construction waste
  • Diagram: A mixture of iron filings and sand — a bar magnet being passed over — iron filings sticking to the magnet, sand left behind.
15. Sublimation
  • Principle: One component sublimes (changes directly from solid to vapour on heating) while the other does not. The vapour is then cooled (deposition) to get pure solid back.
  • Method: Heat the mixture in a china dish covered with an inverted funnel. Cotton plug in funnel stem. Sublimable substance turns to vapour → deposits on cool inner surface of funnel as pure crystals.
  • Examples:
    — Camphor + sand mixture (camphor sublimes, collects as crystals; sand remains)
    — Iodine + common salt (iodine sublimes, salt remains)
    — Ammonium chloride + salt (ammonium chloride sublimes)
    — Naphthalene (mothballs) from mixtures
  • Diagram: A china dish on a tripod stand — mixture in dish — inverted funnel over it with cotton plug at neck — heating — sublimed substance depositing on cool funnel walls.

Solutions — Key Terms & Types

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. Solute = the substance that dissolves (minor component). Solvent = the substance that does the dissolving (major component).

Aqueous solution: A solution where water is the solvent. Most common in nature and chemistry!

Concentration of a Solution

Concentration tells us how much solute is present in a given amount of solution.

  • Mass by mass percentage (% w/w): (Mass of solute ÷ Mass of solution) × 100
    Example: 10g salt in 100g solution = 10% w/w salt solution.
  • Mass by volume percentage (% w/v): (Mass of solute in g ÷ Volume of solution in mL) × 100
    Example: 5g sugar in 100mL solution = 5% w/v sugar solution.

Solubility — Factors Affecting

Solubility = Maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in 100g of solvent at a given temperature to form a saturated solution.

  • Temperature:
    — Solids in liquids: Solubility increases with temperature (e.g., more sugar dissolves in hot tea than cold).
    — Gases in liquids: Solubility decreases with temperature (e.g., cold drinks lose fizz when warm — CO₂ escapes).
  • Pressure:
    — Gases in liquids: Solubility increases with pressure (Henry's Law). Example: Cold drinks are bottled under high pressure to dissolve more CO₂; scuba divers get decompression sickness ("the bends") due to N₂ bubbles forming when pressure reduces.
  • Nature of solute and solvent: "Like dissolves like" — polar solutes (salt, sugar) dissolve in polar solvents (water); non-polar solutes (oil, grease) dissolve in non-polar solvents (petrol, kerosene).

Solubility curve: A graph showing how solubility changes with temperature. Different substances have different curves — useful for separating mixtures by crystallization.

Teacher Says:
"Like dissolves like" — think of it like friends: Polar (water) friends mix with polar friends, non-polar (oil) friends stick with non-polar friends. That's why oil and water never become friends — they form separate layers!

26. In a salt solution, what is the solvent?
1. Salt
2. Water
3. Both salt and water
4. Neither
Answer: 2. Water (the substance present in larger amount that dissolves the solute)
27. The Rf value in chromatography is calculated as:
1. Distance by solute ÷ Distance by solvent
2. Distance by solvent ÷ Distance by solute
3. Distance by solute × Distance by solvent
4. Height of paper ÷ Width of paper
Answer: 1. Distance moved by solute ÷ Distance moved by solvent
28. Burning of LPG in a gas stove is a:
1. Physical change
2. Chemical change
3. Reversible change
4. Both physical and chemical
Answer: 2. Chemical change (LPG + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + heat — new substances formed)
29. Which of these mixtures can be separated by winnowing?
1. Salt and water
2. Rice and stones
3. Husk and grain
4. Ink and water
Answer: 3. Husk and grain (lighter husk blown away by wind, heavier grain falls down)
30. The intermolecular force of attraction is strongest in:
1. Solids
2. Liquids
3. Gases
4. Same in all states
Answer: 1. Solids (particles are tightly packed with strong forces holding them)
