1. Physical Properties of Metals & Non-Metals

Metals and non-metals differ in almost every physical property. Think of metals as tough guys - they can be hammered into shape, drawn into wires, and ring like a bell. Non-metals are like old crackers - brittle and break easily.

Analogy:
Metal is like a tough guy - can be hammered into shape (malleable), drawn into wires (ductile), and rings like a bell (sonorous). Non-metal is like an old cracker - brittle, breaks on hammering, and makes no sound when struck.

Exceptions to Remember
  • Mercury (Hg) - only liquid metal at room temperature.
  • Gallium (Ga) & Cesium (Cs) - melt in your palm (low m.p.).
  • Sodium (Na) & Potassium (K) - soft metals, cut with a knife.
  • Graphite (C) - non-metal that conducts electricity (used in electrodes).
  • Diamond (C) - hardest natural substance (non-metal).
  • Iodine (I₂) - shiny non-metal (lustrous).
Key Insight:
Malleability and ductility are unique to metals. Gold (Au) is the most ductile metal - 1 gram can be drawn into a wire 2 km long! Aluminium foil is the most common example of malleability.

2. Chemical Properties of Metals

Reaction with Oxygen (Oxidation)

Metal + Oxygen → Metal Oxide (basic oxide). Most metal oxides are basic in nature.

  • Sodium (Na) - Burns with bright yellow flame. 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O (sodium oxide). Stored under kerosene.
  • Magnesium (Mg) - Burns with dazzling white light. 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (used in fireworks, photoflash).
  • Iron (Fe) - Rusts slowly. 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ (iron oxide / rust).
  • Copper (Cu) - Blackens on heating. 2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO (black copper oxide).
  • Aluminium (Al) - Forms protective oxide layer. 4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃ (prevents further corrosion).
  • Zinc (Zn) - Burns with greenish flame. 2Zn + O₂ → 2ZnO.
Reaction with Water

Metal + Water → Metal Hydroxide / Oxide + H₂ gas. Reactivity determines violence of reaction.

MetalReaction with WaterEquation
Na, KReact violently even with cold water2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑
CaLess violent, H₂ bubbles observedCa + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂↑
MgReacts with hot water, not coldMg + 2H₂O → Mg(OH)₂ + H₂↑
Al, FeReacts with steam only2Al + 3H₂O → Al₂O₃ + 3H₂↑ (steam)
ZnReacts with steamZn + H₂O → ZnO + H₂↑ (steam)
Cu, Ag, AuNo reaction with water at allNo reaction
Reaction with Acids

Metal + Dilute Acid → Salt + Hydrogen gas ↑. Only metals above hydrogen in reactivity series react.

  • Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑ (most common lab preparation of H₂)
  • Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂↑ (very fast reaction)
  • Fe + 2HCl → FeCl₂ + H₂↑ (moderate speed)
  • Cu, Ag, Au - do not react with dilute acids (below H in series)

Test for H₂ gas: Bring a burning splinter - it burns with a pop sound.

Reaction with Bases

Some metals (amphoteric) react with strong bases to produce H₂ gas.

  • Aluminium: 2Al + 2NaOH + 2H₂O → 2NaAlO₂ (sodium aluminate) + 3H₂↑
  • Zinc: Zn + 2NaOH → Na₂ZnO₂ (sodium zincate) + H₂↑
  • Other metals like Fe, Cu, Ag do NOT react with bases.
Displacement Reactions

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

  • Fe + CuSO₄ (blue) → FeSO₄ (green) + Cu↓ - Iron nail becomes brown, blue solution turns green.
  • Zn + CuSO₄ (blue) → ZnSO₄ (colourless) + Cu↓ - Blue colour fades.
  • Cu + 2AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag↓ - Silver tree experiment.
Analogy:
Displacement is like a bully in a queue - the stronger (more reactive) metal pushes the weaker one out and takes its place!
Fact:
Aqua regia (1 part HNO₃ : 3 parts HCl) can dissolve even noble metals like Gold (Au) and Platinum (Pt). It was used by Hans Christian Ørsted to purify gold.

3. Reactivity Series of Metals

The reactivity series (also called activity series) arranges metals in decreasing order of reactivity. The most reactive metal is at the top, the least reactive at the bottom.

Analogy:
Reactivity series is like a popularity ranking - the most reactive (K, Na) are at the top, the least reactive (Au, Ag) are at the bottom. Top ones are "social butterflies" who react with everything!

Mnemonic - Reactivity Series:
"King Peter Can Manage A Zinc Island So Cheaply; Copper, Silver, Gold Are Left" - K, Ca(Ca), Mg(Ma), Al, Zn(A), Zn, Fe(Iron), Sn(Island), Pb(So), Cu(Copper), Ag(Silver), Au(Are Left). Or simpler: "KNaCa MgAl ZnFe SnPb H CuAgAu" - just remember the sequence!
What Reactivity Series Tells Us
  • Displacement: A metal higher in series displaces a lower metal from its salt solution.
  • Reaction with Water: Top metals (K, Na, Ca) react with cold water; Mg with hot water; Al, Zn, Fe with steam; lower ones no reaction.
  • Reaction with Acids: Metals above H in series react with dilute acids to produce H₂ gas. Metals below H (Cu, Ag, Au) do NOT react.
  • Extraction Method: Top metals need electrolytic reduction; middle ones use carbon reduction; bottom ones exist free in nature.
  • Corrosion: Top metals are highly reactive → corrode easily. Bottom metals are noble → resist corrosion.
Key Insight:
Potassium (K), Sodium (Na), Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), and Aluminium (Al) - these 5 highly reactive metals are never found in free state in nature. They are always extracted by electrolytic reduction.

4. Occurrence & Extraction of Metals

Minerals vs Ores
  • Mineral: Any naturally occurring substance from which a metal can be extracted (profitably or not).
  • Ore: A mineral from which a metal can be extracted profitably and conveniently.
  • Gangue: Impurities (sand, mud, rock) present in the ore that need to be removed.
Steps in Extraction of Metals

Step 1: Enrichment of Ore (Concentration)

Removing gangue from ore to increase concentration of desired metal.

MethodUsed ForPrinciple
HandpickingLarge pieces of gangue visibleManual separation
Hydraulic WashingIron ores (haematite)Different densities - lighter gangue washed away
Froth FloatationSulphide ores (ZnS, PbS, Cu₂S)Ore particles attach to froth; gangue sinks
Magnetic SeparationIron ores (magnetite)Magnetic ore separated from non-magnetic gangue

Step 2: Conversion to Oxide (Roasting / Calcination)

ProcessUsed ForWhat HappensExample
RoastingSulphide oresHeated in excess air - sulphide converts to oxide + SO₂2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂↑
CalcinationCarbonate oresHeated in limited/absence of air - carbonate decomposes to oxide + CO₂ZnCO₃ → ZnO + CO₂↑ (heat)

Step 3: Reduction to Metal (Smelting)

  • Carbon Reduction (Smelting): For moderately reactive metals (Zn, Fe, Cu, Pb). Metal oxide + Carbon → Metal + CO/CO₂.
  • Electrolytic Reduction: For highly reactive metals (Na, K, Ca, Mg, Al). Molten metal ore is electrolysed.

Extraction of Iron from Haematite (Blast Furnace)

  • Raw Materials: Haematite (Fe₂O₃), Coke (C), Limestone (CaCO₃), Hot air.
  • Reactions:
C + O₂ → CO₂ + Heat (coke burns)
CO₂ + C → 2CO (carbon monoxide formed)
Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂ (reduction of iron ore)
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (limestone decomposes)
CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (slag formation - removes impurities)
  • Products: Molten iron collected at bottom; slag (CaSiO₃) floats on top.

Step 4: Refining (Purification)

  • Electrolytic Refining: Impure metal is made anode, pure metal is cathode. Metal ions from anode dissolve and deposit on cathode.
  • Used for: Cu, Zn, Ag, Au, Al.
Remember:
Roasting = for sulphide ores + excess air. Calcination = for carbonate ores + limited air. Trick: Roasting has 'O' for Oxygen (excess). Calcination has 'C' for Carbonate.
Teacher's Tip:
The extraction method depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series. Top (K-Ca-Mg-Al) → Electrolysis. Middle (Zn-Cu-Pb) → Carbon reduction. Bottom (Ag-Au) → Found free, just need physical separation!

5. Corrosion of Metals

Corrosion is the gradual destruction of metals by chemical reaction with their environment (air, water, acids, etc.).

Rusting of Iron
  • What is rust? Hydrated iron(III) oxide - Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (reddish-brown flaky substance).
  • Conditions needed: Both oxygen (air) and water (moisture) are required. In dry air or boiled water (no O₂), iron does NOT rust.
4Fe + 3O₂ + 2xH₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust)
Prevention of Rusting
Analogy:
Corrosion prevention is like wearing a raincoat - painting/oiling is like a raincoat that keeps water away from the metal. Galvanizing is like getting a security guard (zinc) who gets attacked instead of you!
MethodHow it WorksExample
PaintingCreates barrier between metal and air/waterIron gates, bridges, vehicles
Oiling / GreasingOil film prevents contact with moistureTools, machine parts, bicycle chain
GalvanizingCoating with Zn (zinc is more reactive, corrodes first)Iron sheets, buckets, pipes (GI pipes)
Chrome PlatingElectroplating with chromiumCar bumpers, taps, kitchenware
AlloyingMixing with other metals to prevent corrosionStainless steel (Fe + Cr + Ni)
Sacrificial ProtectionAttaching a more reactive metal (Zn, Mg) to the ironUnderground pipelines, ship hulls
TinningCoating with tinFood cans (tin-plated steel)
Corrosion of Other Metals
  • Silver tarnishing: Ag + H₂S → Ag₂S (black) - Silver reacts with H₂S in air.
  • Copper patina: Cu + CO₂ + H₂O + O₂ → CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂ (green) - Statue of Liberty is green due to this.
  • Aluminium: Forms protective Al₂O₃ layer (does NOT rust further - self-protecting).
Fact:
The Statue of Liberty was originally copper-coloured (brownish-red). Over time, it developed a green patina (basic copper carbonate) which now protects it from further corrosion!

6. Alloys

Alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal with a non-metal. Alloys are created to improve properties of the base metal.

Why Make Alloys?
  • Increase strength - Pure metals are often soft (e.g., pure gold is too soft for jewellery).
  • Prevent corrosion - Stainless steel resists rusting unlike pure iron.
  • Change colour - Brass (yellow) vs Cu (reddish-brown).
  • Lower melting point - Solder (Pb+Sn) melts at lower temp than either Pb or Sn alone. Used for welding.
  • Increase hardness - Steel (Fe+C) is harder than pure iron.
Teacher's Tip:
Amalgam is the special name for an alloy of mercury (Hg) with another metal. Used in dental fillings (Ag-Sn-Hg amalgam). Mercury is liquid, so amalgams are paste-like before setting.

Fact:
22-carat gold is an alloy - 22 parts gold + 2 parts copper/silver. Pure 24-carat gold is too soft for jewellery. The other metals add strength and change the colour (white gold uses palladium).
Mnemonic - Alloys:
"Brass = Cu + Zn" - Br(ass) has Z(inc) in it. "Bronze = Cu + Sn" - Think "Bronze Statues" (Sn sounds like 'statue'). "Solder = Pb + Sn" - Solder is soft like Lead (Pb) and Tin (Sn).

Thermite Reaction:
The thermite reaction (2Al + Fe₂O₃ → 2Fe + Al₂O₃) produces enormous heat (~3000°C). It is used for welding railway tracks and joining iron parts because aluminium is more reactive than iron and displaces it.

8. Non-Metals in Daily Life

Non-metals are just as important as metals in our daily life! Here is how each major non-metal is used.

Non-MetalSymbolPhysical StateKey Uses

Key Applications Explained
  • Oxygen: 21% of air. Essential for respiration. Used in hospitals (O₂ cylinders), steel industry, and welding.
  • Nitrogen: 78% of air. Used in Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃) for making fertilizers. Food packets filled with N₂ to prevent spoilage.
  • Chlorine: Used to purify drinking water. Also bleaches coloured substances - removes colour by oxidation.
  • Carbon allotropes: Diamond (hardest, used in drilling/cutting), Graphite (soft, lubricant, pencil lead - conducts electricity!), Fullerene (C₆₀ - football-shaped).
  • Sulphur: Used in vulcanization of rubber (makes rubber hard and elastic). Contact process: S → SO₂ → SO₃ → H₂SO₄.
  • Iodine: Tincture of iodine (2-3% I₂ in alcohol-water) is applied on wounds as antiseptic. Goitre (swollen thyroid) is due to iodine deficiency.
  • Phosphorus: White P is highly reactive (burns in air), stored in water. Red P is safer, used on matchbox striking surface.
Mnemonic - Air Composition:
"NO ARG" - N₂ (78%) + O₂ (21%) + Ar (0.93%) + Rest (CO₂, Ne, He, etc.). Or: "78-21-1" - Nitrogen 78%, Oxygen 21%, Others 1%.
Did You Know?
Diamond and Graphite are both pure carbon but have different properties due to different arrangements of carbon atoms (allotropes). Diamond is the hardest natural substance; Graphite is one of the softest and conducts electricity!

10. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Reactivity Series
K Na Ca Mg Al Zn Fe Sn Pb H Cu Ag Au:
"King Naughty Cat Made A Zoo Full of Snakes, Lions & Pigs. Here Comes A Great Ass!" - K(ing) Na(ughty) C(at) M(ade) A(l) Z(oo) F(ull) o(f) S(nakes) L(ions) & P(igs). H(ere) C(omes) A(great) G(reat) A(ss)... okay, last part. Better: "KNaCa MgAl ZnFe SnPb H CuAgAu" - just cram the sequence!
Reaction with Water Pattern
K, Na, Ca vs Mg vs Al, Zn, Fe vs Cu, Ag, Au:
"KNaCa = Cold water" (violent). "Mg = Hot water". "AlZnFe = Steam only". "CuAgAu = No reaction". Remember: "Cold, Hot, Steam, None" - decreasing reactivity.
Extraction Method by Reactivity
Electrolysis vs Carbon vs Free:
"Top = Electrolysis (K-Al)". "Middle = Carbon reduction (Zn-Pb)". "Bottom = Free (Cu-Au)". Trick: "TEC MFB" - Top = Electrolysis, Middle = Carbon, Bottom = Free.
Precious Metals (Least Reactive)
Noble Metals - Cu, Ag, Au:
"CAG" - Copper, silver, Gold. Or "Clean Air Gas" - these metals don't react with air (oxidize). Also called noble metals - they resist corrosion.
Alloys and Their Composition
Brass vs Bronze:
"Brass" = Cu + Zinc - Br(Z)ass. "Bronze" = Cu + Sn (Tin) - Bronze Statues (S). "Stainless" = Fe + Chromium + Nickel - sounds like "CrNi" (crispy).
Conditions for Rusting
Rust needs two things:
"O + W = Rust" - Oxygen + Water. Without either one, iron does NOT rust. Test: Boiled water (no O₂) = no rust. Anhydrous CaCl₂ (absorbs moisture) = no rust.

21. Which non-metal is used in the manufacture of sulphuric acid?
A) Carbon
B) Sulphur
C) Phosphorus
D) Nitrogen
Answer: B) Sulphur (Contact process)
22. Which gas is used in food packaging to prevent spoilage?
A) Oxygen
B) Nitrogen
C) Chlorine
D) Hydrogen
Answer: B) Nitrogen (creates oxygen-free environment)
23. Tincture of iodine is used as:
A) Fuel
B) Fertilizer
C) Antiseptic
D) Bleaching agent
Answer: C) Antiseptic (applied on wounds)
24. Which of the following is NOT an allotrope of carbon?
A) Diamond
B) Graphite
C) Fullerene
D) Sulphur
Answer: D) Sulphur (it is a different element)
25. The process of heating a sulphide ore in excess air is called:
A) Calcination
B) Roasting
C) Smelting
D) Refining
Answer: B) Roasting
26. In the thermite reaction, which metal is used to reduce iron oxide?
A) Zn
B) Mg
C) Al
D) Na
Answer: C) Al (2Al + Fe₂O₃ → 2Fe + Al₂O₃)
27. Which of the following metals reacts with a base to produce H₂ gas?
A) Fe
B) Cu
C) Zn
D) Ag
Answer: C) Zn (amphoteric, reacts with NaOH)
28. Silver articles turn black due to:
A) AgO
B) Ag₂S
C) AgCl
D) Ag₂CO₃
Answer: B) Ag₂S (silver sulphide) due to H₂S in air
29. Froth flotation method is used for the concentration of:
A) Oxide ores
B) Carbonate ores
C) Sulphide ores
D) Native metals
Answer: C) Sulphide ores (e.g., ZnS, PbS, Cu₂S)
30. Which metal is used in the galvanization of iron?
A) Copper
B) Tin
C) Zinc
D) Aluminium
Answer: C) Zinc (Zn is more reactive than Fe, corrodes first)
