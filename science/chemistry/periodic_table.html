1. Early Attempts at Classification

Before the modern periodic table, scientists tried to organize elements based on their atomic masses and similar properties. Two major early attempts were Dobereiner's Triads and Newlands' Law of Octaves.

Dobereiner's Triads (1817 - Johann Wolfgang Dobereiner)

Dobereiner grouped elements into sets of three called triads. In each triad, the three elements had similar chemical properties, and the atomic weight of the middle element was approximately the average of the other two.

Analogy:
Think of Dobereiner's Triads like a family of three siblings - the middle child's height is roughly the average of the eldest and youngest. Similarly, the middle element's atomic weight was the average of its two companions!

Limitation: Only a few elements could be grouped into triads. Many elements did not fit into such groups.

Newlands' Law of Octaves (1864 - John Newlands)

Newlands arranged elements in increasing order of atomic masses and noticed that every 8th element had properties similar to the first - just like musical notes (Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni - the octave).

Analogy:
Newlands' Octaves are like musical notes - after 7 distinct notes, the 8th (Sa') sounds similar to the first (Sa). Similarly, after every 7 elements, the 8th element repeated properties of the 1st. But this worked only for the first 17 elements!

Limitations:

  • Law worked only up to Calcium (Ca). After Ca, every 8th element did NOT have similar properties.
  • Newlands assumed only 56 elements existed (no room for noble gases discovered later).
  • He placed two elements in same slot (e.g., Co & Ni) to force fit.
  • Not accepted by the scientific community - laughed at for linking music to chemistry.
Fact:
Newlands called his arrangement the "Law of Octaves" because the pattern reminded him of the seven musical notes in an octave (do-re-mi-fa-so-la-ti). He was awarded the Davy Medal in 1887 for his work, though it was initially ridiculed.

2. Mendeleev's Periodic Table (1869)

Who was Mendeleev?

Dmitri Ivanovich Mendeleev (1834-1907), a Russian chemist, arranged all 63 known elements in order of increasing atomic mass. His great genius was leaving gaps for undiscovered elements.

Mendeleev's Periodic Law

"The properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic masses."

Analogy - Housing Society:
Mendeleev's table was like a housing society with empty plots reserved for future buildings. He left gaps for undiscovered elements and even predicted what they would look like - their properties, densities, atomic masses, and compounds! He was like an architect who could describe a house before it was built.
Key Features of Mendeleev's Table
  • Elements arranged in increasing atomic mass.
  • Divided into 8 vertical groups (I to VIII) and horizontal periods.
  • Groups I-VII contain sub-groups A & B (e.g., IA, IB). Group VIII had three elements in each row (transition metals).
  • Left gaps for elements not yet discovered. Predicted properties of these elements with remarkable accuracy.
  • Corrected atomic masses of some elements (e.g., changed Be from 14 to 9, In from 76 to 113).

Achievements
  • First systematic classification that accommodated all known elements.
  • Left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties correctly.
  • Corrected atomic masses of several elements (Be, In, U).
  • Could explain periodicity - elements with similar properties occurred at regular intervals.
Limitations
  • Could not explain isotopes (same element, different masses - e.g., Cl-35 and Cl-37 would need different positions but are chemically identical).
  • Some elements placed wrong order of atomic mass (e.g., Co - 58.93 placed before Ni - 58.69; Te - 127.60 before I - 126.90).
  • Based on atomic mass - later replaced by atomic number.
  • Position of hydrogen was ambiguous (could be in group I or VII).
  • No place for noble gases (discovered later - added as Group 0).
Teacher's Tip:
For exams: Mendeleev's main contribution = leaving gaps for undiscovered elements and correcting atomic masses. The biggest limitation = isotopes problem and wrong order (Co-Ni, Te-I). Mendeleev is called "Father of the Periodic Table".

3. Modern Periodic Table

Moseley's Contribution (1913)

Henry Moseley (British physicist) studied X-ray spectra of elements and discovered that atomic number (Z) - not atomic mass - is the fundamental property of elements. This resolved the anomalies in Mendeleev's table.

Modern Periodic Law

"The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic numbers."

Key Difference:
Mendeleev: Periodic function of atomic mass. Modern: Periodic function of atomic number. This one change fixed the Co-Ni and Te-I reversal problem - because Co (Z=27) comes before Ni (Z=28) even though Co has higher atomic mass!
Structure of the Modern Periodic Table

The modern table has 18 groups (vertical columns) and 7 periods (horizontal rows).

Analogy - School:
The modern periodic table is like a school - same group = same class (same uniform/valence electrons). Same period = same grade (same number of shells/classrooms). Just like students in the same class wear the same uniform, elements in the same group have the same valence electrons!

Groups (18 vertical columns):

  • Elements in same group have same number of valence electrons.
  • They show similar chemical properties.
  • Valence electrons: Group 1 = 1, Group 2 = 2, Group 13 = 3, Group 14 = 4, Group 15 = 5, Group 16 = 6, Group 17 = 7, Group 18 = 8 (except He = 2).
  • Groups 3 to 12 are transition metals (d-block).

Periods (7 horizontal rows):

  • Elements in same period have same number of electron shells.
  • Period 1: 2 elements (H, He) - 1 shell.
  • Period 2: 8 elements (Li to Ne) - 2 shells.
  • Period 3: 8 elements (Na to Ar) - 3 shells.
  • Period 4: 18 elements (K to Kr) - 4 shells.
  • Period 5: 18 elements (Rb to Xe) - 5 shells.
  • Period 6: 32 elements (Cs to Rn) - 6 shells (includes lanthanoids).
  • Period 7: 32 elements (Fr to Og) - 7 shells (includes actinoids).

Classification into Blocks

Elements are classified into four blocks based on which orbital the last electron enters:

  • s-block → Groups 1 & 2 (alkali & alkaline earth metals).
  • p-block → Groups 13 to 18 (metals, non-metals, metalloids, noble gases).
  • d-block → Groups 3 to 12 (transition metals).
  • f-block → Below main table (inner transition - lanthanoids & actinoids).
Teacher's Tip:
To find group from electronic configuration: If last electron is in s-orbital → group = number of valence electrons. If in p-orbital → group = 10 + valence electrons (for Groups 13-18). If in d-orbital → group = number of electrons in (n-1)d + ns. For f-block → group 3 (always).

4. Periodic Trends

Periodic trends are regular variations in properties of elements across a period (left to right) and down a group (top to bottom). These are crucial for BPSC/UPSC exams.

Valence Electrons

Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost shell of an atom. They determine the chemical properties and valency.

  • Across a period: Increases from 1 to 8 (e.g., Period 3: Na=1, Mg=2, Al=3, Si=4, P=5, S=6, Cl=7, Ar=8).
  • Down a group: Remains same (all alkali metals have 1 valence electron, all halogens have 7).
Atomic Size (Atomic Radius)

Atomic radius is the distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron. It determines how large an atom is.

Analogy - Squeezing a Balloon:
Atomic size across a period is like squeezing a balloon - as you add more protons (positive charge), the electrons get pulled in tighter, making the atom smaller. Across a period, nuclear charge increases, pulling electrons closer → size decreases.

Ionization Energy (IE)

Ionization energy is the minimum energy required to remove an electron from an isolated gaseous atom. Higher IE means the atom holds its electrons tightly.

Analogy - Removing a Student:
Ionization energy is like how hard you need to pull to remove a student from class. If the atom is large (like a student sitting near the door), it's easy to remove an electron (low IE). If the atom is small (sitting in the middle, tightly held), it's hard (high IE).

Electronegativity

Electronegativity is the tendency of an atom to attract shared electrons in a chemical bond. Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element (4.0 on Pauling scale).

Fact:
Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element (4.0). Caesium (Cs) is the least electronegative (0.79). Noble gases have zero electronegativity (they don't form bonds). Among groups, electronegativity decreases down the group and increases across a period.
Metallic Character

Metallic character refers to the tendency of an element to lose electrons and form positive ions (cations). Metals are good conductors of heat and electricity, are malleable and ductile.

Non-metals tend to gain electrons (form negative ions). They are poor conductors, brittle, and have high electronegativity.

5. Position of Elements - From Electronic Configuration

How to Find Group & Period

To find the position of an element in the periodic table, write its electronic configuration:

  • Period = Number of shells (highest n value).
  • Group = Number of valence electrons (for s-block and p-block).
  • For s-block (Groups 1-2): Group = number of valence electrons.
  • For p-block (Groups 13-18): Group = 10 + valence electrons.
  • For d-block (Groups 3-12): Group = ns + (n-1)d electrons.

6. Classification into s, p, d, f Blocks

s-Block Elements

Groups 1 & 2. The last electron enters the s-orbital of the outermost shell.

  • Group 1 = Alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr) - 1 valence e⁻, highly reactive, +1 charge.
  • Group 2 = Alkaline earth metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra) - 2 valence e⁻, +2 charge.
  • Soft metals, low melting points, good conductors of electricity.
  • Reactivity increases down the group (Francium is most reactive).
  • They form ionic compounds and are strong reducing agents.
p-Block Elements

Groups 13 to 18. The last electron enters the p-orbital of the outermost shell.

  • Includes metals (Al, Ga, In, Tl, Pb, Bi), non-metals (C, N, O, P, S, Se, halogens), metalloids (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te), and noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn).
  • Valence electrons: Group 13 = 3, 14 = 4, 15 = 5, 16 = 6, 17 = 7, 18 = 8 (except He = 2).
  • Most diverse block - contains elements with very different properties.
  • Noble gases (Group 18) are chemically inert (stable octet).
d-Block Elements (Transition Metals)

Groups 3 to 12. The last electron enters the d-orbital of the penultimate (n-1) shell.

  • All are metals - hard, high melting points, good conductors.
  • Variable oxidation states (e.g., Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺; Mn²⁺, Mn³⁺, Mn⁴⁺, Mn⁶⁺, Mn⁷⁺).
  • Form coloured compounds (e.g., CuSO₄ is blue, FeSO₄ is green, KMnO₄ is purple).
  • Used as catalysts (e.g., Fe in Haber process, Pt/Pd in catalytic converters).
  • Exhibit paramagnetism (attracted by magnetic field due to unpaired electrons).
  • Known for forming complex compounds (coordination compounds).
f-Block Elements (Inner Transition Metals)

The last electron enters the f-orbital of the anti-penultimate (n-2) shell. These are placed below the main table.

  • Lanthanoids (Ce to Lu, Z=58-71): Also called rare earth elements. Similar properties. Used in lasers, magnets, phosphors.
  • Actinoids (Th to Lr, Z=90-103): All are radioactive. Used in nuclear energy (U, Pu).
  • All are metals with high melting points.
  • Show variable oxidation states (especially actinoids).

The d-block contains 40 elements (3d, 4d, 5d series). The f-block contains 28 elements (14 lanthanoids + 14 actinoids). The longest period (6) has 32 elements and includes the lanthanoids placed below the table.

Among halogens, a more reactive halogen displaces a less reactive one. The order of reactivity: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂. So Cl₂ can displace Br⁻ and I⁻ from their salts, but F₂ can displace all others!

8. Electronic Configuration & Finding Position

How to Write Electronic Configuration

Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy following the Aufbau principle, Pauli's exclusion principle, and Hund's rule.

For NCERT Class 6-10 level, use the 2, 8, 18, 32... distribution: 1st shell (K) = 2, 2nd (L) = 8, 3rd (M) = 18, 4th (N) = 32.

Step-by-Step Method to Find Position

Step 1: Write electronic configuration (2, 8, 18...).
Step 2: Count number of shells → that is the Period.
Step 3: Count valence electrons (outermost shell electrons).
Step 4: If valence e⁻ = 1 or 2 → Group = valence e⁻ (s-block).
Step 5: If valence e⁻ = 3 to 8 → Group = 10 + valence e⁻ (p-block, Groups 13-18).
Step 6: If element is in d-block → Group = (n-1)d + ns electrons.

Example - Na:
Na (Z=11): Config = 2, 8, 1. 3 shells → Period 3. 1 valence electron → Group 1. It's in the s-block, an alkali metal.
Example - Cl:
Cl (Z=17): Config = 2, 8, 7. 3 shells → Period 3. 7 valence electrons → Group = 10 + 7 = 17. It's in the p-block, a halogen.
Teacher's Tip:
For BPSC/UPSC exam: Remember the special cases: Hydrogen (unique - placed in Group 1 but has properties of both 1 and 17). Helium (Group 18 despite 2 electrons in s-orbital). Also note that group number = number of valence electrons only works for s-block; for p-block it's 10 + valence electrons.
