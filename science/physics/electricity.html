1. Electric Charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. Think of it like a currency — every particle either has a positive (+) or negative (-) charge.

Types of Charge
  • Positive Charge (+) – Carried by protons. Like having extra money you can give away.
  • Negative Charge (−) – Carried by electrons. Like being in debt (attracted to positive).
  • Neutral – Equal number of protons and electrons. Balanced — no net charge.
Properties of Charge
  • Like charges repel (++) or (−−) → Push each other away. Think of two north poles of magnets.
  • Unlike charges attract (+−) → Pull toward each other. Like north and south poles.
  • Quantization of Charge: Charge on any body is always an integer multiple of the charge on an electron (e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C). You cannot have 0.5 times e — charge comes in packets!
  • Conservation of Charge: Charge can neither be created nor destroyed — it just transfers from one body to another. Total charge in an isolated system is always constant.
  • SI Unit: Coulomb (C). 1 C = charge of 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons.

Analogy: Think of charges as magnetic poles. North-North repel (like charges), North-South attract (unlike). The stronger the charge, the stronger the push or pull — just like bigger magnets!

2. Conductors & Insulators

Based on how easily electric charge flows through them, materials are divided into conductors, insulators, and semiconductors.

Definitions
  • Conductor – A material that allows electric charge to flow through it easily. Why? Because it has free electrons that can move. E.g., all metals (copper, aluminium, silver, gold), graphite, human body.
  • Insulator – A material that does NOT allow electric charge to flow easily. Electrons are tightly bound to atoms. E.g., rubber, plastic, wood, glass, dry air, paper.
  • Semiconductor – Conductivity is between conductors and insulators. Conductivity increases with temperature. E.g., silicon (Si), germanium (Ge).

Why Do Metals Conduct?

Metals have a sea of free electrons — outermost electrons are loosely held and can drift through the metal lattice. When you apply a voltage, these electrons drift in one direction → electric current!

3. Electric Current

Definition

Electric Current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor. In simple words: how many electrons pass a point in one second.

I = Q / t

Where: I = Current (Amperes), Q = Charge (Coulombs), t = Time (seconds).

SI Units

Direction of Current
  • Conventional Current: Flows from Positive (+) → Negative (−) terminal. This is the older convention (Benjamin Franklin's guess).
  • Electron Flow: Actually, electrons flow from Negative (−) → Positive (+). Real direction is opposite to conventional current!
  • Exam Tip: In circuit diagrams, always show conventional current direction (+ to −). Unless asked specifically about electron flow.

5. Ohm's Law

Statement

At a constant temperature, the potential difference (V) across a conductor is directly proportional to the current (I) flowing through it.

V ∝ I  ⇒  V = I × R

Where: V = Voltage / Potential Difference (Volts), I = Current (Amperes), R = Resistance (Ohms, Ω).

Water Pipe Analogy
Teacher's Analogy: Imagine a water pipe system. Voltage (V) = Water pressure (how hard the pump pushes). Current (I) = Flow of water (litres per minute). Resistance (R) = Narrowness of pipe (a thin pipe resists flow). Ohm's Law says: More pressure (V) → More flow (I), IF the pipe width (R) stays the same. If you make the pipe narrower (increase R), flow (I) decreases for the same pressure.
V-I Graph (Ohmic Conductor)

For an ohmic conductor (obeys Ohm's law), the V-I graph is a straight line through the origin. The slope of this line = Resistance (R). Higher slope = higher resistance.

Ohmic vs Non-Ohmic Conductors
FeatureOhmic (Obeys Ohm's Law)Non-Ohmic (Does NOT obey)
V-I GraphStraight line through originCurved / Non-linear
ResistanceConstant (at fixed temp)Changes with V or I
ExamplesCopper wire, Nichrome, Carbon resistorBulb filament, Diode, LED, Thermistor

Experimental Setup to Verify Ohm's Law
  • Connect a resistor (R), an ammeter (A) in series, and a voltmeter (V) in parallel across the resistor.
  • Vary the voltage using a variable battery (or rheostat).
  • Record V and I for each setting.
  • Plot V on Y-axis, I on X-axis → should get a straight line.
  • Slope = V/I = R (constant).
Numerical Example

Q: A 12 V battery is connected to a 4 Ω resistor. Find the current flowing. Ans: I = V/R = 12/4 = 3 A.

Factors Affecting Resistance

Resistance is the opposition to the flow of current. Four factors determine it:

FactorEffect on ResistanceAnalogy

Series vs Parallel Combination of Resistors

Series: Resistors end-to-end. Same current through each. Total resistance increases.

Req = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ... (for series)

Parallel: Resistors side-by-side. Same voltage across each. Total resistance decreases.

1/Req = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ... (for parallel)

VoltageDivided across componentsSame across all branches

If one bulb failsAll go out (circuit breaks)Others stay on

AnalogyTrain coaches — one detaches, all stopHighway lanes — one closed, others work
Numerical Examples

Series: Three resistors of 2 Ω, 3 Ω, 5 Ω in series → Req = 2 + 3 + 5 = 10 Ω.

Parallel: Two resistors of 6 Ω and 3 Ω in parallel → 1/Req = 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/6 + 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2 → Req = 2 Ω.

Memory Tip: "Series = Same current, Sum of resistances". "Parallel = Potential same, Reciprocal sum". Or: Series makes resistance bigger (adding), Parallel makes it smaller (more paths for current).

7. Heating Effect of Current

Joule's Law of Heating

When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted into heat energy. The amount of heat produced is given by:

H = I² × R × t  (Joule's Law)

Where: H = Heat produced (Joules), I = Current (A), R = Resistance (Ω), t = Time (s).

Why H = I²Rt? (Derivation)

Work done by battery = V × Q = V × I × t. Using V = IR → W = (IR) × I × t = I²Rt. This work appears as heat! So H = I²Rt.

ApplianceHeating ElementWhy that material?

Electric Heater / GeyserNichrome wireSame as above — produces controlled heat
Electric BulbTungsten filamentVery high melting point (3422°C), glows white-hot
Electric FuseThin wire of low melting point (Pb-Sn alloy)Melts and breaks circuit when current exceeds safe limit
Electric ToasterNichrome wireEven heating, long life
Electric Power

Electric Power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or produced.

P = V × I = I² × R = V² / R

SI Unit: Watt (W). 1 Watt = 1 Joule/second = 1 Volt × 1 Ampere.

8. Commercial Unit — kWh & Electricity Bill

Definition

The SI unit of energy is Joule (J). But 1 Joule is very small. For commercial use, we use kilowatt-hour (kWh) — the energy consumed by a 1 kW appliance in 1 hour.

1 kWh = 1 kW × 1 h = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Formula for Electricity Bill
Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) × Time (h)
Electricity Bill = Total Units (kWh) × Rate per Unit
Numerical Example

Problem: A house uses 5 LED bulbs (10 W each) for 6 hours/day, a fan (75 W) for 10 hours/day, and a refrigerator (200 W) for 12 hours/day. Calculate the monthly bill (30 days) if rate is ₹6.50 per kWh.

Solution:

  • Bulbs: 5 × 10 W × 6 h = 300 Wh = 0.3 kWh/day
  • Fan: 75 W × 10 h = 750 Wh = 0.75 kWh/day
  • Fridge: 200 W × 12 h = 2400 Wh = 2.4 kWh/day
  • Total per day = 0.3 + 0.75 + 2.4 = 3.45 kWh
  • Total per month = 3.45 × 30 = 103.5 kWh (units)
  • Bill = 103.5 × 6.50 = ₹672.75
Unit Conversions
ConvertOperationExample
kW → WMultiply by 10001.5 kW = 1500 W
W → kWDivide by 1000200 W = 0.2 kW
Hours → secondsMultiply by 36001 h = 3600 s
kWh → JoulesMultiply by 3.6 × 10⁶1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Remember: 1 unit of electricity in your bill = 1 kWh. So if your bill says "200 units consumed", it means 200 kWh of energy was used.

9. Electric Fuse & Safety

Electric Fuse

A fuse is a safety device that protects circuits from overloading and short-circuit. It contains a thin wire of low melting point (tin-lead alloy) that melts and breaks the circuit when current exceeds a safe limit.

Earthing

Earthing (grounding) connects the metal body of appliances to the Earth through a thick copper wire. If a live wire touches the metal body, current flows directly to the ground instead of through the user. This prevents electric shock.

Safety Precautions
  • Never touch electrical appliances with wet hands — water conducts electricity.
  • Always use three-pin plugs with proper earthing.
  • Do not overload a single socket (too many appliances = too much current = fire risk).
  • Switch off the main switch before replacing a fuse or repairing.
  • Use fuse or MCB of correct rating for each circuit.
  • Keep electrical wires away from water sources and children.
  • In case of electric fire, never use water — use fire extinguisher (CO₂ type) or sand.

Analogy: A fuse is like a pressure relief valve in a water pipe. If pressure gets too high, the valve bursts and releases water — protecting the pipe. Similarly, if current gets too high, the fuse wire melts and breaks the circuit — protecting your appliances!

11. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Ohm's Law Triangle
The V = IR Triangle: Cover the quantity you want to find. "VIR" — V on top, I and R below. V = I × R (top = product of bottom two). I = V/R (cover I → V over R). R = V/I (cover R → V over I). Or remember: "Very Important Rule" = V = I × R.
Resistance Factors
Factors of Resistance: "L MAT" = Length, Material, Area (cross-sectional), Temperature. Or: "R = ρ L/A" — think "Rho L over A" for the formula.
Series vs Parallel
Series vs Parallel: "Series = S" → Same current, Sum of resistances, all Stop if one fails. "Parallel = P" → Potential same (voltage), Paths divided, each works independently. Or use: "S-S-S" = Series = Same current (I) = Sum of R.
Power Formulas
Three Power Formulas: "P = VI" (basic), "P = I²R" (heating), "P = V²/R" (substitute I = V/R). Trick: "Papa VI loves I²R from V²/R" — silly but works!
Colour Code of Resistors
Resistor Colour Code (BBROYGBVGW): "B B ROY of Great Britain has a Very Good Wife" = Black (0), Brown (1), Red (2), Orange (3), Yellow (4), Green (5), Blue (6), Violet (7), Grey (8), White (9).
