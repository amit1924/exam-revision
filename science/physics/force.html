1. Force – Definition, Effects & Types

Force is a push or a pull that changes or tends to change the state of rest or of uniform motion of an object. In simple terms — anything that makes an object move, stop, speed up, slow down, change direction, or change shape is a force.

Effects of Force
  • Change in state of motion: A stationary ball starts moving when kicked; a moving bicycle stops when brakes are applied.
  • Change in speed: Pedalling a bicycle increases speed; friction with the ground slows it down.
  • Change in direction: A footballer kicks a moving ball to change its path; Earth's gravity bends the Moon's orbit.
  • Change in shape/size: Squeezing a sponge changes its shape; hammering a hot iron flattens it.
Types of Forces

A) Contact Forces (force acts through physical contact)

  • Muscular Force: Force applied by muscles of humans/animals. E.g., lifting a bucket, pulling a cart, chewing food.
  • Frictional Force: Force that opposes relative motion between two surfaces in contact. E.g., rubbing hands together, walking, brake pads on bicycle wheel.
  • Mechanical Force: Force applied by machines, tools, or engines. E.g., a crane lifting steel beams, a hammer driving a nail.
  • Normal Force (N): The supporting force exerted by a surface perpendicular to the object resting on it. E.g., a book on a table — the table pushes upward with normal force equal to the book's weight.
  • Tension Force (T): The force transmitted through a string, rope, cable, or wire when pulled by forces acting from opposite ends. E.g., a tug-of-war rope, a hanging lamp from the ceiling.

B) Non-Contact Forces (force acts without physical contact — action at a distance)

  • Gravitational Force: The attractive force between any two objects with mass. E.g., Earth pulls everything towards its centre; the Sun holds planets in orbit.
  • Magnetic Force: The force exerted by a magnet on ferromagnetic materials (iron, nickel, cobalt) or other magnets. E.g., magnet attracting iron pins; compass needle aligning with Earth's magnetic field.
  • Electrostatic Force: The force between charged particles. Like charges repel; unlike charges attract. E.g., rubbing a plastic comb on hair attracts paper bits; lightning discharge between clouds.
Analogy: Force is like a remote control. Muscular force is like pressing a button with your finger (contact). Gravitational force is like a remote that works from across the room (non-contact). Magnetic force is like a remote that only works with certain devices (specific materials).

2. Net Force, Balanced & Unbalanced Forces

Net Force

Net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object. If two people push a box in the same direction with 10 N each, net force = 20 N. If they push in opposite directions, net force = 0 N (they cancel).

Balanced vs Unbalanced Forces
PropertyBalanced ForcesUnbalanced Forces
DefinitionEqual magnitude, opposite direction — net force = 0Unequal magnitude or not opposite — net force ≠ 0
Effect on objectNo change in motion (object at rest stays at rest; moving object continues moving at same speed)Change in motion (speed up, slow down, or change direction)
Example 1 – Tug of WarBoth teams pull equally — rope doesn't moveOne team pulls harder — rope moves towards them
Example 2 – Pushing a BoxTwo people push from opposite sides with equal force — box stays stillOne person pushes from left, no one from right — box moves left
Example 3 – CarCar moving at constant speed — engine force = friction + air dragCar accelerating — engine force > friction + drag
Analogy: Balanced forces are like two teams in a tug of war pulling equally — no one moves. Unbalanced forces are like one team suddenly pulling harder — the rope (and the other team) moves in that direction. In daily life, balanced forces keep a book stationary on a table (gravity down = normal force up).
Newton's Second Law & Net Force

Newton's Second Law connects net force, mass, and acceleration:

Fnet = m × a

Where Fnet = net force (N), m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (m/s²). SI unit of force is Newton (N). 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².

3. Pressure – Definition, Units & Daily Examples

Definition

Pressure is the force acting perpendicularly (thrust) per unit area.

P = F / A

Where P = Pressure, F = Force (thrust), A = Area on which force acts.

Units of Pressure
  • SI unit: Pascal (Pa). 1 Pa = 1 N/m².
  • Other units: atm (atmosphere), bar, torr, mm of Hg.
  • 1 atm = 101,325 Pa = 1.01325 bar = 760 mm of Hg.
Daily Life Examples (with simple analogies)
  • Why a knife cuts: A sharp blade has a very small area, producing high pressure even with moderate force. Analogy: Pressure = distributing weight — wearing a heavy backpack feels lighter with wide straps than thin ones because force spreads over more area.
  • School bag straps: Wide straps spread the force over a larger shoulder area → less pressure → more comfortable. Thin straps dig into shoulders because force is concentrated on a small area → high pressure.
  • Nails have pointed tips: Small area → high pressure → easily penetrates wood. The flat head spreads the hammer force over a larger area to avoid damaging the surface.
  • Tractors have wide tyres: Large area → low pressure → don't sink into soft mud. Similarly, snowshoes prevent sinking into snow by spreading weight over a large area.
  • Building foundations: Wide base spreads the building's weight → reduces pressure on the ground → prevents sinking.
Analogy: Pressure = "The concentration of push". It's like the difference between poking someone with a finger (small area, high pressure) versus pushing them with a whole open hand (large area, low pressure). Same force, but very different results!

4. Pressure in Fluids & Pascal's Law

Liquid Pressure

Fluids (liquids and gases) also exert pressure. Liquid pressure increases with depth and density.

  • Depends on depth: Pressure increases as you go deeper. That's why dams are thicker at the bottom — to withstand higher water pressure.
  • Depends on density: Denser fluids exert more pressure at the same depth. Mercury (density 13.6 g/cm³) exerts much higher pressure than water at the same depth.
  • Acts equally in all directions: At any given depth, liquid pressure acts perpendicular to all surfaces.
  • Formula: P = h × ρ × g (h = depth, ρ = density of fluid, g = acceleration due to gravity).
Pascal's Law

Pascal's Law (Blaise Pascal, 1653): Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid and the walls of the containing vessel.

Applications of Pascal's Law

  • Hydraulic Lift (Car jack): A small force applied on a small-area piston creates pressure that is transmitted through the fluid to a large-area piston, multiplying the force. Fout = (Aout / Ain) × Fin. This is the principle of hydraulic multiplication.
  • Hydraulic Brakes (in cars/bikes): When you press the brake pedal, force is transmitted through brake fluid to the brake pads, which press against the wheels to slow them down.
  • Hydraulic Press: Used in industry to compress materials (like shaping metal sheets, compressing cotton bales).
  • Syringes and droppers: Pushing the plunger increases pressure on the fluid, forcing it out through the narrow needle.
Analogy: Pascal's law is like a crowd at a concert — when someone at the front pushes, the push is transmitted through the whole crowd equally. Or like squeezing a toothpaste tube — pressure is transmitted throughout the paste, forcing it out of the nozzle.

5. Atmospheric Pressure

What is Atmospheric Pressure?

The layer of air (atmosphere) surrounding Earth has weight. This weight exerts pressure on everything on Earth's surface — this is called atmospheric pressure.

  • At sea level, atmospheric pressure is about 101,325 Pa (1 atm).
  • We don't feel it because our body fluids exert equal pressure from inside.
  • Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude (thinner air above).
Classic Demonstrations
  • Magdeburg Hemispheres (1654): Otto von Guericke pumped air out of two joined hemispheres. Two teams of horses could not pull them apart because the outside atmospheric pressure held them together. When air was let back in, they fell apart easily!
  • Drinking Straw: When you suck air out of a straw, the pressure inside decreases. The higher atmospheric pressure on the liquid surface pushes the drink up into your mouth.
  • Syringe: Pulling the plunger creates low pressure inside; atmospheric pressure pushes liquid into the barrel.
  • Candle in Water: A burning candle in a dish of water is covered with a glass. As the candle consumes oxygen and the air cools, pressure inside drops — water rises into the glass.
  • Rubber Sucker (Suction Hook): Pressing the sucker pushes air out. The higher atmospheric pressure outside holds it firmly against the wall.
Applications
  • Barometer: Instrument to measure atmospheric pressure. A mercury barometer uses a column of mercury whose height changes with pressure.
  • Weather Forecasting: Rapid drop in atmospheric pressure indicates a storm or cyclone. High pressure generally means clear weather.
  • Altitude measurement: Aeroplanes use altimeters (barometers calibrated for altitude). As altitude increases, atmospheric pressure decreases.
  • Working of pumps: Water pumps, hand pumps, and vacuum pumps all utilise atmospheric pressure.
Analogy: We live at the bottom of an ocean of air. Just as water pressure increases with depth in the ocean, atmospheric pressure is maximum at sea level (the "bottom" of our air ocean) and decreases as we go up mountains.

6. Buoyancy & Archimedes' Principle

Buoyancy

When an object is placed in a fluid, the fluid exerts an upward force called buoyant force or upthrust. This is why some objects float and others sink.

  • Direction of buoyant force: Always upward (opposite to gravity).
  • Magnitude: Depends on the volume of fluid displaced by the object.
  • An object floats if its density is less than the fluid's density. It sinks if its density is greater.
Archimedes' Principle

Archimedes' Principle: When an object is partially or fully immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object.

Buoyant Force (Fb) = Weight of displaced fluid = ρfluid × Vdisplaced × g

Why Ships Float (A Classic Puzzle):

A ship made of steel (density ~7800 kg/m³) floats because of its shape. The hollow hull displaces a large volume of water. As long as the weight of water displaced equals the ship's total weight, it floats. This is why a steel ship floats but a steel nail sinks — the nail displaces too little water.

Relative Density

Relative Density (R.D.) or Specific Gravity is the ratio of the density of a substance to the density of water (at 4°C).

R.D. = Density of substance / Density of water
  • R.D. > 1 → substance sinks in water (e.g., iron R.D. = 7.8).
  • R.D. < 1 → substance floats on water (e.g., ice R.D. = 0.9, wood R.D. ≈ 0.8).
  • R.D. = 1 → substance just submerges (neutral buoyancy).
  • Relative density has no units (it's a ratio).
Story: Archimedes discovered this principle while taking a bath! He noticed water overflowing as he got in and realised the volume of water displaced equals his body's volume. He reportedly ran naked through the streets shouting "Eureka!" (I found it!). He used this to detect if the king's crown was made of pure gold or adulterated with silver.

7. Gravitation – Universal Law of Gravitation

Newton's Universal Law of Gravitation

Every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force that is:

  • Directly proportional to the product of their masses (m₁ × m₂).
  • Inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centres (r²).
F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r²

Where:

  • F = Gravitational force between two objects (N).
  • m₁, m₂ = masses of the two objects (kg).
  • r = distance between their centres (m).
  • G = Universal Gravitational Constant = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg².
Importance of Gravitation
  • Holds planets in orbit around the Sun.
  • Holds the Moon in orbit around Earth (causes tides).
  • Keeps our atmosphere from escaping into space.
  • Responsible for the formation of stars, galaxies, and the universe itself.
  • Objects fall to the ground because of Earth's gravitational pull.
Cavendish Experiment (1798)

Henry Cavendish measured the value of G using a torsion balance. He suspended a rod with two small lead balls and brought large lead balls near them. The tiny gravitational attraction between the balls twisted the fibre. By measuring the twist, he calculated G. This is known as "weighing the Earth" because knowing G allowed calculating Earth's mass (5.97 × 10²⁴ kg).

Analogy: Gravitational force is like a weak but universal friendship — every object has some attraction to every other object, but you only notice it when the masses are huge (like Earth and Sun). Between two people, the gravitational force is about the same as the weight of a single skin cell!

8. Free Fall & Acceleration Due to Gravity (g)

Free Fall

Free fall is the motion of an object under the sole influence of gravity (no air resistance). In free fall, all objects accelerate towards Earth at the same rate, regardless of their mass. This was famously demonstrated by Galileo's Leaning Tower of Pisa experiment (or more precisely, using inclined planes).

Acceleration Due to Gravity (g)

The acceleration produced in a freely falling body due to Earth's gravitational pull is called acceleration due to gravity, denoted by g.

g = G × M / R²

Where M = mass of Earth (6 × 10²⁴ kg), R = radius of Earth (6.4 × 10⁶ m). Substituting: g ≈ 9.8 m/s².

Variation of g
  • With Altitude (height above Earth): g decreases as we go higher. gh = g × (R/(R+h))². At the top of Mount Everest (~8.8 km), g is about 0.3% less.
  • With Depth (inside Earth): g decreases linearly as we go deeper. At the centre of Earth, g = 0.
  • With Latitude: g is maximum at the poles (9.83 m/s²) and minimum at the equator (9.78 m/s²). This is due to Earth's rotation and its oblate shape (bulging at equator).
  • With Rotation of Earth: Earth's rotation slightly reduces g at the equator due to centrifugal force. If Earth stopped spinning, g at equator would increase by about 0.34%.
Analogy: Think of g as "Earth's gravitational grip". On Earth, the grip tightens (g = 9.8). On the Moon, Earth's grip is weaker (g = 1.62) — that's why astronauts feel light and bounce around. If you jump on the Moon, you go about 6 times higher than on Earth!

9. Mass vs Weight – Key Differences

Definitions

Mass is the amount of matter in an object. It is a scalar quantity.

Weight is the gravitational force exerted on an object. It is a vector quantity (direction: towards the centre of Earth).

W = m × g

Weight on Different Planets

Your mass stays the same everywhere. But your weight changes because g changes. A person with mass 60 kg (weight on Earth = 588 N) would weigh:

  • On Moon: ~97 N (about 1/6 of Earth weight).
  • On Mars: ~222 N (about 0.38 of Earth weight).
  • On Jupiter: ~1488 N (about 2.5 times Earth weight).
  • On Sun: ~16,440 N (about 28 times Earth weight — you'd be crushed!).
Weightlessness

Weightlessness is the sensation of having no weight. It occurs when an object is in free fall — the only force acting is gravity. Astronauts in the International Space Station (ISS) experience weightlessness not because there is no gravity (g there is about 90% of Earth's surface value), but because they are in continuous free fall towards Earth, moving sideways so fast that they keep missing it (orbital motion).

Analogy: Your mass is like the number of groceries you bought — it doesn't change. Your weight is like how heavy the bag feels — it depends on where you carry it (on Earth, on the Moon, or in space). The groceries (mass) are the same, but the feeling (weight) changes with gravity!

10. Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Johannes Kepler (1571–1630) formulated three laws of planetary motion based on the precise astronomical observations of Tycho Brahe. These laws describe how planets move around the Sun.

First Law – Law of Ellipses

Each planet moves around the Sun in an elliptical orbit, with the Sun at one of the two foci.

Simple explanation: The orbit is like a flattened circle (oval), not a perfect circle. The Sun is not at the centre but slightly off to one side.

Second Law – Law of Equal Areas

A line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time.

Simple explanation: Planets move faster when they are closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when farther away (aphelion). It's like a skater spinning — arms in (closer to axis) = faster, arms out (farther) = slower.

Third Law – Law of Periods

The square of the orbital period (T²) of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis (r³) of its orbit.

T² ∝ r³   or   T² / r³ = constant

Simple explanation: Planets farther from the Sun take much longer to complete one orbit. Mercury (closest) takes just 88 days; Neptune (farthest) takes 165 years!

Memory Aid: Kepler's Laws in 3 words: SHAPE (elliptical orbits), SPEED (faster when closer), SCALE (farther planets take longer). The three S's!
Mnemonic: "Kepler Cried: "EAS!"" — E = Elliptical orbits (Law 1), A = Areas equal (Law 2), S = Square-cube relationship (Law 3). Or: "Every Astronomer's Secret" — Ellipse, Area, Square-cube.

12. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Types of Forces
Contact Forces: "My Fingers Never Touch Metal" = Muscular, Friction, Normal, Tension, Mechanical. All five require physical contact to act.
Non-Contact Forces: "GME" = Gravitational, Magnetic, Electrostatic. Think of these as the three "action-at-a-distance" forces. They work even in a vacuum!
Pressure Formula
P = F/A: "Pressure = Force over Area" — think "Push per Area". More force = more pressure. More area = less pressure. "Small tip, big push" = high pressure (nails, needles, knives).
Kepler's Laws
Kepler's 3 Laws: "Every Astronomer's Secret" — Ellipse (Law 1: orbits are elliptical), Area (Law 2: equal areas in equal time), Square-cube (Law 3: T² ∝ r³). Or simply: "Shape, Speed, Scale."
Universal Gravitation
Gravitational Force Formula: F = G·m₁·m₂ / r² — "Gee, two masses (m₁m₂) are attracted, but distance (r²) pulls them apart!" Remember: G is a tiny number (6.67 × 10⁻¹¹) — gravity is the weakest fundamental force, which is why we don't feel attracted to nearby objects.

13. Practice Questions – 15 MCQs

1. The SI unit of pressure is:
A) Newton
B) Pascal
C) Joule
D) Watt
Answer: B) Pascal (Pa) — 1 Pa = 1 N/m².
2. Which of the following is a non-contact force?
A) Frictional force
B) Muscular force
C) Gravitational force
D) Tension force
Answer: C) Gravitational force — it acts without physical contact.
3. What is the value of acceleration due to gravity (g) on Earth's surface?
A) 9.8 m/s
B) 9.8 m/s²
C) 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²
D) 10 N
Answer: B) 9.8 m/s².
4. Pascal's law is used in which of the following?
A) Hydraulic lift
B) Drinking straw
C) Barometer
D) Thermometer
Answer: A) Hydraulic lift — Pascal's law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished throughout the fluid.
5. A sharp knife cuts better than a blunt knife because:
A) Sharp knife has more area
B) Sharp knife has less area → more pressure
C) Sharp knife has more mass
D) Blunt knife is heavier
Answer: B) A sharp knife has a smaller cutting area, producing higher pressure for the same applied force (P = F/A).
6. According to Archimedes' principle, the buoyant force on an object is equal to:
A) Weight of the object
B) Weight of fluid displaced
C) Volume of the object
D) Density of the fluid
Answer: B) Weight of the fluid displaced by the object.
7. The weight of an object on the Moon is about _____ of its weight on Earth.
A) 1/2
B) 1/4
C) 1/6
D) 1/10
Answer: C) 1/6 (Moon's g = 1.62 m/s² ≈ 1/6 of Earth's 9.8 m/s²).
8. Kepler's first law states that planets move in:
A) Circular orbits with Sun at centre
B) Elliptical orbits with Sun at one focus
C) Parabolic orbits
D) Random paths
Answer: B) Elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus of the ellipse.
9. Which of the following forces opposes motion between two surfaces in contact?
A) Gravitational force
B) Magnetic force
C) Frictional force
D) Normal force
Answer: C) Frictional force — it always opposes relative motion.
10. Who measured the value of the universal gravitational constant G?
A) Isaac Newton
B) Galileo Galilei
C) Henry Cavendish
D) Johannes Kepler
Answer: C) Henry Cavendish (1798) using a torsion balance experiment.
11. The value of atmospheric pressure at sea level is about:
A) 10⁵ Pa
B) 10³ Pa
C) 10² Pa
D) 10⁷ Pa
Answer: A) Approximately 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa (101,325 Pa = 1 atm).
12. Two balls, one of iron and one of wood, are dropped from the same height in vacuum. Which reaches the ground first?
A) Iron ball
B) Wooden ball
C) Both reach together
D) Depends on size
Answer: C) Both reach together — in free fall (vacuum), all objects accelerate at the same rate (g = 9.8 m/s²), regardless of mass.
13. If the distance between two objects is doubled, the gravitational force between them becomes:
A) Double
B) Half
C) One-fourth
D) Four times
Answer: C) One-fourth (F ∝ 1/r², so doubling r reduces force by 2² = 4 times).
14. What happens to the value of g as we go deep inside the Earth?
A) Increases
B) Decreases
C) Remains same
D) First increases then decreases
Answer: B) Decreases linearly with depth. At Earth's centre, g = 0.
15. An iron ship floats on water while an iron nail sinks because:
A) Ship is heavier than nail
B) Ship displaces more water (greater buoyant force)
C) Nail is more dense than water
D) Ship is made of stronger iron
Answer: B) The hollow shape of the ship displaces a large volume of water, generating a buoyant force equal to the ship's weight. The nail displaces too little water to overcome its weight.
