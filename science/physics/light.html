1. Nature of Light

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see. It travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation) and behaves both as a wave and as a particle — this is called wave-particle duality.

Wave vs Particle Nature

Proposed byChristiaan Huygens (1678)Isaac Newton (1704)

Speed of Light
  • Speed of light in vacuum = 3 × 10⁸ m/s (≈ 3,00,000 km/s) — the fastest speed in the universe.
  • Symbol: c (from Latin celeritas = speed). Nothing can travel faster than light.
  • Light takes 8.3 minutes to travel from the Sun to Earth.
  • Speed decreases when light enters a medium (glass, water, diamond).
Electromagnetic Spectrum

Light is part of the electromagnetic spectrum. Visible light is only a tiny portion between infrared and ultraviolet.

Visible Spectrum — VIBGYOR

White light is composed of 7 colours. In order of increasing wavelength (decreasing frequency):

  • V — Violet (400 nm) | I — Indigo | B — Blue | G — Green | Y — Yellow | O — Orange | R — Red (700 nm)
  • Trick to remember: "VIBGYOR" — Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red.
  • Red has the longest wavelength and lowest frequency. Violet has the shortest wavelength and highest frequency.
Analogy: Think of light waves like ocean waves. Violet is like tiny, rapid ripples (short λ, high frequency). Red is like big, slow swells (long λ, low frequency). Both travel at the same speed — just like different-sized waves move at the same speed across the ocean.

2. Reflection of Light

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it strikes a surface. The surface that reflects is called a mirror.

Laws of Reflection
  • First Law: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
  • Second Law: The angle of incidence (∠i) is always equal to the angle of reflection (∠r). ∠i = ∠r
  • Normal: An imaginary line drawn perpendicular to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence.
Regular vs Diffuse Reflection
PropertyRegular (Specular) ReflectionDiffuse (Irregular) Reflection
SurfaceSmooth, polished (mirror, still water)Rough, uneven (paper, wall, road)
Image formedClear, sharp imageNo image formed
Reflected raysParallel to each otherScattered in different directions
ExampleLooking in a mirrorSeeing a book under a lamp
Laws followed?YesYes — each ray obeys laws individually
Important: Even in diffuse reflection, each individual ray obeys the laws of reflection (∠i = ∠r). The rays scatter only because the surface normals point in different directions.

3. Mirrors — Plane & Spherical

Plane Mirror

A flat, smooth surface that reflects light regularly. The image formed by a plane mirror has these characteristics:

  • Virtual — cannot be obtained on a screen.
  • Erect — same orientation as the object.
  • Same size — magnification = 1.
  • Lateral inversion — left appears right and vice versa (e.g., ambulance writing).
  • Distance — image distance = object distance behind the mirror.
Spherical Mirrors — Concave & Convex

Spherical mirrors are mirrors curved like a sphere. They are of two types:

  • Concave mirror — curved inward like the inside of a spoon. Converging mirror (focuses light).
  • Convex mirror — curved outward like the back of a spoon. Diverging mirror (spreads light).

  • For concave mirror, f is negative (by sign convention).

Analogy: Concave mirror is like a magnifying glass for light — it collects and focuses. Convex mirror is like a wide-angle security camera — it spreads the view to show more area.

4. Mirror Formula & Magnification

Mirror Formula

The mirror formula relates focal length (f), image distance (v), and object distance (u):

1/f = 1/v + 1/u
Magnification (m)

Magnification tells us how big or small the image is compared to the object:

m = height of image (hᵢ) / height of object (hₒ) = -v / u
  • If m > 1 → image is magnified (larger).
  • If m < 1 → image is diminished (smaller).
  • If m is positive → image is erect (virtual).
  • If m is negative → image is inverted (real).
Sign Convention (Cartesian)
QuantityConcave MirrorConvex Mirror
Focal length (f)Negative (-)Positive (+)
Object distance (u)Always negative (-)Always negative (-)
Image distance (v)Negative (real) / Positive (virtual)Positive (+) (always virtual)
Object height (hₒ)Always positive (+)Always positive (+)
Image height (hᵢ)Negative (inverted) / Positive (erect)Positive (+)
Numerical Problems — Examples

Example 1:

An object is placed 30 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 15 cm. Find the position and nature of the image.

Solution: u = -30 cm, f = -15 cm. Using 1/f = 1/v + 1/u → 1/v = 1/f - 1/u = -1/15 + 1/30 = -1/30 → v = -30 cm. m = -v/u = -(-30)/(-30) = -1. Image is real, inverted, same size, at C.

Example 2:

A 2 cm tall object is placed 10 cm in front of a convex mirror of focal length 15 cm. Find the image position and size.

Solution: u = -10 cm, f = +15 cm. 1/v = 1/f - 1/u = 1/15 + 1/10 = 5/30 = 1/6 → v = +6 cm. m = -v/u = -6/(-10) = +0.6. hᵢ = m × hₒ = 0.6 × 2 = 1.2 cm. Image is virtual, erect, diminished (1.2 cm).

Exam Tip: In sign convention, remember: u is always negative (object is always on the left side). f is negative for concave, positive for convex. v is negative for real images (concave), positive for virtual images.

6. Refraction of Light

Definition

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another. This happens because the speed of light changes when moving from one medium to another.

Why Does Refraction Happen?
  • When light moves from a rarer to a denser medium, its speed decreases, and it bends towards the normal.
  • When light moves from a denser to a rarer medium, its speed increases, and it bends away from the normal.
  • If light hits the boundary at 90° (normal incidence), no bending occurs, but speed still changes.
Analogy — Car in Mud: Refraction is like a car moving from a road (rare medium) into mud (dense medium). One wheel hits the mud first and slows down, causing the car to turn. When the car exits the mud back onto the road, the wheel that gets out first speeds up, turning the car the other way!
Examples of Refraction in Daily Life
  • Pencil in water — appears bent or broken at the water surface.
  • Pool looks shallower — the bottom appears raised because light bends as it leaves water.
  • Fish in water — appears closer to the surface than it actually is (spearing a fish is tricky!).
  • Twinkling of stars — starlight refracts through different layers of atmosphere.
  • Lens formation — glasses, microscopes, telescopes all use refraction.

7. Laws of Refraction & Snell's Law

Laws of Refraction
  • First Law: The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
  • Second Law (Snell's Law): The ratio of the sine of angle of incidence to the sine of angle of refraction is constant for a given pair of media.
Snell's Law Formula
n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r  or  sin i / sin r = n₂ / n₁ = ¹n₂ (refractive index of medium 2 w.r.t. medium 1)

Where n₁ = refractive index of first medium, n₂ = refractive index of second medium, i = angle of incidence, r = angle of refraction.

Refractive Index

Absolute refractive index (n) of a medium is the ratio of speed of light in vacuum (c) to speed of light in that medium (v):

n = c / v

Relative refractive index = n₂ / n₁ (medium 2 with respect to medium 1).

8. Lenses — Convex & Concave

A lens is a transparent material (usually glass) with two curved surfaces that refracts light to form images.

Types of Lenses

Light effectConverges (brings together) light raysDiverges (spreads apart) light rays
FocusReal focus (rays actually meet)Virtual focus (rays appear to diverge from)

Important Terms for Lenses
  • Optic Centre (O) — Central point of the lens. Light passes through undeviated.
  • Principal Focus (F) — Point where parallel rays converge (convex) or appear to diverge from (concave).
  • Focal Length (f) — Distance between optic centre and principal focus.
  • Each lens has two foci — one on each side (F₁ and F₂).
Ray Diagrams — Convex Lens

Rule 1: A ray parallel to principal axis passes through focus (F₂) after refraction.

Rule 2: A ray passing through focus (F₁) becomes parallel to principal axis after refraction.

Rule 3: A ray passing through optic centre (O) goes straight without deviation.

Between F₁ and 2F₁Beyond 2F₂Real, inverted, magnifiedProjector, cinema

Ray Diagrams — Concave Lens
  • A ray parallel to principal axis appears to diverge from the focus (F) on the same side.
  • A ray directed towards the focus on the other side becomes parallel after refraction.
  • A ray through optic centre passes straight through.
  • Image always: Virtual, erect, diminished, between O and F (on the same side as the object).
Analogy: Convex lens is like a magnifying glass — it brings light together and can burn paper. Concave lens is like wearing myopia glasses — it spreads light out and reduces image size.

9. Lens Formula & Power of Lens

Lens Formula

The lens formula is similar to the mirror formula, but with a minus sign for the image distance term:

1/f = 1/v - 1/u

Where f = focal length, v = image distance, u = object distance.

Magnification (Lens)
m = hᵢ / hₒ = v / u
  • Note: For lenses, m = v/u (NOT -v/u as in mirrors).
  • m is positive for virtual (erect) images, negative for real (inverted) images.
Power of Lens

Power of a lens is the ability to converge or diverge light. It is the reciprocal of focal length (in metres):

P = 1 / f (in metres)
  • Unit: Dioptre (D).
  • Convex lens: Power is positive (P > 0).
  • Concave lens: Power is negative (P < 0).
  • Higher power = shorter focal length = stronger bending of light.
  • Example: A lens with f = +50 cm = 0.5 m → P = 1/0.5 = +2 D.
Sign Convention for Lenses
QuantityConvex LensConcave Lens
Focal length (f)Positive (+)Negative (-)
Object distance (u)Negative (-)Negative (-)
Image distance (v)Positive (real) / Negative (virtual)Negative (-) (always virtual)
Power (P)Positive (+)Negative (-)

Numerical Example:

A 5 cm tall object is placed 20 cm from a convex lens of focal length 10 cm. Find image position, size, and nature.

Solution: u = -20 cm, f = +10 cm. 1/v = 1/f + 1/u = 1/10 + 1/(-20) = 1/10 - 1/20 = 1/20 → v = +20 cm. m = v/u = 20/(-20) = -1. hᵢ = -1 × 5 = -5 cm. Image is real, inverted, same size (5 cm), at 2F₂.

Notice: Mirror formula is 1/f = 1/v + 1/u. Lens formula is 1/f = 1/v - 1/u. The minus is the only difference! For lenses, u is always negative, so 1/v - 1/(-|u|) = 1/v + 1/|u| — this is why the lens formula effectively becomes 1/f = 1/v + 1/|u|.

10. Dispersion of Light

Dispersion — What & How?

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its constituent colours when it passes through a prism. This happens because different colours have different wavelengths and bend by different amounts.

ColourWavelengthBending in Prism
VioletShortest (~400 nm)Bends the most (deviated maximum)
RedLongest (~700 nm)Bends the least (deviated minimum)
Prism
  • A prism is a transparent object with triangular cross-section — two triangular bases and three rectangular faces.
  • When white light enters a prism, it refracts (bends) twice — once at entry and once at exit.
  • Since violet bends more than red, the colours spread out into a spectrum (VIBGYOR).
  • The angle of deviation is different for each colour.
Rainbow Formation
  • A rainbow is a natural spectrum of sunlight formed by dispersion + total internal reflection in water droplets.
  • Sunlight enters a raindrop → refracts (dispersion) → reflects from the back of the drop → refracts again as it exits.
  • We see the rainbow as a circular arc of VIBGYOR colours.
  • Rainbow is always seen with the Sun behind us and rain in front.
  • Secondary rainbow has reversed colour order (red inside, violet outside) due to two internal reflections.
Mnemonic for Colour Order (Increasing λ): "Violet Is In Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red" — V I B G Y O R. Or remember the name: VIBGYOR.
Memory Aid: Violet bends most (highest frequency, shortest wavelength). Red bends least (lowest frequency, longest wavelength). Think: Violet = Very bendy, Red = Relaxed bend.

11. Human Eye — Structure & Defects

Structure of the Human Eye
PartFunction
CorneaTransparent front covering; does most of the refraction (about 2/3 of total power).
IrisColoured part that controls the amount of light entering the eye.
PupilOpening in the iris that expands/dilates to control light entry.
Crystalline LensBiconvex lens behind the cornea; changes shape for focusing (accommodation).
Ciliary MusclesMuscles that change the lens shape (contract → lens thickens for near objects; relax → lens thins for far objects).
RetinaLight-sensitive screen at the back of the eye; contains rods and cones.

Blind SpotPoint where optic nerve leaves the eye — no light-sensitive cells here.
Accommodation

Accommodation is the ability of the eye lens to adjust its focal length to see objects at different distances. The ciliary muscles change the curvature of the lens: for near objects, muscles contract → lens becomes thicker (shorter f). For far objects, muscles relax → lens becomes thinner (longer f).

Near Point, Far Point & Range of Vision
  • Near point (least distance of distinct vision) = 25 cm for a normal eye.
  • Far point = Infinity for a normal eye.

Analogy: The eye is like a camera. Cornea = camera lens cover, Iris = aperture (f-stop), Pupil = aperture opening, Lens = focusing ring, Retina = film/sensor. Accommodation = auto-focus. Myopia = camera that can't focus on far objects. Hypermetropia = camera that can't focus on nearby objects.

13. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

EM Spectrum Order (Increasing Frequency): "Rabbits Mate In Very Unusual eXpensive Gardens"
R = Radio, M = Microwave, I = Infrared, V = Visible, U = Ultraviolet, X = X-ray, G = Gamma.
Or: "Ricky Martin Is Very Unhappy eXcept on Good days".
Concave vs Convex — Spoon Trick: Hold a spoon: the inside (hollow) = Concave (C = Cave = Hollow). The outside (back) = Convex (X = eXpand = Bulging). Concave focuses like a funnel. Convex spreads like a dome.
Myopia vs Hypermetropia: "Myopia = Minus power" (M→M: Myopia→Minus/Concave). "Hypermetropia = Plus power" (H→P: Hyper→Plus/Convex).
"Myopia = See near (M = near)" — can't see far. "Hypermetropia = See far (H = far)" — can't see near.

1. The speed of light in vacuum is:
A) 3 × 10⁶ m/s
B) 3 × 10⁸ m/s
C) 3 × 10¹⁰ m/s
D) 3 × 10⁴ m/s
Answer: B) 3 × 10⁸ m/s
2. According to the laws of reflection, the angle of incidence is:
A) Half the angle of reflection
B) Equal to the angle of reflection
C) Twice the angle of reflection

3. Which mirror is used as a rear-view mirror in vehicles?

B) Concave mirror
C) Convex mirror

4. The focal length (f) of a spherical mirror is related to its radius of curvature (R) by:
A) f = R
B) f = 2R
C) f = R/2

5. A concave mirror is used as a shaving mirror because it gives:
A) Real, inverted, diminished image

6. When light passes from air to glass, its speed:
A) Increases
B) Decreases
C) Remains the same
D) Becomes zero
Answer: B) Decreases (glass is optically denser than air)

B) i < r (light bends away from normal)

10. The splitting of white light into its constituent colours is called:
A) Reflection
B) Refraction
C) Dispersion

B) Concave mirror only

14. The phenomenon of light that causes a pencil to appear bent when partially dipped in water is:
A) Reflection
B) Refraction
