1. Magnets – Types, Properties & Materials

A magnet is an object that produces a magnetic field and attracts materials like iron, nickel, and cobalt. The word "magnet" comes from the region Magnesia in ancient Greece where natural magnets (lodestones) were first found.

Types of Magnets
TypeSource / MethodExamplesStrength
Natural MagnetsFound in nature – iron ore (Fe₃O₄) called lodestone or magnetiteLodestone (Magnetite)Weak (permanent)
Artificial Permanent MagnetsMan-made by magnetising steel/iron alloysBar magnet, horseshoe magnet, U-shaped magnet, ring magnetModerate to strong

Basic Properties of Magnets
  • Attracts magnetic materials – iron, nickel, cobalt, and some alloys like steel. Non-magnetic materials like wood, plastic, glass, and paper are NOT attracted.
  • Two Poles – North & South – Every magnet has two poles. The end that points towards the geographic North is called the North Pole (N); the other end is the South Pole (S).
  • Like poles repel, unlike poles attract – N–N and S–S repel; N–S attract. This is the fundamental law of magnetism.
  • Poles exist in pairs – You cannot isolate a single pole. If you cut a bar magnet into two, each piece becomes a complete magnet with its own N and S poles.
  • Directive property – A freely suspended magnet always aligns itself in the N–S direction (used in compasses).

Analogy: Think of ferromagnetic materials like loyal fans — they strongly follow the magnet's lead and become fans themselves. Paramagnetic materials are like shy fans — they follow weakly. Diamagnetic materials are like rebels — they push away from the magnet!

2. Magnetic Field – Lines, Patterns & Earth's Magnetism

Definition

The magnetic field is the region around a magnet (or a current-carrying conductor) where another magnet or magnetic material experiences a force. It is represented by magnetic field lines (also called lines of force).

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines
  • Magnetic field lines are closed continuous curves. Outside the magnet, they go from North to South. Inside the magnet, they go from South to North.
  • They never intersect each other. If they did, at the point of intersection the compass needle would point in two directions — impossible!
  • The closeness of lines indicates field strength. Dense lines = strong field (near poles). Sparse lines = weak field.
  • The direction of the field at any point is the direction in which a North pole would move if placed there.
  • Field lines are crowded at the poles and spread out as they move away, showing that the magnetic field is strongest near the poles.
Analogy: Magnetic field lines are like the paths ants follow around their colony — they form closed loops from N to S outside the magnet and S to N inside, just like ants leaving from one entrance and returning through another.
Earth's Magnetism

The Earth itself behaves like a giant bar magnet with its magnetic field extending far into space. This is why a compass needle always points North.

  • Magnetic North Pole is near the geographic South Pole. Wait – confusing? Here's the key: The geographic North Pole of Earth acts as the magnetic South pole (because the North pole of a compass needle is attracted to it).
  • Magnetic Declination – The angle between the geographic meridian (true north) and the magnetic meridian (direction of compass needle). This varies with location.
  • Magnetic Inclination (Dip) – The angle that the Earth's magnetic field makes with the horizontal plane. At the magnetic equator, dip = 0°. At magnetic poles, dip = 90°.

3. Magnetic Field Due to Electric Current

Oersted's Experiment (1820)

Hans Christian Oersted discovered that a current-carrying wire deflects a nearby compass needle. This was the first evidence linking electricity and magnetism.

  • Setup: A wire is placed parallel to a compass needle. When current flows, the needle deflects.
  • Conclusion: Electric current produces a magnetic field around it.
  • The direction of deflection depends on the direction of current.
Magnetic Field of a Straight Current-Carrying Conductor

The magnetic field lines around a straight wire are concentric circles with the wire at the centre. The strength of the field depends on:

  • Current (I) – Higher current → stronger field (directly proportional).
  • Distance (r) – Farther from wire → weaker field (inversely proportional).
Right-Hand Thumb Rule (Maxwell's Corkscrew Rule)

Imagine holding the current-carrying wire in your right hand with your thumb pointing in the direction of current. Then your curled fingers show the direction of magnetic field lines.

Magnetic Field of a Circular Loop
  • When current flows through a circular loop, the magnetic field lines are concentrated at the centre of the loop.
  • The field at the centre is perpendicular to the plane of the loop.
  • Strength depends on: Number of turns (N), Current (I), Radius (r). More turns → stronger field. Larger radius → weaker field.
  • The field can be increased by: using more turns, using larger current, or reducing radius.
Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire with many turns, shaped like a cylindrical helix. When current flows through it, it behaves like a bar magnet with a North Pole at one end and a South Pole at the other.

  • The magnetic field inside a solenoid is uniform and parallel (like a bar magnet's internal field).
  • Outside the solenoid, the field pattern is similar to that of a bar magnet.
  • Uses: Electromagnets, doorbells, MRI machines, switches and relays.
Analogy: A solenoid is like a slinky toy with electricity — each turn is like a tiny circular loop, and together they combine to produce a strong uniform field, just like many small fans blowing in the same direction create a strong wind.

4. Electromagnet – Construction, Working & Uses

What is an Electromagnet?

An electromagnet is a temporary magnet made by passing electric current through a coil of wire wrapped around a soft iron core. It behaves as a magnet only when current flows through it.

Construction
  • Take a soft iron rod (the core).
  • Wind a coil of insulated copper wire around it (many turns).
  • Connect the ends of the wire to a battery through a switch.
Working

When current flows through the coil, a magnetic field is produced. The soft iron core becomes strongly magnetised. When the current is switched off, the core loses its magnetism almost completely because soft iron has low retentivity (does not retain magnetism).

5. Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

Motor Effect

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force. This is called the motor effect — because this is the principle behind electric motors.

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule (Motor Rule)

Hold your left hand so that the thumb, forefinger (index finger), and middle finger are perpendicular to each other:

  • Forefinger – points in the direction of the Magnetic Field (N → S).
  • Middle finger – points in the direction of the Current (positive to negative).
  • Thumb – points in the direction of Force or Motion (thrust).
Mnemonic: "FBI" – Forefinger = Field, Thumb = Thrust (Force), Middle finger = Current. Or "Left hand = Motor rule" for remembering which hand to use.
Factors Affecting the Force

The magnitude of force depends on:

  • Magnetic Field Strength (B) – Stronger field → larger force.
  • Current (I) – Larger current → larger force.
  • Length of Conductor (L) inside the field – Longer conductor → larger force.
  • Angle between conductor and field (θ) – Maximum force when conductor is perpendicular to field (θ = 90°); zero when parallel (θ = 0°).
F = B × I × L × sin θ
Analogy: Imagine pushing a shopping cart — if you push from the side (perpendicular), it moves easily. If you push from the front (parallel), it does nothing! Similarly, the force on a conductor is maximum when it's perpendicular to the magnetic field.

6. Electric Motor – Principle, Construction & Working

Principle

An electric motor works on the principle that a current-carrying coil placed in a magnetic field experiences a torque (rotational force) and rotates. This is based on the motor effect (Fleming's Left-Hand Rule).

Construction of a Simple DC Motor
PartMaterialFunction
Armature (Coil)Rectangular coil of insulated copper wire wound on a soft iron coreRotates when current flows; the core concentrates the magnetic field
MagnetPermanent magnet or electromagnet (horseshoe shape often)Provides the magnetic field in which the coil rotates
Split Rings (Commutator)Two halves of a copper ring, split and insulated from each otherReverses the direction of current in the coil every half-turn so the coil keeps rotating in the same direction
BrushesCarbon rods that press against the split ringsConduct current from the battery to the split rings
Battery / Power SupplyDC sourceProvides the electric current
AxleMetal rodTransmits rotation from armature to outside load
Working of a DC Motor
  • Step 1: Current enters the coil through brush → split ring → flows through armature.
  • Step 2: The two sides of the coil experience opposite forces (due to Fleming's LHR — current directions are opposite on the two sides).
  • Step 3: This creates a couple (torque) that rotates the coil.
  • Step 4: After half a rotation, the split ring (commutator) swaps the contacts, reversing the current direction in the coil.
  • Step 5: This ensures the coil continues to rotate in the same direction (instead of oscillating back and forth).
AC Motor vs DC Motor
PropertyDC MotorAC Motor
Power SourceDirect Current (battery)Alternating Current (mains)
CommutatorSplit rings (2 halves)No commutator; uses slip rings
Speed ControlEasy (adjust voltage)Harder (requires frequency control)
Starting TorqueHighLow
ApplicationsElectric vehicles, toys, fans, drillsIndustrial machines, pumps, compressors, household fans
MaintenanceHigher (brush wear)Lower (brushless)
Applications of Electric Motors
  • Household: Ceiling fans, mixers, washing machines, vacuum cleaners, refrigerators, AC units.
  • Transport: Electric vehicles (EVs), trains, trams, elevators.
  • Industry: Pumps, conveyor belts, cranes, drills, lathe machines.
  • Small devices: Toys, electric razors, computer fans, hard disk drives.

7. Electromagnetic Induction – Faraday's Experiments

Definition

Electromagnetic Induction (EMI) is the process of generating an electric current in a conductor by changing the magnetic field around it. This is the opposite of the motor effect — here motion produces electricity instead of electricity producing motion.

Faraday's Experiments (1831)

Michael Faraday conducted a series of experiments showing that a changing magnetic field induces current in a nearby conductor.

  • Experiment 1: A coil connected to a galvanometer. When a bar magnet is moved towards or away from the coil, the galvanometer deflects — showing induced current.
  • Experiment 2: When the magnet is stationary inside the coil, no current flows (galvanometer shows zero). Current flows only when the magnet moves.
  • Experiment 3: Faster motion → larger deflection. Stronger magnet → larger deflection. More turns in coil → larger deflection.
  • Experiment 4: Reversing the magnet's direction reverses the direction of induced current.
Analogy: Electromagnetic induction is like a dance — when you move a magnet near a coil, the coil "feels" it and produces current, just like a dancer responding to their partner's movement. When the partner stops moving, the dance stops. Faster movement = more energetic response!
Fleming's Right-Hand Rule (Generator Rule)

Hold your right hand so that the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger are perpendicular to each other:

  • Forefinger – points in the direction of the Magnetic Field.
  • Thumb – points in the direction of Motion of the conductor.
  • Middle finger – points in the direction of Induced Current.
Factors Affecting Induced EMF / Current
  • Speed of motion – Faster relative motion → larger induced EMF.
  • Strength of magnet – Stronger magnet → larger induced EMF.
  • Number of turns (N) – More turns → larger induced EMF.
  • Area of the coil (A) – Larger area → larger induced EMF.
  • Rate of change of magnetic flux – This is the key! Faster change in flux → larger induced EMF.
Induced EMF (ε) = –N × (ΔΦ / Δt)  (Faraday's Law with Lenz's Law sign)

8. Electric Generator – AC Generator & DC Generator

Principle

An electric generator works on the principle of electromagnetic induction. When a coil is rotated in a magnetic field, the magnetic flux linked with the coil changes, which induces an EMF (and hence current) in the coil.

AC Generator (Alternator) – Construction
PartFunction
Field MagnetUsually an electromagnet that produces a strong magnetic field
Armature (Coil)Rectangular coil of insulated copper wire that is rotated in the magnetic field
Slip RingsTwo complete rings — each end of the coil is connected to one ring. The rings rotate with the coil.
BrushesCarbon brushes press against the slip rings to collect the induced current and send it to the external circuit
AC Generator – Working
  • Step 1: Coil is rotated in the magnetic field (by external mechanical energy — steam, water, wind, etc.).
  • Step 2: As the coil rotates, the magnetic flux through it changes continuously, inducing an alternating EMF.
  • Step 3: In one full rotation, the current direction reverses twice (hence the name alternating current).
  • Step 4: The slip rings ensure that the external circuit always receives current flowing in the same relative direction at each terminal — wait, no! The slip rings actually maintain the alternating nature of the current.
  • Key: With slip rings, the output is AC. With split rings (commutator), the output is DC.
AC Generator vs DC Generator
PropertyAC Generator (Alternator)DC Generator (Dynamo)
Output currentAlternating (changes direction periodically)Direct (flows in one direction)
Ring typeSlip rings (two complete rings)Split rings (Commutator)
Direction reversalCurrent reverses every half-turnCommutator reverses coil connections to produce unidirectional current
BrushesPress against slip ringsPress against commutator
EfficiencyHigher (no sparking at brushes)Lower (sparking at commutator)
MaintenanceLowerHigher (brush/commutator wear)
ApplicationsPower plants (thermal, hydro, nuclear, wind), most grid electricityBattery charging, electroplating, DC applications, old cars
Analogy: Think of a generator as a bicycle dynamo — when you pedal (rotate the coil), you generate electricity for the headlight. Stop pedalling, the light goes out! In power plants, steam or water does the "pedalling".

9. Domestic Electric Circuits & Safety

Household Wiring – Three Wires
WireColour Code (India)Potential / Function
Live Wire (Phase)Red or BrownCarries current from the supply at 220V (in India). Dangerous to touch!
Neutral WireBlack or BlueCompletes the circuit. At zero potential (connected to ground at the substation).
Earth Wire (Ground)Green or Yellow/GreenSafety wire – connected to the ground through a metal plate buried in the earth. Provides a low-resistance path for leakage current.
Parallel Circuits in Homes

All electrical appliances in a house are connected in parallel across the live and neutral wires. Why parallel is used:

  • Independent operation – Each appliance works independently. Switching off one doesn't affect others.
  • Same voltage – All appliances get the same voltage (220V in India), so they work at their designed power.
  • Individual control – Each appliance can have its own switch.
  • Current division – Total current = sum of individual currents, allowing each appliance to draw what it needs.
Safety Devices: Fuse & MCB
DeviceConstructionWorkingResettable?
FuseThin wire made of tin-lead alloy or copper with low melting point, housed in a cartridgeWhen current exceeds safe limit, the fuse wire melts and breaks the circuit. "Sacrificial" device.No (must be replaced)
MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker)Electromagnetic switch with a bimetallic stripDetects overload or short circuit and trips automatically to break the circuitYes (just flip back on)
Three-Pin Plug & Earthing
  • Three pins: Live (right, smaller), Neutral (left, smaller), Earth (top, larger and thicker). The earth pin is longer so it makes contact first when plugging in.
  • Earthing: If an appliance's metal body becomes live due to a fault (e.g., loose wire touching the metal case), current flows through the earth wire to the ground instead of through the user. This prevents electric shock.
  • Importance: All appliances with metal bodies (refrigerators, washing machines, iron boxes) MUST be earthed.
Electrical Safety Tips
  • Never touch electrical appliances with wet hands — water conducts electricity.
  • Always use three-pin plugs with earth connection for heavy appliances.
  • Do not overload a single socket — too many appliances drawing high current can overheat the wiring.
  • Use fuses/MCBs of proper rating — too high rating won't protect; too low rating will trip unnecessarily.
  • In case of fire, never use water on electrical fires — use sand or CO₂ extinguisher.
  • Always switch off the main supply before repairing any electrical appliance.

11. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Fleming's Hand Rules
Fleming's Rules: "Left = Motor, Right = Generator". Count letters: Left has 4 letters, Motor has 5 — but both start with M-ish sounds. Better: "LMR" = Left = Motor. "RIG" = Right = Generator (RIG sounds like "rig up" electricity).
FBI for Left Hand (Motor): Forefinger = Field, Thumb = Thrust, Middle = Current. Or use "Fleming's Left Hand = Force" — the thumb gives the direction of Force (motion).
Magnetic Materials: "Ferro = Fe" (Iron loves magnets). "Para = Pt, Al" (Platinum and Aluminium are slightly attracted). "Diama = Cu, Bi" (Copper and Bismuth are repelled by magnets). Trick: "F-P-D" sounds like "F-P-D" → "Firmly attracted, Partially attracted, Distantly repelled".
Earth's Magnetism: The geographic North Pole is actually the magnetic South pole. Trick: "Opposites attract" — the North pole of your compass (which is a North pole) is attracted to the South pole of Earth's magnet. So Earth's magnetic South is near the geographic North.
Right-Hand Thumb Rule
Right-Hand Thumb Rule: "Thumb = Current, Curled fingers = Field". Think of a corkscrew — as you turn the corkscrew (current direction), it goes into the cork (field direction). "Right hand for a straight wire".

12. Practice Questions – 15 MCQs for BPSC / UPSC

Click any question to reveal the answer.

1. Who discovered the connection between electricity and magnetism?
A) Michael Faraday
B) Hans Christian Oersted
C) James Clerk Maxwell
D) Andre-Marie Ampere
Answer: B) Hans Christian Oersted (1820) – observed that a current-carrying wire deflects a compass needle.
2. The magnetic field inside a solenoid is:
A) Zero
B) Non-uniform
C) Uniform and parallel
D) Radial
Answer: C) Uniform and parallel (the field lines inside a solenoid are parallel and equally spaced).
3. Fleming's Left-Hand Rule is used to find the direction of:
A) Induced current in a generator
B) Force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field
C) Magnetic field around a straight wire
D) Magnetic field of a solenoid
Answer: B) Force (motion) on a current-carrying conductor – used for motors.
4. In a DC motor, the commutator is made of:
A) Slip rings
B) Split rings
C) Carbon brushes
D) Soft iron core
Answer: B) Split rings (the commutator reverses current direction every half-turn).
5. Which of the following is NOT a ferromagnetic material?
A) Iron
B) Cobalt
C) Aluminium
D) Nickel
Answer: C) Aluminium – it is paramagnetic, not ferromagnetic. Fe, Ni, Co are ferromagnetic.
6. The angle between Earth's magnetic field and the horizontal is called:
A) Declination
B) Inclination (Dip)
C) Latitude
D) Azimuth
Answer: B) Inclination (Dip) – 0° at magnetic equator, 90° at magnetic poles.
7. An electric generator converts:
A) Electrical energy to mechanical energy
B) Mechanical energy to electrical energy
C) Chemical energy to electrical energy
D) Heat energy to electrical energy
Answer: B) Mechanical → Electrical (based on electromagnetic induction).
8. Which part of an AC generator ensures that the output current is alternating?
A) Split rings
B) Slip rings
C) Brushes
D) Armature
Answer: B) Slip rings – they maintain the alternating nature of the induced current.
9. The earth wire in domestic circuits is coloured:
A) Red
B) Black
C) Green
D) Blue
Answer: C) Green (or yellow/green) – earth wire for safety.
10. Which of the following devices uses an electromagnet?
A) Compass
B) Refrigerator magnet
C) Electric doorbell
D) Bar magnet
Answer: C) Electric doorbell – an electromagnet pulls the striker to hit the bell.
11. The force on a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field is maximum when the angle between the conductor and the field is:
A) 0°
B) 45°
C) 60°
D) 90°
Answer: D) 90° (perpendicular) – F = BIL sinθ, max when sinθ = 1, θ = 90°.
12. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is:
A) Weber
B) Tesla
C) Gauss
D) Henry
Answer: B) Tesla (T). 1 T = 10⁴ Gauss. Named after Nikola Tesla.
13. Which rule is used to determine the direction of induced current in a generator?
A) Fleming's Left-Hand Rule
B) Fleming's Right-Hand Rule
C) Right-Hand Thumb Rule
D) Left-Hand Thumb Rule
Answer: B) Fleming's Right-Hand Rule – used for generators (motion → induced current).
14. In domestic wiring, appliances are connected in:
A) Series
B) Parallel
C) Series-parallel combination
D) Depends on the appliance
Answer: B) Parallel – so each appliance gets the same voltage and works independently.
15. A fuse wire should have:
A) High melting point and high resistance
B) Low melting point and low resistance
C) Low melting point and high resistance
D) High melting point and low resistance
Answer: C) Low melting point and high resistance – so it heats up quickly and melts when excess current flows.
