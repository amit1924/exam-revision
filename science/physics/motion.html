1. Motion – Distance, Displacement, Speed & Velocity

Motion is a change in position of an object with time. When an object changes its position with respect to a fixed reference point, we say it is in motion. Everything in the universe is in motion – even the Earth is spinning!

Distance vs Displacement

Think of it this way: distance is the total path you walked, displacement is how far you are from home as the crow flies.

Speed vs Velocity

Speed tells you how fast you are going. Velocity tells you how fast and in which direction.

Uniform vs Non-uniform Motion

  • Uniform Motion: Object covers equal distances in equal intervals of time (no matter how small the interval). Example: A car cruising at constant 40 km/h on a straight highway.
  • Non-uniform Motion: Object covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time. Example: A bus moving through traffic – sometimes fast, sometimes slow.
Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. In simple words: how quickly your speed (or direction) changes.

  • Formula: a = (v – u) / t where v = final velocity, u = initial velocity, t = time
  • SI Unit: m/s² (metre per second squared)
  • Positive acceleration: velocity increases (speeding up) – e.g., a car starting from rest
  • Negative acceleration (Retardation/Deceleration): velocity decreases (slowing down) – e.g., brakes applied
  • Uniform acceleration: velocity changes by equal amount in equal time intervals – e.g., free fall under gravity (g = 9.8 m/s²)
  • Non-uniform acceleration: velocity changes by unequal amounts in equal time intervals
Equations of Motion (Constant Acceleration)

These three equations are the holy grail of kinematics. They apply only when acceleration is constant (uniform).

#EquationMissing VariableWhat it tells you
1v = u + ats (displacement)Final velocity after time t

Variables: u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time, s = displacement

Analogy for Equations of Motion

Imagine you are on a train that starts at rest (u=0) and picks up speed steadily (uniform acceleration). The equations help you predict exactly where you'll be after 10 seconds, how fast you'll be going, and how much track you'll cover.

Graphical Analysis
  • Distance–Time Graph: A straight line = uniform speed; curved line = non-uniform speed. Slope = speed.
  • Velocity–Time Graph: Straight horizontal line = uniform velocity; sloping line = uniform acceleration. Slope = acceleration. Area under graph = displacement.
  • Acceleration–Time Graph: Horizontal line = constant acceleration. Area under graph = change in velocity.
Remember: If a velocity–time graph is a straight line sloping upwards, the object has uniform acceleration. The steeper the slope, the greater the acceleration.

2. Scalar vs Vector Quantities

Scalars only have magnitude (size/number). Vectors have both magnitude AND direction. Think: "Vector has a direction arrow; Scalar is just a number."

More Examples for Easy Recall
  • Scalar: "The temperature is 30°C" – no direction needed. "My mass is 70 kg."
  • Vector: "Wind is blowing 20 km/h towards East" – direction matters. "Apply a force of 10 N upward."
  • Trick: If you can answer "which way?" for a quantity, it's a vector!
Quick Tip: Distance + Direction = Displacement. Speed + Direction = Velocity. Always add a direction to convert a scalar into a vector!

3. Uniform Circular Motion (UCM)

Uniform Circular Motion is the motion of an object moving along a circular path with constant speed. Even though speed is constant, the velocity keeps changing because direction changes at every point!

Key Concepts
  • Speed is constant, but velocity is NOT constant (direction changes) → this means the object is accelerating!
  • This acceleration is called centripetal acceleration – it always points towards the centre of the circle.
  • Formula for centripetal acceleration: ac = v² / r (where v = speed, r = radius)
  • Force required for UCM: Fc = mv² / r (centripetal force)
Real-Life Examples
  • A stone tied to a string swung in a circle
  • Earth revolving around the Sun (almost circular orbit)
  • A car taking a turn on a circular road
  • Satellites orbiting Earth
  • A merry-go-round / Ferris wheel
  • Electrons revolving around the nucleus (simplified model)

Think: When you swing a bucket of water in a vertical circle, the water does not fall because of centripetal force. If the string breaks, the stone flies off tangentially (not radially) – that's Newton's First Law in action!

4. Newton's Laws of Motion

Sir Isaac Newton (1642–1727) gave three laws that form the foundation of classical mechanics. These laws describe how objects move when forces act on them.

First Law – Law of Inertia

Statement: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by an external unbalanced force.

  • Inertia is the natural tendency of objects to resist change in their state of motion. "Matter resists change" – more mass = more inertia.
  • If you are in a bus that suddenly stops, you lurch forward – your body wants to keep moving (inertia of motion).
  • If you are in a bus that suddenly starts, you fall backward – your body wants to stay at rest (inertia of rest).
Analogy: Think of inertia as "laziness" of objects. A heavy box is "lazy" – it does not want to start moving, and once moving, it does not want to stop. The heavier it is, the lazier!
Second Law – Law of Momentum / F = ma

Statement: The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force, and takes place in the direction of the force.

  • Momentum (p) = mass × velocity = p = mv. Unit: kg·m/s
  • Force = F = ma (mass × acceleration). Because F = Δp/Δt = m·Δv/Δt = ma
  • Newton's Second Law gives us the definition of force: 1 Newton = force required to give 1 kg mass an acceleration of 1 m/s².
  • Impulse: Force × time = change in momentum (F·Δt = Δp). That's why airbags save lives – they increase time of impact, reducing force.
Real-Life Example: A cricket fielder pulls his hands back while catching a ball. He increases the time of impact (Δt increases), so the force (F) decreases – the ball does not hurt his hands. Same impulse, more time = less force.
Third Law – Action-Reaction

Statement: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. These two forces act on different bodies.

  • Action and reaction are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.
  • They act on two different bodies – that's why they do NOT cancel each other out.
  • You cannot touch without being touched! When you push a wall, the wall pushes you back with the same force.
Think of it as: When you walk, your foot pushes the ground backward (action). The ground pushes your foot forward (reaction) – that reaction is what moves you forward! Without friction, walking would be impossible.

5. Conservation of Momentum

Definition & Law

Law of Conservation of Momentum: In an isolated system (no external force), the total momentum before collision equals the total momentum after collision. Momentum is neither created nor destroyed – it is only transferred.

Derivation (using Newton's Laws)

Consider two balls A and B of masses m₁ and m₂ moving with velocities u₁ and u₂. They collide and after collision have velocities v₁ and v₂.

  • Force on A due to B: FAB = rate of change of momentum of A = (m₁v₁ – m₁u₁) / t
  • Force on B due to A: FBA = rate of change of momentum of B = (m₂v₂ – m₂u₂) / t
  • By Newton's Third Law: FAB = –FBA
  • So: (m₁v₁ – m₁u₁) / t = – (m₂v₂ – m₂u₂) / t
  • Simplifying: m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
  • This proves: Total momentum before collision = Total momentum after collision
Example 1: Recoil of a Gun

When a bullet is fired from a gun, the gun recoils (jerks backward). Before firing, both gun and bullet are at rest → total momentum = 0.

  • Let M = mass of gun, m = mass of bullet, V = recoil velocity of gun, v = velocity of bullet
  • After firing: 0 = MV + mv (total momentum conserved)
  • So: V = – (mv) / M (negative sign = opposite direction)
  • The gun recoils backward. Heavier the gun, smaller the recoil velocity.
Example 2: Rocket Propulsion
  • A rocket works on the same principle – conservation of momentum.
  • Hot gases are expelled downward at high speed (action). The rocket moves upward (reaction).
  • Momentum of (rocket + fuel) before = 0. As gases go down, rocket goes up to keep total momentum = 0.
  • Formula: mgvg = mrvr (mass of gases × velocity = mass of rocket × velocity of rocket)
  • This is why Newton's Third Law + Conservation of Momentum makes space travel possible!
Analogy: Imagine you are on a frictionless skateboard and you throw a heavy ball forward. What happens? You roll backward! The ball's forward momentum equals your backward momentum. Rocket works the same way – just with hot gases instead of a ball.

6. Friction

Friction is a force that opposes the relative motion between two surfaces in contact. Think of it as the "resistance" that surfaces offer when one slides over another.

Types of Friction
TypeDescriptionExampleMagnitude
Static FrictionFriction between two surfaces that are NOT moving relative to each other. It prevents motion from starting.Pushing a heavy box that does not move; a book resting on a slanted table without slidingMaximum of all three
Sliding (Kinetic) FrictionFriction between two surfaces that ARE sliding relative to each other.A box sliding across the floor; sliding a chairMedium (less than static)
Rolling FrictionFriction when one surface rolls over another.A ball rolling on ground; wheels of a car; roller skatesMinimum of all three
Key Comparison: Static > Sliding > Rolling friction. That's why we use wheels (rolling friction) instead of dragging things (sliding friction). And why it's harder to start pushing a car than to keep it moving.
Advantages of Friction
  • Walking is possible because friction between shoes and ground gives grip
  • Vehicles can brake and turn on roads due to friction between tyres and road
  • Writing with a pen/pencil requires friction between tip and paper
  • Lighting a matchstick works by friction
  • Nails and screws stay in walls because of friction
  • Belt drives in machines use friction to transmit power
Disadvantages of Friction
  • Causes wear and tear of moving parts (engine parts, shoe soles, tyres)
  • Produces heat – engines overheat without proper cooling
  • Wastes energy – you need extra fuel to overcome friction
  • Reduces efficiency of machines
  • Causes noise in moving machinery
Methods to Reduce Friction
  • Lubricants (oil, grease, graphite) – fill the irregularities between surfaces
  • Polishing surfaces – makes them smoother
  • Ball bearings – convert sliding friction into rolling friction (much less)
  • Streamlining – aerodynamic shapes reduce fluid friction (air resistance)
  • Using wheels instead of dragging
  • Applying powders (talcum) between surfaces
Methods to Increase Friction
  • Treads on tyres and shoe soles – create more grip
  • Grooved surfaces (sandpaper, rough floor tiles)
  • Using brake pads in vehicles
  • Spikes on sports shoes for better grip on ground
  • Sand or gravel on icy roads to increase friction
Interesting Fact: Without friction, you could not walk, cars could not move, and even a nail would not stay in the wall. But too much friction wastes energy. That's why engineers balance friction – enough for grip, but not too much for efficiency.

8. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Three Equations of Motion
"v = u + at" – Think "Very Useful + Always True". "s = ut + ½at²" – Think "See You Tomorrow + Half At Two". "v² = u² + 2as" – Think "Velocity Square = U Square + 2 A S".
What's Missing in Each Equation
"Shoes = v = u + at" (missing s – displacement). "Tie = s = ut + ½at²" (missing t – time? No! v is missing). Actually think: "uva – s" = first eqn has u, v, a, t → missing s. "usa – v" = second has u, s, a, t → missing v. "usv – t" = third has u, s, v, a → missing t.
Newton's Laws in One Line
"Newton 1 = Lazy Objects" (inertia = laziness). "Newton 2 = F = ma" (force makes things accelerate). "Newton 3 = Push = Push Back" (action = reaction). Or remember: "No Force → No Change" (1st), "Force → Acceleration" (2nd), "Force → Opposite Force" (3rd).
Friction Types – Order
"SSR" = Static > Sliding > Rolling. Trick: "Super Strong Resistance" (Static = Super, Sliding = Strong, Rolling = Resistance). Or: "Stop, Slide, Roll" – the force needed to stop (static) > slide (sliding) > roll (rolling).
Scalar vs Vector – Key Examples
"Scar = M.D.T.T.E.W.P" – Scalar examples: Mass, Distance, Time, Temperature, Energy, Work, Power. "Very = D.V.A.F.M" – Vector examples: Displacement, Velocity, Acceleration, Force, Momentum. Trick: "Scar" = scalar, "Very" = vector.
