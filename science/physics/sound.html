1. Production of Sound

Sound is produced by vibrations — the back-and-forth motion of an object. Think of a guitar string: when you pluck it, it vibrates and you hear a sound. No vibration = no sound.

Vibration: The Source of All Sound
  • Every sound originates from a vibrating object. When the object moves to-and-fro, it disturbs the surrounding air particles.
  • If you touch your throat while speaking, you can feel the vocal cords vibrating. That vibration is what produces your voice.
  • A tuning fork has two prongs (tines) that vibrate when struck. The vibration is visible if you dip the prongs in water — you'll see ripples!
How Vocal Cords Work

In our larynx (voice box), two bands of tissue called vocal cords are stretched across the windpipe. When we speak, air from the lungs rushes past these cords, making them vibrate. The vibration produces sound waves. Men have longer, thicker cords → deeper voice. Women have shorter, thinner cords → higher voice.

Analogy: Your vocal cords are like rubber bands stretched across a box. Pluck a thick rubber band — you get a low sound. Pluck a thin one — you get a high sound. Same with your vocal cords!

TypeHow Sound is ProducedExamples

Remember: A vibrating object produces sound, but the object itself does NOT move from its place — only the energy travels. Like a pendulum: it swings but stays in one location.

2. Propagation of Sound

Sound waves are like ripples in a pond — the disturbance travels outward but the water itself stays put. Sound needs a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel. It cannot travel through vacuum.

Sound Needs a Medium
  • Sound is a mechanical wave — it needs particles to pass along the energy.
  • In a vacuum (empty space with no particles), sound cannot travel. This is why in space, no one can hear you scream!
  • Bell Jar Experiment: Place an electric bell inside a glass jar. Evacuate the air with a vacuum pump. The sound of the bell fades away as the air is removed. When air is let back in, the sound returns.
Analogy: Sound needs a medium just like a train needs tracks. Without tracks, the train cannot move. Without particles (solid/liquid/gas), sound cannot travel. Simple!
Longitudinal Waves: Compression & Rarefaction

Sound waves in air are longitudinal waves — the particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel. Think of a slinky pushed and pulled along its length.

  • Compression (C): Region where particles are pushed together (high pressure, high density). Represented by crests in graphs.
  • Rarefaction (R): Region where particles are spread apart (low pressure, low density). Represented by troughs in graphs.
  • One full wave = one Compression + one Rarefaction.
Analogy: Imagine a train of dominoes. When you push the first domino, it falls and knocks the next, and so on — the push travels forward, but each domino stays roughly where it is. That's exactly how sound propagates: the energy moves forward but the air particles just vibrate back and forth.
Speed in Different Media

Sound travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases. Why? Because particles are packed more tightly in solids, so the energy transfer is quicker.

MediumSpeed (approx. m/s)Why?
Solids (e.g., Steel)~5000 – 6000 m/sParticles tightly packed, energy transfers fast
Liquids (e.g., Water)~1400 – 1500 m/sParticles moderately close
Gases (e.g., Air at 20°C)~343 m/sParticles far apart, slowest transfer

3. Characteristics of Sound

Sound is described by five key quantities. Think of them as the "ID card" of a sound wave — they tell you everything about it.

Key Terms
QuantitySymbolDefinitionUnit
AmplitudeAMaximum displacement of a vibrating particle from its mean positionmetre (m)
FrequencyfNumber of complete oscillations per secondHertz (Hz)
Time PeriodTTime taken to complete one oscillation (T = 1/f)second (s)
Wavelengthλ (lambda)Distance between two consecutive compressions (or rarefactions)metre (m)
VelocityvSpeed at which the wave travels through the mediumm/s
The Wave Equation: v = fλ

The velocity of a sound wave is the product of its frequency and wavelength.

v = f × λ
  • v = velocity of sound in the medium (m/s)
  • f = frequency (Hz)
  • λ = wavelength (m)
  • Also: f = 1/T, where T = time period

Example: A tuning fork of 256 Hz produces a sound wave of wavelength 1.34 m in air. What is the speed? v = 256 × 1.34 = 343 m/s.

Analogy — Amplitude: Amplitude is like how hard you hit a drum. Hit it softly → small amplitude → quiet sound. Hit it hard → large amplitude → loud sound. Simple!
Analogy — Frequency: Frequency is like the speed of a train's wheel clicks. Slow clicks = low frequency = deep sound. Fast clicks = high frequency = shrill sound.
Analogy — Wavelength: Wavelength is like the distance between two consecutive waves in a rope. If you shake a rope slowly, the loops (wavelength) are long. Shake fast — short loops!

Speed in Different Media

General rule: Solid > Liquid > Gas. Sound travels fastest in solids because molecules are tightly packed and can transfer energy quickly.

Factors Affecting Speed of Sound
  • Temperature: Speed increases with temperature. In air, speed increases by about 0.6 m/s per °C. So sound travels faster on a hot day than a cold day. v ∝ √T (T = absolute temperature).
  • Pressure: At a given temperature, speed of sound in a gas is independent of pressure (for ideal gases).
  • Humidity: Speed increases with humidity. Moist air is less dense than dry air, so sound travels faster in humid air. This is why sound carries better during the rainy season.
  • Density of Medium: For gases, speed is inversely proportional to √(density). Lighter gases (like hydrogen) transmit sound faster than heavier gases.
Exam Tip: Speed of sound in air at 20°C = 343 m/s (or roughly 330 m/s at 0°C). This is the standard value asked in almost every competitive exam. Remember it!
Lightning & Thunder

You see lightning before you hear thunder because light travels much faster (~3 × 10⁸ m/s) than sound (~343 m/s). Count the seconds between flash and thunder: divide by 3 to get the distance in kilometres.

5. Loudness, Pitch & Quality

Three properties that let us distinguish one sound from another. Even if two sounds have the same pitch and loudness, we can tell them apart by quality (timbre).

Loudness
  • Depends on amplitude of vibration. Greater amplitude → louder sound.
  • Measured in decibels (dB). The faintest sound humans can hear is 0 dB.
  • Loudness ≠ Intensity. Loudness is a subjective sensation; intensity is the physical energy carried by the wave.
  • Normal conversation: ~60 dB. Whisper: ~20 dB. Jet engine: ~140 dB (painful!).
Analogy: Loudness is like turning up the volume on a speaker. The music doesn't change — it just gets louder. Amplitude is what the volume knob controls.
Pitch
  • Depends on frequency. Higher frequency → higher pitch (shrill sound). Lower frequency → lower pitch (flat/deep sound).
  • Examples: A mosquito's buzz is high-pitched (high frequency). A lion's roar is low-pitched (low frequency).
  • A woman's voice is generally higher pitched than a man's because her vocal cords are shorter and vibrate faster.
Quality (Timbre)
  • Timbre (pronounced "tam-ber") is the quality that distinguishes two sounds of the same pitch and loudness.
  • Example: The same note played on a flute and a violin sound different because of different overtones (additional frequencies) produced.
  • Timbre depends on the waveform shape — different instruments produce different wave patterns even for the same frequency.

6. Infrasound & Ultrasound

The human ear can hear frequencies between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). Sounds outside this range are either too low (infrasound) or too high (ultrasound) for us to hear.

Frequency Spectrum
CategoryFrequency RangeWho can hear?
Infrasound< 20 HzElephants, whales, rhinoceroses, pigeons (used for long-distance communication)
Audible sound20 Hz – 20,000 HzHumans
Ultrasound> 20,000 Hz (20 kHz)Dogs, bats, dolphins, cats, mice
Applications of Ultrasound
  • Medical Imaging (Sonography): Ultrasound waves are sent into the body; reflected echoes create images of internal organs. Safe (no radiation), used for pregnancy scans, detecting tumours, gallstones.
  • Breaking Kidney Stones: High-intensity ultrasound waves (lithotripsy) focus on kidney stones and shatter them into tiny pieces that pass out naturally.
  • Cleaning: Ultrasonic cleaners use high-frequency waves to remove dirt from delicate items like jewellery, watches, and surgical instruments. The waves create tiny bubbles that explode (cavitation) and scrub surfaces.
  • Welding & Cutting: Ultrasonic vibrations are used to weld plastics and metals without heat. Also used for cutting food products and medical devices.
  • SONAR: Used for underwater navigation, depth measurement, and detecting submarines (covered in Section 8).
  • Echo-location in Animals: Bats and dolphins use ultrasound to echolocate. They emit high-frequency clicks and listen to the echoes to "see" their surroundings.
  • Industrial Testing: Ultrasound detects cracks and defects in metal structures (non-destructive testing or NDT).
Interesting Fact: Bats are not blind — they can see, but they rely mostly on echolocation using ultrasound (20 kHz to 200 kHz). A bat can detect an insect as thin as a human hair in total darkness! Dolphins use similar echolocation with frequencies up to 150 kHz.

7. Echo & Reverberation

Echo

An echo is the repetition of sound caused by the reflection of sound waves from a hard surface (wall, mountain, building). You shout "Hello!" toward a hill → after a short delay, you hear "Hello!" again.

  • Our brain can distinguish two sounds if they reach the ear at least 0.1 seconds apart (persistence of hearing).
  • Therefore, for an echo to be heard distinctly, the reflecting surface must be at least 17 metres away (at room temperature).
  • Minimum distance formula: d = (v × t) / 2 = (343 × 0.1) / 2 = 17.15 m.
  • If the distance is less than 17 m, the reflected sound mixes with the original sound → reverberation.
Mnemonic for Echo: "17 m for 0.1 s" — The minimum distance for a clear echo is 17 metres because sound takes 0.1 seconds for the round trip. 17 metres → 1-7 → "Echo" starts with 'E' which is the 5th letter, but 17 is the age when you start shouting at walls! (Okay, just remember 17 m!)
Echo Calculation

The total distance travelled by sound = 2 × distance to obstacle.

Distance = (Speed × Time) / 2

Example: A man shouts toward a cliff and hears the echo after 2 seconds. Speed of sound = 343 m/s. Distance to cliff = (343 × 2) / 2 = 343 m.

Reverberation
  • When the reflecting surface is less than 17 m away, the reflected sound arrives within 0.1 s and mixes with the original sound, creating a prolonged or rolling effect called reverberation.
  • Example: In an empty room, your voice sounds "boomy" or echoing. The same room with carpets, curtains, and furniture sounds dead/dry — because soft materials absorb sound.
  • Reverberation time (RT60): The time taken for sound to decay by 60 dB after the source stops. Optimal RT for a lecture hall is ~0.8–1.2 seconds. For a concert hall, ~1.5–2.5 seconds.
Applications in Sound Halls & Auditoriums
  • Sound halls are designed with curved surfaces to distribute sound evenly.
  • Sound boards (like in a conch shell) are placed behind speakers to reflect sound forward without spreading.
  • Walls and ceilings may have sound-absorbing materials (acoustic foam, drapes, carpets) to prevent excessive reverberation.
  • Large auditoriums use multiple speakers placed at different locations to ensure everyone hears clearly.
  • Stethoscopes use the principle of multiple reflections of sound to amplify body sounds.
Did you know? Whales use echo to communicate over hundreds of kilometres underwater. Sound travels faster and farther in water than in air. The blue whale's call can be heard by another whale 800 km away!

8. SONAR

What is SONAR?

SONAR = Sound Navigation Ranging. It is a technique that uses ultrasound waves to detect objects underwater. Think of it as echolocation for ships.

Principle

SONAR works on the principle of echo (sound reflection). A device called a transducer (or transceiver) is mounted on the ship's hull. It performs two functions:

  • Transmitter: Sends a beam of ultrasound waves into the water.
  • Receiver: Detects the reflected echo from objects (seabed, submarine, fish school, etc.).
  • The time interval (t) between sending and receiving is measured.
  • Distance = (v × t) / 2, where v = speed of sound in water (~1531 m/s).
Applications of SONAR
  • Depth Measurement: Ships use SONAR to measure the depth of the ocean (echo sounding).
  • Detecting Submarines: Military uses SONAR for detecting enemy submarines and underwater mines.
  • Fishing: Fishermen use SONAR to locate schools of fish.
  • Seafloor Mapping: SONAR helps create detailed maps of the ocean floor (bathymetry).
  • Underwater Communication: SONAR is used for communication between submarines and ships.
  • Search & Rescue: Locating sunken ships, aircraft wreckage, and underwater debris.
Interesting: Dolphins are nature's SONAR experts. They can tell the difference between a metal coin and a plastic button from 15 metres away using underwater echolocation! The military studies dolphin sonar to improve their technology.
Mnemonic for SONAR formula: "S = UT / 2" — Sound distance = (Ultra Time) / 2. Or just remember: the sound goes down and back, so divide by 2.

9. Human Ear – Structure & Hearing

The human ear is a remarkable organ that converts sound vibrations into electrical signals that the brain can understand. It works in three stages: collection → amplification → transduction.

Structure of the Ear
PartDivisionComponentsFunction
Outer EarPinna (auricle)Fleshy outer partCollects sound waves and funnels them into ear canal
Ear canal~2.5 cm long tubeChannels sound to eardrum; has wax to trap dust
EardrumTympanic membraneThin membrane that vibrates when sound hits it
Middle EarHammer (malleus)First small boneTransmits vibrations from eardrum to anvil
Anvil (incus)Second small boneTransmits vibrations to stirrup
Stirrup (stapes)Third small bone (smallest bone in body)Transmits vibrations to oval window of inner ear
Inner EarCochleaSnail-shaped, fluid-filledConverts mechanical vibrations into electrical signals
Auditory nerveBundle of nervesCarries electrical signals to the brain
Semicircular canalsThree fluid-filled loopsHelp maintain balance (not hearing)
How We Hear — Step by Step
  • 1. Pinna collects sound waves from the environment.
  • 2. Sound travels through the ear canal and strikes the eardrum, making it vibrate.
  • 3. The three bones (hammer, anvil, stirrup) amplify the vibrations and pass them to the oval window of the cochlea.
  • 4. Fluid inside the cochlea ripples like a wave, bending tiny hair cells (cilia) inside.
  • 5. Hair cells convert mechanical motion into electrical signals (nerve impulses).
  • 6. The auditory nerve carries these signals to the brain, which interprets them as sound.
Analogy: The ear is like a reverse speaker. A speaker converts electrical signals into sound (vibrating cone). The ear does the opposite: it converts sound vibrations into electrical signals that the brain understands.
Range of Hearing
  • Humans: 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz (20 kHz)
  • Children can hear up to ~20 kHz; as we age, the upper limit decreases (presbycusis) — older adults may only hear up to 12–15 kHz.
  • Dogs: ~40 Hz – 60 kHz (can hear dog whistles that humans cannot)
  • Cats: ~45 Hz – 85 kHz
  • Bats: ~2 kHz – 200 kHz
  • Dolphins: ~20 Hz – 150 kHz
  • Elephants: ~5 Hz – 12 kHz (can hear infrasound)
Mnemonic for Ear Bones (order): "HAM" — Hammer → Anvil → M (Stirrup — think M for "Malleus, Incus, Stapes"). Or: "HAS" — H = Hammer, A = Anvil, S = Stirrup.

10. Noise Pollution

Noise pollution is unwanted or excessive sound that can harm human health and the environment. Any sound above 85 dB is considered harmful if exposed for prolonged periods.

Sources of Noise Pollution
  • Transportation: Vehicles (cars, trucks, buses, motorcycles), trains, aircraft, ships — especially near airports and highways.
  • Industrial: Factory machinery, generators, construction equipment (drills, jackhammers, bulldozers).
  • Social/Religious: Loudspeakers at weddings, temples, festivals, political rallies.
  • Domestic: Television, music systems, kitchen appliances (mixer, blender), vacuum cleaners, air conditioners.
  • Agricultural: Tractors, threshers, irrigation pumps.
  • Defence: Explosions, gunfire, military aircraft (sonic booms).
Health Effects of Noise Pollution
EffectDescription
Hearing LossProlonged exposure to >85 dB damages hair cells in the cochlea. This is irreversible — once hair cells die, they do not regenerate.
HypertensionConstant noise raises blood pressure and heart rate due to stress response.
Sleep DisturbanceNoise interferes with sleep quality, leading to fatigue and irritability.
TinnitusRinging/buzzing sound in the ears — a common result of noise-induced hearing damage.
Reduced ConcentrationNoise impairs cognitive performance, especially in children (affects learning).
Cardiovascular IssuesChronic noise exposure is linked to increased risk of heart attacks and strokes.
Mental HealthAnxiety, depression, and irritability are common in high-noise areas.
Measures to Control Noise Pollution
  • At Source: Silencers on vehicles and machinery, regular maintenance, use of electric engines instead of diesel, soundproof enclosures for factories.
  • Along the Path: Planting trees (green belts), sound barriers along highways (walls, berms), distance zoning (factories away from residential areas).
  • At the Receiver: Earplugs, earmuffs, noise-cancelling headphones. Workers in noisy industries must wear hearing protection.
  • Legal Measures: Noise Pollution (Regulation & Control) Rules, 2000 in India. Limits set for different zones (see table below).
  • Social Measures: Restricting use of loudspeakers (10 PM – 6 AM), firecracker regulations, public awareness campaigns.
  • Design Solutions: Sound-absorbing building materials (acoustic tiles, carpets, double-glazed windows).
Permissible Noise Limits in India (CPCB Standards)
ZoneDay (6 AM – 10 PM)Night (10 PM – 6 AM)
Silence Zone (near schools, hospitals, courts)50 dB40 dB

Exam Tip: In competitive exams, remember the four noise zones and their limits: Silence (50/40) → Residential (55/45) → Commercial (65/55) → Industrial (75/70). The night limit is always 10 dB less than the day limit.

12. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Ear Bones (Order)
Hammer → Anvil → Stirrup: "HAS" — H = Hammer, A = Anvil, S = Stirrup. Or think: "HAtS" — sounds like "hats". Or use the scientific names: MIS (Malleus, Incus, Stapes) = "MIS" leads to "miss".
Speed Order in Media
Solid > Liquid > Gas: "SLG" — Solid (fastest), Liquid (medium), Gas (slowest). Trick: "Saints Love God" — Saints (Solid) Love (Liquid) God (Gas) — Saints always reach first (fastest)!
Echo Minimum Distance
17 metres for a clear echo: "17 = 0.1 × 343 / 2" — Sound travels 34.3 m in 0.1 sec, half is 17.15 m. Trick: "Age 17 = Echo Age" — teenagers shout and hear echoes!
Human Hearing Range
20 Hz – 20,000 Hz (20 kHz): "20/20 vision for hearing" — just like 20/20 vision is perfect, 20 Hz to 20 kHz is the perfect hearing range. Easy to remember: two 20's — 20 and 20,000!
Wave Formula v = fλ
v = fλ (velocity = frequency × wavelength): "Very Fast Lambdas" — V = Velocity, F = Frequency, λ = Lambda (wavelength). Or: "Violet Flowers Look" — V for velocity, F for frequency, L for lambda.
