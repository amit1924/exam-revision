1. Work – The Scientific Definition

In everyday life, "work" means anything that requires effort. But in physics, work has a very specific meaning — and it's different from what you think!

Definition

Work is said to be done when a force applied on an object causes it to move/displace in the direction of the force.

W = F × s × cosθ

Where:
W = Work done (Joule)
F = Force applied (Newton)
s = Displacement (metre)
θ = Angle between force and displacement

When is Work Done?
  • Force + Displacement in same direction: Positive work. Example — pushing a box forward, pulling a drawer open. W = F × s (since cos 0° = 1)
  • Force opposite to displacement: Negative work. Example — brakes stopping a car (force backwards, car moves forward). W = −F × s (since cos 180° = −1)
  • Force perpendicular to displacement: Zero work. Example — carrying a bag while walking horizontally (force is upward, displacement is horizontal). cos 90° = 0 → W = 0
When is Work NOT Done? — Real Life vs Physics
  • Holding a heavy bag while standing still: Zero work in physics! You apply force upward, but there is zero displacement. No work done — even if you're sweating!
  • Walking with a suitcase: Force is upward (holding it), displacement is horizontal (walking). Since force ⟂ displacement, work done = 0.
  • Pushing a wall: The wall doesn't move (s = 0). Work = 0. You get tired because your muscles contract internally, but physically, no work is done on the wall.
  • A satellite orbiting Earth: The gravitational force is towards Earth's centre, but displacement is tangential. Work done by gravity = 0.

Units of Work
  • SI Unit: Joule (J) — 1 J = 1 N × 1 m. One Joule is work done when a force of 1 N displaces an object by 1 m in its direction.
  • CGS Unit: Erg — 1 erg = 1 dyne × 1 cm. 1 J = 10⁷ ergs.
Fun Analogy: Work in physics is like paying for a taxi ride — you only pay for the distance you actually travel (displacement), not for how hard you sit in the seat! Holding a bag is like sitting in a parked taxi — zero fare, zero work!

2. Energy – The Ability to Do Work

Definition

Energy is defined as the capacity to do work. The more energy you have, the more work you can do. Energy is a scalar quantity.

  • SI Unit: Joule (J) — same as work. Energy and work share the same unit because energy is the ability to do work.
  • Larger Units: Kilojoule (kJ) = 10³ J, Megajoule (MJ) = 10⁶ J

Remember: Energy can't be seen — but we know it exists by its effects! You can't see heat, but you feel it. You can't see wind energy, but you see leaves flying. Energy is like a ghost — invisible but you see its work!

3. Kinetic Energy – Energy of Motion

Definition

Kinetic Energy (KE) is the energy possessed by an object due to its motion. The word "kinetic" comes from Greek kinesis meaning motion.

Formula
KE = ½ × m × v²

Where:
m = mass of the object (kg)
v = velocity of the object (m/s)

Factors Affecting Kinetic Energy
  • Mass (m): KE ∝ m — double the mass, double the KE. A truck moving at 20 km/h has more KE than a bicycle at the same speed.
  • Velocity Squared (v²): KE ∝ v² — this is the most important factor! If you double the speed, KE becomes 4 times. Triple the speed → 9 times the KE!
Derivation of KE = ½mv²

Consider an object of mass m initially at rest. A force F acts on it, causing displacement s, and it reaches velocity v.

Work done = F × s
Using Newton's 2nd law: F = m × a
Using v² = u² + 2as → (u=0) → v² = 2as → s = v² / 2a
So, Work = (m × a) × (v² / 2a) = ½ × m × v²
∴ KE = ½mv²
Real-Life Examples
  • Cricket ball: A fast bowler's ball has high KE — that's why it hits the bat hard. A slower ball has less KE — easier to play.
  • Car crash at high speed: A car at 100 km/h has 4 times the KE of the same car at 50 km/h. This is why speeding is so dangerous — damage at high speed is not double, it's quadruple!
  • Bullet vs thrown stone: A tiny bullet has enormous KE because of its extremely high velocity (v² factor dominates).
  • Wind energy: Moving wind has KE — used by windmills to generate electricity.
Analogy: Kinetic energy is like a moving cricket ball — the faster it goes, the harder it hits. And if you double the speed, the energy QUADRUPLES — that's why cars at high speed cause so much damage. A ball at 40 km/h has 4× the KE of the same ball at 20 km/h!

4. Potential Energy – Stored Energy

Definition

Potential Energy (PE) is the energy stored in an object due to its position, shape, or configuration. It is the "waiting" energy that can be converted to kinetic energy.

Gravitational Potential Energy

The energy stored in an object due to its height above the ground.

PE = m × g × h

Where:
m = mass (kg)
g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
h = height above reference point (m)

  • Higher the height, more the PE: A book on a high shelf has more PE than the same book on a low shelf. If it falls, it will hit harder.
  • Heavier object, more PE: A 10 kg box at height 2 m has more PE than a 1 kg box at the same height.
  • Reference point matters: PE is always measured relative to something. A bird on a tree has PE relative to ground, but zero PE relative to its own branch.
Elastic Potential Energy

Energy stored in objects when they are stretched, compressed, or twisted.

  • Spring: When you compress a spring, you store elastic PE. Release it → PE converts to KE → the spring flies back.
  • Rubber band: Stretch it — you've stored elastic PE. Let go — it zooms across the room (PE → KE).
  • Bow and arrow: Pull the string back = store elastic PE in the bow. Release → PE → KE of the arrow.
  • Trampoline: When you jump on it, the stretched material stores elastic PE, then bounces you up.

Analogy: Potential energy is like a book on a shelf — it has stored energy that can fall. Higher shelf = more potential energy. It's like money in a bank — you're not spending it now, but you can use it later!

5. Law of Conservation of Energy

The Law
"Energy can neither be created nor destroyed — it can only be transformed from one form to another."

The total energy of an isolated system always remains constant. Energy just changes its form — like water changing from ice to liquid to steam, but the amount of water stays the same!

Example 1: Roller Coaster (Most Asked in Exams!)
  • At the top: Maximum PE (highest point), minimum KE (moving slowly).
  • Coming down: PE decreases → KE increases (speed increases). PE is being converted to KE!
  • At the bottom: Minimum PE, Maximum KE (fastest speed).
  • Going up again: KE decreases → PE increases again.
  • Key point: Total Energy (PE + KE) remains constant (ignoring friction).
Example 2: Simple Pendulum
  • At extreme positions (highest): PE = Maximum, KE = 0 (momentarily at rest).
  • At mean position (lowest): PE = Minimum, KE = Maximum (fastest).
  • In between: Some PE converts to KE and vice versa. Total energy = constant.

6. Power – Rate of Doing Work

Definition

Power is defined as the rate at which work is done or the rate of transfer of energy.

P = W / t

Where:
P = Power (Watt)
W = Work done (Joule)
t = Time taken (seconds)

Units of Power
UnitFull FormEquivalent
Watt (W)SI unit of power1 W = 1 J/s
Kilowatt (kW)1000 Watts1 kW = 10³ W
Megawatt (MW)10,00,000 Watts1 MW = 10⁶ W
Horsepower (hp)Old unit (James Watt)1 hp = 746 W

Numerical Examples
Example 1: A person does 600 J of work in 30 seconds. What is their power?
P = 600 / 30 = 20 W
Example 2: A 100 W bulb is used for 10 hours. How much energy does it consume?
Energy = Power × Time = 100 × (10 × 3600) = 3,600,000 J = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Example 3: A motor lifts a 200 kg load to a height of 10 m in 40 seconds. What is the power? (g = 10 m/s²)
Work = mgh = 200 × 10 × 10 = 20,000 J. P = 20,000 / 40 = 500 W
Analogy: Power is like the speed of a car — not the distance you travel, but how fast you do it. Two cars might both cover 100 km, but the one that does it in 1 hour has more power than the one that takes 4 hours. Same work, less time = more power!

7. Commercial Unit of Energy – The kilowatt-hour (kWh)

Why a New Unit?

The Joule is too small for practical electricity billing. Imagine telling someone "Your electricity bill is 360,000,000 J" — confusing! So we use the kilowatt-hour (kWh) — also called "Unit" on your electricity bill.

Definition

1 kilowatt-hour (kWh) is the energy consumed when a device of 1 kW power runs for 1 hour.

1 kWh = 1 kW × 1 h = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J

8. Simple Machines – Doing More with Less

What is a Simple Machine?

A simple machine is a device that helps us multiply force or change its direction. They make work easier — but remember, they don't reduce the total work!

Key Terms
  • Mechanical Advantage (MA): MA = Load / Effort. It tells us how many times the machine multiplies our force. MA > 1 means you need less effort than the load.
  • Efficiency (η): η = (Output Work / Input Work) × 100%. No machine is 100% efficient — some energy is always lost as heat due to friction.
  • Velocity Ratio (VR): VR = Distance moved by effort / Distance moved by load. For an ideal machine (no friction), MA = VR.
Types of Simple Machines

1. Lever — "Give me a lever and I can move the world" (Archimedes)

  • A rigid bar that rotates about a fixed point called fulcrum.
  • Principle: Load × Load arm = Effort × Effort arm
  • Classes: Class 1 (fulcrum in middle — seesaw, scissors), Class 2 (load in middle — wheelbarrow, nutcracker), Class 3 (effort in middle — tweezers, fishing rod)

2. Pulley — Lifting made easy

  • A wheel with a rope around it. Fixed pulley changes direction (pull down → lift up). Movable pulley multiplies force (MA = 2).
  • Block and tackle system: Multiple pulleys = high MA. Used in cranes, elevators.

3. Inclined Plane — The ramp that saves effort

  • A sloping surface. MA = Length of ramp / Height of ramp.
  • Longer the ramp, less effort needed — but you have to travel a longer distance!
  • Examples: Roads in hilly areas (zigzag roads reduce steepness), ramp for wheelchair, loading truck.

4. Wheel & Axle — Rolling is easier

  • A large wheel attached to a smaller axle. Both rotate together.
  • MA = Radius of wheel / Radius of axle.
  • Examples: Steering wheel, door knob, screwdriver, water tap, bicycle pedals.

5. Screw — The spiral inclined plane

  • An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder.
  • MA = 2πr / Pitch (smaller pitch = more MA).
  • Examples: Jar lid, bolt, drill, corkscrew, car jack (screw jack).

6. Wedge — Splitting force

  • Two inclined planes back-to-back. It converts a force applied at the blunt end into a splitting force at the sharp end.
  • Examples: Knife, axe, nail, needle, chisel, tooth.

10. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks

Kinetic Energy Formula
KE = ½mv²: Think of a "Half-MV" (Half times Mass times Velocity squared). Picture "½" as a half-sandwich, "m" as a man, "v²" as a very fast vehicle. The half is the most forgotten part — don't forget the ½!
Forms of Energy
8 Forms of Energy: "M. K. P. T. C. E. N. L." — My Kind Professor Teaches Chemistry Every Night Late.
M = Mechanical, K = Kinetic, P = Potential, T = Thermal, C = Chemical, E = Electrical, N = Nuclear, L = Light
Positive vs Negative Work
Work Direction Trick: "SAME = Positive" — when force and displacement are in the SAME direction, work is positive. "OPPO = Negative" — Opposite directions = negative work. "RIGHT ANGLE = ZERO" — perpendicular = zero work.
Simple Machines
6 Simple Machines: "L. P. I. W. S. W." = Lever, Pulley, Inclined Plane, Wheel & Axle, Screw, Wedge.
Or remember: "Lazy People Invented Wonderful Simple Widgets" — because simple machines make work easier!
Law of Conservation of Energy
"ECND" — Energy Can't be (C)reated Nor (D)estroyed, only "Transformed". Think of energy like LEGO blocks — you can rearrange them into different shapes (forms), but the total number of blocks stays the same!
