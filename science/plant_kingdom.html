1 Introduction — What Are Plants?
Basic Characteristics
  • Autotrophic — can synthesize their own food via photosynthesis
  • Photosynthetic — contain chlorophyll in chloroplasts
  • Cell wall — made of cellulose (rigid outer covering)
  • Eukaryotic — true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles
  • Immobile — fixed to substratum (except some algae)
  • Primary producers — base of most food chains
  • Store food as starch (main reserve carbohydrate)
  • Unlimited growth — thanks to meristematic tissues
Types of Plants (Based on Size & Stem)
  • Herbs — Small, soft stem, non-woody (e.g., Wheat, Grass, Mint)
  • Shrubs — Medium-sized, woody stem near base, branches near ground (e.g., Rose, Hibiscus, Lemon)
  • Trees — Tall, thick woody stem (trunk), branches above ground (e.g., Mango, Banyan, Pine)
  • Creepers — Weak stem, spread on ground (e.g., Watermelon, Pumpkin, Strawberry)
  • Climbers — Weak stem, use support to climb (e.g., Money plant, Grapevine, Bean)
Parts of a Flowering Plant
  • Root — Underground, anchors plant, absorbs water & minerals. Types: Tap root (dicots), Fibrous root (monocots)
  • Stem — Supports leaves, transports water & food, may store food (potato, ginger)
  • Leaf — Photosynthesis, transpiration. Parts: Lamina, Petiole, Midrib, Veins
  • Flower — Reproductive organ. Parts: Sepal, Petal, Stamen (male), Carpel/Pistil (female)
  • Fruit — Ripened ovary, protects seeds, aids in dispersal
  • Seed — Contains embryo, stored food, seed coat. Types: Monocot (1 cotyledon), Dicot (2 cotyledons)
Classification by Life Cycle
  • Annual — Complete life cycle in one season/year (e.g., Wheat, Rice, Mustard, Sunflower)
  • Biennial — Two-year cycle: vegetative growth in year 1, flowering & fruiting in year 2 (e.g., Carrot, Radish, Beetroot, Cabbage)
  • Perennial — Live for many years, may flower every year (e.g., Mango, Apple, Banyan, Rose, Grass)
Did You Know?
Some plants show ephemeral life cycles — completing their life cycle in just a few weeks (e.g., desert annuals).

Alternation of Generations — Overview

Alternation of generations refers to the alternation between two multicellular phases: haploid gametophyte (n) and diploid sporophyte (2n).

  • Haplontic — Gametophyte dominant, sporophyte is only the zygote (e.g., most algae like Spirogyra, Chlamydomonas)
  • Diplontic — Sporophyte dominant, gametophyte reduced to few cells (e.g., animals, some algae like Fucus, angiosperms)
  • Haplo-diplontic — Both phases multicellular and free-living, but one dominates (e.g., Bryophytes, Pteridophytes, Gymnosperms)
3 Algae (Thallophyta)
General Characteristics
  • Thalloid body — No differentiation into root, stem, or leaves
  • Autotrophic — Contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis
  • Aquatic — Mostly found in freshwater (ponds, lakes) and marine (oceans, seas)
  • Also found in moist terrestrial habitats (tree bark, damp soil, walls)
  • Cell wall made of cellulose (in most algae)
  • Reserve food — Starch (green algae), Floridean starch (red algae), Laminarin & Mannitol (brown algae)
  • Reproduction: Vegetative (fragmentation), Asexual (zoospores, aplanospores), Sexual (isogamy, anisogamy, oogamy)
  • Size range: Microscopic (Chlamydomonas — 10µm) to giant kelps (60-100m long — Macrocystis)
Detailed Classification & Reproduction Types

Algae show a remarkable diversity in form, from unicellular to multicellular, and in their reproductive strategies. The classification is primarily based on pigment composition, reserve food, and cell wall composition.

3.1 Green Algae (Chlorophyceae)
Pigments
  • Chlorophyll a
  • Chlorophyll b
  • Carotenoids (yellow-orange)
  • Xanthophylls
Reserve Food & Cell Wall
  • Reserve food: Starch
  • Cell wall: Cellulose
  • Pyrenoids store starch
  • Habitat: Freshwater (85%), Marine (15%)
Key Examples
  • Chlamydomonas — Unicellular, biflagellate, cup-shaped chloroplast
  • Spirogyra — Filamentous, spiral chloroplast, conjugation
  • Ulothrix — Filamentous, unbranched, band-shaped chloroplast
  • Chara — Stonewort, branched, calcified, oogamous
  • Volvox — Colonial, spherical, daughter colonies inside
  • Ulva — Sea lettuce, multicellular, sheet-like thallus

Chlorophyceae are called green algae because of the dominance of chlorophyll a and b. They are mostly freshwater forms. The chloroplasts can be cup-shaped (Chlamydomonas), spiral (Spirogyra), or band-shaped (Ulothrix). Sexual reproduction ranges from isogamy (similar gametes) to oogamy (egg and sperm).

3.2 Red Algae (Rhodophyceae)
Pigments
  • Chlorophyll a
  • Chlorophyll d
  • r-Phycoerythrin (red, dominant)
  • r-Phycocyanin (blue)
Reserve Food & Cell Wall
  • Reserve food: Floridean starch
  • Cell wall: Cellulose, Pectin, Agar
  • Habitat: Mostly marine
  • Found in deeper waters (absorb blue light)
Key Examples
  • Polysiphonia — Filamentous, branched, red
  • Porphyra — Used as Nori (sushi wrap)
  • Gracilaria — Agar source
  • Gelidium — Main agar source
  • Batrachospermum — Freshwater red alga

Rhodophyceae appear red due to the pigment r-phycoerythrin which masks chlorophyll. This pigment absorbs blue light that penetrates deeper water, allowing red algae to live at greater depths than other algae. They are the source of agar (used as a solidifying agent in microbiology) and nori (used in sushi).

3.3 Brown Algae (Phaeophyceae)
Pigments
  • Chlorophyll a
  • Chlorophyll c
  • Fucoxanthin (brown pigment, dominant)
  • Carotenoids
Reserve Food & Cell Wall
  • Reserve food: Laminarin, Mannitol
  • Cell wall: Cellulose + Algin
  • Habitat: Marine, cold waters
  • Mostly found in intertidal zones
Key Examples
  • Laminaria — Kelp, large brown algae
  • Sargassum — Gulfweed, free-floating
  • Fucus — Rockweed, intertidal
  • Dictyota — Fan-shaped thallus
  • Macrocystis — Giant kelp, largest algae (up to 100m)

Phaeophyceae are olive green to brown due to fucoxanthin pigment. They are almost exclusively marine and are the largest and most complex algae. Macrocystis (giant kelp) forms massive underwater forests off the coast of California, supporting rich marine ecosystems. Algin (alginic acid) extracted from brown algae is used as a thickener in ice creams, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals.

Algae Habitats & Ecological Roles

Habitat Diversity:

  • Freshwater — Ponds, lakes, rivers, streams (Spirogyra, Chlamydomonas, Ulothrix, Volvox, Chara)
  • Marine — Oceans, seas, intertidal zones (Ulva, Sargassum, Macrocystis, Polysiphonia, Porphyra)
  • Terrestrial — Damp soil, tree bark, walls, rocks (Pleurococcus, Trentepohlia)
  • Symbiotic — Lichens (algae + fungi), coral symbiosis (zooxanthellae)
  • Extreme habitats — Hot springs (Cyanobacteria), snow (Chlamydomonas nivalis — red snow), high salinity (Dunaliella)
  • Epiphytic — Growing on other plants (especially in tropical rainforests)

Ecological Significance:

  • Oxygen production — Algae (especially phytoplankton) produce ~50% of Earth's oxygen via photosynthesis
  • Carbon fixation — Major role in global carbon cycle, sequestering CO₂ from atmosphere
  • Food chain base — Phytoplankton forms the base of most aquatic food webs
  • Nitrogen fixation — Cyanobacteria fix atmospheric nitrogen (Anabaena, Nostoc)
  • Primary succession — Algae are pioneer species on bare rocks, start soil formation
  • Bioindicators — Algal diversity indicates water quality; some are pollution indicators
  • Algal blooms — Rapid growth due to eutrophication can be harmful (red tides, dead zones)
  • Coral reef formation — Symbiotic zooxanthellae (dinoflagellate algae) provide energy to coral polyps
Economic Importance of Algae
Agar

From Gelidium & Gracilaria; used in microbiology as solid culture medium, in jellies, laxatives, cosmetics

Carrageenan

From red algae (Chondrus crispus, Gigartina); stabilizer in dairy, toothpaste, pet food, cosmetics

Algin/Alginic Acid

From brown algae (Laminaria, Macrocystis); thickener in ice creams, jellies, dental impressions, textiles

Biofuel

Algae are a promising source of biodiesel (high lipid content up to 60% dry weight) and bioethanol

Fertilizer & Soil Conditioner

Seaweed (Sargassum, Laminaria, Ascophyllum) used as organic fertilizer rich in K, trace minerals, growth hormones

Food & Supplements

Nori (Porphyra) for sushi, Chlorella & Spirulina as dietary supplements, DHA (omega-3) from algae, kelp as food

Pharmaceuticals

Antiviral (sulfated polysaccharides), antitumor (carrageenan), anticoagulant, anti-inflammatory compounds

Wastewater Treatment

Algae remove excess nutrients (N, P) from wastewater, reduce eutrophication, produce biomass for biofuel

Bioremediation

Algae absorb heavy metals (Pb, Cd, Hg), radioactive isotopes, and organic pollutants from water

4 Bryophytes — Amphibians of Plant Kingdom
Why "Amphibians of Plant Kingdom"?
  • They live in moist, shady habitats (damp soil, tree bark, walls, rock crevices)
  • Require water for fertilization — biflagellate sperm must swim through water to reach egg in archegonium
  • Can survive on land but are dependent on water for sexual reproduction
  • Like amphibians (frogs), they are tied to water for completion of their life cycle
  • They are the first land plants but not fully adapted to dry terrestrial conditions
General Characteristics
  • Body is thalloid (flat, ribbon-like, no differentiation) or leafy (stem-like axis + leaf-like appendages)
  • No true roots — have rhizoids for anchorage (not for water/mineral absorption)
  • No vascular tissue (xylem & phloem absent) — water transport by diffusion and capillary action
  • Gametophyte (haploid, n) is the dominant, independent, photosynthetic generation
  • Sporophyte (diploid, 2n) is dependent on gametophyte for water and nutrition
  • Sex organs: Antheridium (male — produces biflagellate sperm) and Archegonium (female — flask-shaped, contains egg)
  • Fertilization produces a zygote → embryo → sporophyte (foot + seta + capsule) → spores (via meiosis) → new gametophyte
  • Water is essential for sperm motility — without water, fertilization cannot occur

The zygote in bryophytes is the first cell of the sporophyte generation. Unlike algae, the sporophyte does not become free-living. Instead, it remains attached to the gametophyte and derives nutrition from it throughout its life. The sporophyte consists of three parts: foot (anchored in gametophyte tissue), seta (stalk), and capsule (sporangium). Meiosis occurs inside the capsule to produce haploid spores.

4.1 Liverworts (Hepaticopsida)
Characteristics
  • Thalloid or leafy body, dorsiventral (distinct upper and lower surface)
  • Unicellular rhizoids
  • Asexual reproduction by gemmae (small multicellular bodies in gemma cups)
  • Sporophyte has foot, seta, and capsule
  • Spores germinate to form new thallus
Examples
  • Marchantia — Thalloid with gemma cups, rosette-shaped, dioecious
  • Riccia — Simple thalloid, rosette-like, dichotomously branched
  • Pellia — Thalloid, irregularly branched
Key Point
Gemmae are green, multicellular structures in gemma cups on Marchantia thallus; they get dispersed by rain splash and grow into new thallus.
4.2 Mosses (Bryopsida)
Characteristics
  • Leafy with stem-like and leaf-like structures
  • Multicellular rhizoids for anchorage
  • Life cycle: Spore → Protonema (thread-like, filamentous stage) → Leafy gametophore
  • Protonema is the juvenile stage that develops from spores
  • Sporophyte has foot, seta (stalk), and capsule (with operculum & peristome)
Examples & Economic Importance
  • Funaria — Carpet moss, common, found on damp soil & walls
  • Sphagnum — Peat moss, high ecological importance
  • Polytrichum — Hair cap moss, tall, found in forests
Sphagnum — Peat Moss
Sphagnum forms peat bogs in wetlands. Peat is used as fuel, soil conditioner (highest water holding capacity), and for packing plants. Also used in whisky production (peat-smoked malt).
4.3 Hornworts (Anthocerotopsida)
Characteristics
  • Thalloid body, horn-like sporophyte (elongated, finger-like capsule)
  • Sporophyte has meristematic tissue at base (continuous growth)
  • Cells contain single large chloroplast (unique)
  • Often have symbiotic cyanobacteria (Nostoc) in thallus cavities
Examples
  • Anthoceros — Most common genus; dark green, rosette-forming thallus
  • Named for its horn-shaped sporophyte (1-5 cm tall)
  • Sporophyte has stomata (unlike other bryophytes)
5 Pteridophytes — First Vascular Land Plants
Key Characteristics
  • True roots, stem, and leaves — first plants with organ differentiation
  • Vascular tissue present — Xylem (water transport) and Phloem (food transport)
  • Xylem lacks vessels (tracheids only) — except in some ferns
  • Spore-producing — No seeds or flowers
  • Sporophyte (2n) dominant — independent, large generation
  • Gametophyte (n) — called prothallus, small, independent, heart-shaped
  • Heterosporous in some — produce microspores (male) and megaspores (female)
  • Water required for fertilization (flagellated sperm swim to egg)
Evolutionary Significance

Pteridophytes are the first true land plants with a vascular system. They were the dominant vegetation during the Carboniferous period (~360-300 million years ago) — known as the "Age of Ferns."

Coal Formation
The massive forests of pteridophytes during the Carboniferous period were buried and compressed over millions of years, forming coal deposits that we use today as fossil fuel.
5.1 Four Classes of Pteridophytes
1. Psilopsida (Whisk Ferns)
  • Most primitive pteridophytes
  • No true roots — have rhizomes with rhizoids
  • Example: Psilotum — whisk fern, found in tropics
  • Dichotomously branched stems
2. Lycopsida (Club Mosses)
  • True roots and stems present
  • Leaves small (microphylls)
  • Examples: Lycopodium (club moss), Selaginella (spike moss)
  • Selaginella is heterosporous
  • Lycopodium spores used as flash powder (earlier photography)
3. Sphenopsida (Horsetails)
  • Jointed stems with nodes and internodes
  • Whorls of small leaves at nodes
  • Example: Equisetum — horsetail, "living fossil"
  • Silica deposits in stem → used as scouring rush
4. Pteropsida (Ferns)
  • Most advanced pteridophytes
  • Large leaves called fronds (megaphylls)
  • Examples: Dryopteris (wood fern), Pteris, Adiantum (maidenhair), Marsilea (water fern), Azolla (mosquito fern), Pteridium (bracken)
  • Azolla has Anabaena symbiosis (N-fixing cyanobacterium) → used as biofertilizer
Economic Importance of Pteridophytes
  • Ornamental — Ferns are popular indoor and garden plants (Adiantum (maidenhair), Dryopteris (wood fern), Nephrolepis (Boston fern), Pteris)
  • Medicinal — Equisetum used in diuretic preparations (rich in silica); Lycopodium spores used in homoeopathy (Lycopodium clavatum) and as dusting powder
  • Biofertilizer — Azolla-Anabaena symbiosis fixes atmospheric nitrogen at an impressive rate; used as green manure in rice paddies of S.E. Asia, China, India
  • Soil binding & Erosion control — Ferns and club mosses prevent soil erosion on slopes and in forests
  • Coal formation — Fossil pteridophytes from Carboniferous period formed major coal deposits used as fossil fuel today
  • Food — Young fiddleheads of some ferns are eaten as vegetables (Matteuccia, Pteridium); Marsilea tubers edible
  • Indicator plants — Some pteridophytes indicate soil type, mineral content, and moisture levels
  • Floristry — Fern fronds widely used in flower arrangements and bouquets
Sporophyte vs Gametophyte in Pteridophytes
Sporophyte (2n)
  • Dominant, large, long-lived generation
  • Well-differentiated: roots, stem, leaves (fronds)
  • Has vascular tissue (xylem + phloem)
  • Produces sporangia on leaves (sporophylls)
  • Sori = clusters of sporangia (in ferns)
  • Indusium = protective cover over sorus
  • Independent, free-living, photosynthetic
Gametophyte — Prothallus (n)
  • Small, inconspicuous, short-lived
  • Heart-shaped (in ferns), green, photosynthetic
  • No vascular tissue
  • Thalloid body with rhizoids (not roots)
  • Bears antheridia (on lower surface, near apex)
  • Bears archegonia (near notch of heart)
  • Free-living but requires moist conditions
  • Water needed for sperm to swim to archegonia
6 Gymnosperms — Naked Seed Plants
General Characteristics
  • "Gymnos" = naked, "sperma" = seed → seeds are not enclosed in fruits
  • Seeds are borne on cones (strobili) — male and female cones
  • Sporophyte (2n) dominant — tall trees, well-differentiated body
  • Heterosporous — produce microspores (male) and megaspores (female)
  • No flowers — reproductive structures are cones (strobili)
  • Xylem has tracheids only (no vessels) — except Gnetopsida
  • Phloem has sieve cells (no companion cells)
  • Leaves may be needle-like (Pinus) or scale-like to reduce water loss
  • Pollination is by wind (anemophilous)
  • Water not required for fertilization — pollen tube carries sperm to egg
Evolutionary Position

Gymnosperms are transitional between pteridophytes and angiosperms. They evolved seeds (like angiosperms) but lack flowers and fruits (like pteridophytes). They were dominant in the Mesozoic Era ("Age of Gymnosperms" ~250-65 mya).

Key Point
Gymnosperms are called "naked seed" plants because the seeds are not enclosed in an ovary wall. The seeds lie exposed on the surface of cone scales.
6.1 Cycadopsida (Cycads)
Characteristics
  • Palm-like, unbranched stem with crown of leaves
  • Large, pinnate leaves (like palms)
  • Dioecious — male and female cones on separate plants
  • Flagellated sperm (primitive, like pteridophytes)
  • Slow-growing, long-lived
Examples
  • Cycas — Sago palm, most common cycad, used for sago starch
  • Zamia — Coontie, produces edible starch (but toxic if raw)
  • Cycas is the only gymnosperm with circinate vernation (like ferns)
6.2 Coniferopsida (Conifers)
Characteristics
  • Cone-bearing (conifers), branched trees
  • Needle-like or scale-like leaves (reduce water loss)
  • Monoecious or dioecious
  • Woody cones, resin canals present
  • Evergreen (retain leaves year-round)
  • Form large forests in cold/temperate regions (Taiga)
Examples
  • Pinus — Pine, most widespread, source of timber & turpentine
  • Cedrus — Cedar/Deodar, aromatic wood, resistant to decay
  • Abies — Fir, Christmas tree, softwood timber
  • Picea — Spruce, used for paper and musical instruments
  • Araucaria — Monkey puzzle tree, ornamental
6.3 Gnetopsida
Advanced Features
  • Most advanced gymnosperms
  • Vessels in xylem (like angiosperms) — unique among gymnosperms
  • Flower-like cones in some (Gnetum)
  • Some have insect pollination (advanced feature)
Examples
  • Ephedra — Joint fir, desert shrub, contains ephedrine (medicinal — decongestant)
  • Gnetum — Vine or tree, broad leaves like angiosperms, found in tropics
  • Welwitschia — Only 2 leaves that grow continuously, found in Namib desert; can live 1000+ years
Economic Importance of Gymnosperms
Timber

Pine, Cedar (Deodar), Spruce, Fir — used in construction, furniture, railway sleepers

Resin & Turpentine

From Pinus; resin used in varnishes, paints; turpentine as solvent & medicine

Paper

Softwood of conifers (Picea, Pinus) major source of wood pulp for paper industry

Medicinal

Ephedra → ephedrine (bronchodilator); Taxus → taxol (anti-cancer drug)

Ornamental

Araucaria, Thuja, Cupressus — widely planted as ornamental trees in gardens

Food

Sago starch from Cycas; pine nuts (seeds) from Pinus species — edible

7 Angiosperms — Flowering Plants
General Characteristics
  • "Angios" = covered/vessel, "sperma" = seed → seeds enclosed within fruits
  • Flowers present — reproductive organs
  • Double fertilization — unique to angiosperms
  • Endosperm formation — triploid (3n) nutritive tissue for embryo
  • Sporophyte (2n) dominant — large, complex, well-differentiated
  • Gametophyte highly reduced — just a few cells (embryo sac, pollen grain)
  • Xylem has vessels (tracheids + vessels) — efficient water transport
  • Phloem has sieve tubes + companion cells
  • Pollination by wind, water, insects, birds, bats
Unique Features of Angiosperms
  • Double Fertilization — One sperm (n) fuses with egg (n) → zygote (2n); second sperm (n) fuses with two polar nuclei (2n) → triploid endosperm (3n)
  • Triploid Endosperm — Provides nutrition to the developing embryo; unique to angiosperms
  • Fruit Formation — Ovary ripens into fruit; ovules become seeds; fruit protects & aids seed dispersal
  • Highly efficient vascular system — vessels in xylem, companion cells in phloem
  • Diverse pollination strategies — co-evolution with pollinators
7.1 Monocotyledons (Monocots)
Characteristics
  • 1 cotyledon in seed
  • Parallel venation in leaves
  • Fibrous root system
  • Floral parts in multiples of 3
  • Vascular bundles scattered in stem
  • No secondary growth (no cambium)
  • Pollen grain with single pore (monocolpate)
Examples
  • Cereals: Wheat (Triticum), Rice (Oryza), Maize (Zea mays), Barley, Oats
  • Sugarcane (Saccharum officinarum) — major sugar source
  • Bamboo — fastest growing plant, used in construction
  • Coconut (Cocos nucifera) — versatile palm
  • Grasses — Poaceae family (largest plant family)
  • Lily (Lilium), Orchid (Orchidaceae), Banana (Musa)
  • Onion (Allium cepa), Garlic (Allium sativum)
7.2 Dicotyledons (Dicots)
Characteristics
  • 2 cotyledons in seed
  • Reticulate venation in leaves
  • Tap root system
  • Floral parts in multiples of 4 or 5
  • Vascular bundles in ring (arranged in a circle)
  • Secondary growth present (cambium active)
  • Pollen grain with 3 pores (tricolpate)
Examples
  • Mango (Mangifera indica) — National fruit of India
  • Pea (Pisum sativum) — Mendel's experimental plant
  • Rose (Rosa), Sunflower (Helianthus)
  • Mustard (Brassica) — oilseed
  • Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum), Potato (Solanum tuberosum)
  • Peepal (Ficus religiosa), Banyan (Ficus benghalensis)
  • Apple (Malus domestica), Groundnut (Arachis hypogaea)
Angiosperm Life Cycle — Detailed Steps
1. Sporogenesis

Microsporogenesis (in anther): microspore mother cell (2n) → meiosis → 4 microspores (n) → pollen grains. Megasporogenesis (in ovule): megaspore mother cell (2n) → meiosis → 4 megaspores (n) — only 1 functional.

2. Gametogenesis

Pollen grain (male gametophyte): generative cell → 2 sperm cells + tube cell. Embryo sac (female gametophyte): 7 cells, 8 nuclei — egg cell, 2 synergids, 3 antipodals, 2 polar nuclei (central cell).

3. Pollination

Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma. Self-pollination (autogamy — same flower; geitonogamy — same plant). Cross-pollination (xenogamy — different plant). Agents: wind (anemophily), insects (entomophily), birds (ornithophily), bats (chiropterophily), water (hydrophily).

4. Fertilization

Pollen tube enters ovule via micropyle. Double fertilization: Sperm 1 + egg → diploid zygote (2n). Sperm 2 + 2 polar nuclei → triploid primary endosperm nucleus (3n). This is the defining feature of angiosperms!

5. Seed Development

Zygote → embryo (radicle, plumule, cotyledons). Endosperm (3n) → nutritive tissue. Integuments → seed coat. Ovule → seed. Monocot: 1 cotyledon (scutellum). Dicot: 2 cotyledons.

6. Fruit Formation

Ovary → fruit (pericarp: epicarp, mesocarp, endocarp). Types: fleshy (mango, apple, tomato), dry (pea pod, mustard), aggregate (strawberry), multiple (pineapple). Parthenocarpy: fruit without fertilization (banana, seedless grapes).

7. Seed Dispersal

Wind (cotton, dandelion — pappus), water (coconut — fibrous husk), animals (Xanthium — hooks; berries — eaten & excreted), explosive (castor, balsam — dehiscence). Ensures colonization of new areas.

8. Germination

Seed absorbs water → radicle emerges → plumule emerges → seedling → mature sporophyte. Epigeal (cotyledons above ground — bean) vs Hypogeal (cotyledons underground — pea, maize).

Flower Structure & Types

Parts of a Typical Flower:

  • Sepals (Calyx) — Outermost whorl, green, protect flower bud
  • Petals (Corolla) — 2nd whorl, colorful, attract pollinators
  • Stamens (Androecium) — Male reproductive whorl: anther (pollen production) + filament (stalk)
  • Carpels/Pistil (Gynoecium) — Female reproductive whorl: stigma (pollen reception) + style (tube passage) + ovary (contains ovules)
  • Receptacle — Thickened part of flower stalk that holds floral organs
  • Nectaries — Glands that secrete nectar to attract pollinators

Floral Classification:

  • Complete vs Incomplete — Has all 4 whorls vs missing one or more
  • Perfect vs Imperfect — Bisexual (both stamens & carpels) vs Unisexual (male/female flowers separate)
  • Monoecious vs Dioecious — Male & female flowers on same plant (maize, cucumber) vs on separate plants (papaya, date palm)
  • Actinomorphic vs Zygomorphic — Radial symmetry (rose) vs Bilateral symmetry (pea, orchid)
  • Hypogynous vs Perigynous vs Epigynous — Based on ovary position relative to other floral parts
  • Inflorescence types — Racemose (indeterminate, e.g., radish) vs Cymose (determinate, e.g., jasmine)
8 Plant Life Cycles & Alternation of Generations
Understanding Alternation of Generations

Alternation of generations is the phenomenon where the life cycle of a plant alternates between two distinct multicellular phases: the haploid (n) gametophyte which produces gametes by mitosis, and the diploid (2n) sporophyte which produces spores by meiosis. This is a fundamental characteristic of all land plants and many algae.

The key event that links these two generations is fertilization (fusion of gametes → diploid zygote → sporophyte) and meiosis (in sporangia → haploid spores → gametophyte). The relative prominence of these two phases varies greatly among different plant groups.

Haplontic Life Cycle
  • Gametophyte (n) is dominant and free-living — photosynthetic, independent
  • Sporophyte stage is only the zygote (2n) — single-celled, not free-living
  • Zygote undergoes zygotic meiosis (immediate meiosis) to produce haploid spores
  • No multicellular sporophyte stage ever develops
  • Found in: Most algae (Chlamydomonas, Spirogyra, Ulothrix, Volvox, Chara)
  • Considered the primitive ancestral condition from which other life cycles evolved
Example: Chlamydomonas cycle
Haploid cell → produces gametes by mitosis → fusion → diploid zygote → zygotic meiosis → 4 haploid spores → new haploid cells
Diplontic Life Cycle
  • Sporophyte (2n) is dominant and free-living — large, complex, independent
  • Gametophyte stage is highly reduced to just a few cells (pollen grain, embryo sac)
  • Gametophyte produces gametes by mitosis (no meiosis needed in gametophyte)
  • Zygote grows directly into multicellular sporophyte
  • Meiosis occurs in sporogenous tissue to produce spores
  • Found in: Animals, some brown algae (Fucus, Sargassum), Gymnosperms, Angiosperms
  • Considered the most evolutionarily advanced life cycle type
Example: Angiosperm cycle
Sporophyte (tree) → meiosis in anthers & ovules → microspores & megaspores → pollen grain & embryo sac (reduced gametophytes) → fertilization → seed → new sporophyte
Haplo-diplontic Life Cycle
  • Both sporophyte (2n) and gametophyte (n) are multicellular and free-living
  • Both stages are photosynthetic and independent (in pteridophytes)
  • In bryophytes: sporophyte is dependent on gametophyte for nutrition
  • In pteridophytes: both are independent; gametophyte (prothallus) is small but free-living
  • Found in: Bryophytes, Pteridophytes
  • Intermediate between haplontic and diplontic — a transitional evolutionary stage
Example: Fern life cycle
Sporophyte (fern frond) → sporangia → meiosis → spores → prothallus (heart-shaped gametophyte) → antheridia & archegonia → fertilization → new sporophyte

11 Economic Importance of Plants
Food Plants
  • Cereals — Wheat (Triticum), Rice (Oryza), Maize (Zea), Barley, Millet — staple food for humanity
  • Pulses — Gram (Cicer), Pea (Pisum), Lentil (Lens), Bean (Phaseolus) — protein source
  • Vegetables — Potato (Solanum), Tomato, Onion, Carrot, Spinach, Cabbage, Cauliflower
  • Fruits — Mango, Apple, Banana, Orange, Grape, Papaya, Guava — vitamins & minerals
  • Oilseeds — Mustard, Groundnut, Soybean, Sunflower, Coconut — edible oils
  • Spices — Black pepper, Turmeric, Chilli, Cardamom, Cinnamon, Clove — flavoring & medicinal
Medicinal Plants
  • Cinchona — Bark yields quinine (anti-malarial drug)
  • Rauwolfia serpentina — Sarpagandha; contains reserpine (anti-hypertensive)
  • Digitalis purpurea — Foxglove; contains digitalin (cardiac stimulant)
  • Papaver somniferum — Opium poppy; source of morphine (painkiller), codeine
  • Taxus brevifolia — Pacific yew; contains taxol (anti-cancer drug)
  • Ephedra — Contains ephedrine (bronchodilator for asthma)
  • Azadirachta indica — Neem; antibacterial, antifungal, insecticidal
  • Ocimum sanctum — Tulsi; adaptogen, anti-inflammatory
Timber Plants
  • Teak (Tectona grandis) — most valuable timber, furniture, shipbuilding
  • Sal (Shorea robusta) — Indian timber, railway sleepers, construction
  • Pine (Pinus) — softwood, construction, paper pulp, resin
  • Deodar (Cedrus deodara) — Himalayan cedar, durable, aromatic wood
  • Rosewood (Dalbergia sissoo) — Shisham, furniture, plywood
  • Sandalwood (Santalum album) — fragrant wood, carving, essential oil
  • Mahogany (Swietenia) — cabinet making, furniture
Industrial Plants
  • Rubber (Hevea brasiliensis) — natural rubber from latex; used in tires, gloves
  • Jute (Corchorus) — golden fiber; used in sacks, ropes, carpets
  • Cotton (Gossypium) — natural fiber for textiles; seed oil
  • Sugarcane (Saccharum) — sugar, ethanol, molasses
  • Tea (Camellia sinensis) — beverage industry
  • Coffee (Coffea arabica) — global stimulant beverage
  • Tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) — cigarettes, contains nicotine
  • Bamboo — construction, paper, textiles, scaffolding
